# Scientific Style

Use this reference after higher-order concerns are clear, or when the user asks for sentence-level scientific clarity.

## Edit Order

Fix higher-order concerns before polishing lower-order concerns:

1. Reader, purpose, and claim.
2. Section and paragraph organization.
3. Cohesion and coherence.
4. Sentence clarity, grammar, punctuation, and style.

Mechanical edits matter when they block trust or comprehension, especially in titles, abstracts, openings, headings, and topic sentences.

## Concrete Subjects and Verbs

Scientific prose becomes easier to follow when the grammatical subject matches the conceptual focus.

- Prefer concrete subjects that can perform the action: "cells migrated", "the model predicted", "we measured".
- Keep subjects short enough that the reader reaches the verb quickly.
- Put subject and verb close together.
- Avoid empty openings such as "there is", "there are", "it is important that", and "the fact that" when a concrete subject can do the work.
- Replace unnecessary nominalizations with verbs: "analyze" instead of "conduct an analysis", "increase" instead of "experience an increase".

Keep a nominalization when it improves cohesion by naming a prior action as the next sentence's familiar topic.

## Active and Passive Voice

Do not treat active voice as automatically better.

- Use active voice when the actor, responsibility, decision, or interpretation matters.
- Use passive voice when the procedure, object, result, or previous sentence topic matters more than the actor.
- In Methods, passive voice is often acceptable because procedures may matter more than researchers.
- For cohesion, choose the voice that lets the sentence begin with familiar information.
- Avoid passive constructions that create dangling modifiers or hide necessary responsibility.

## Information Weight

Readers expect sentences to move from easier context toward harder information.

- Avoid long front-loaded introductory clauses when the main clause is short.
- Move heavy conditions, exceptions, and methodological details later when possible.
- Put new, technical, or emphasized information near the end of the sentence.
- Avoid noun strings that force the reader to work backward; unpack them with prepositions or verbs.

## Modifiers

Related information should stay close together.

- Place limiting words such as "only", "nearly", "almost", "merely", and "simply" next to what they limit.
- Place "that" and "which" clauses immediately after the noun they modify.
- If a "which" clause could refer to more than one idea, split the sentence or repeat the noun.
- Check opening participial or infinitive phrases: the subject of the main clause must be able to perform the action.
- In scientific prose, watch for subjects such as "samples", "experiments", "focus", or "series" doing actions only researchers can do.

## Parallelism

Parallel structure helps readers compare equivalent ideas.

- Make list items grammatically alike: nouns with nouns, verbs with verbs, clauses with clauses.
- In vertical lists, use the first item as the pattern for all others.
- In comparisons with "while", "whereas", "as", or "than", keep both sides structurally comparable.
- Use parallelism in aims, hypotheses, contribution statements, figure callouts, and limitation lists.

## Punctuation for Clarity

Use punctuation to remove ambiguity, not to make sentences look formal.

- Join two independent clauses with a comma plus a coordinating conjunction, or separate them with a period.
- Use a semicolon only when two independent clauses are tightly related.
- Put a semicolon before connectors such as "however", "therefore", "thus", and "for example" if they join two independent clauses.
- Add commas after introductory infinitive phrases, participial phrases, and long prepositional phrases.
- Prefer shorter sentences when punctuation is doing too much work.
