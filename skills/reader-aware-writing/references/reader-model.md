# Reader Model

Use this reference when the audience is ambiguous, the stakes are high, or the same content must work for multiple reader groups.

## Minimum Reader Model

Capture these fields before drafting or revising:

- Reader: role, group, or named stakeholder.
- Current knowledge: what they already know, misunderstand, or do not care about.
- Reading job: the decision, action, approval, learning, or trust-building task the text must support.
- Stakes: what happens if they misunderstand, disagree, ignore, or act on the text.
- Constraints: time, attention, politics, legal/compliance concerns, technical depth, emotional state, channel, and format.
- Objections: likely skepticism, confusion, fear, or hidden criteria.
- Tone target: direct, warm, rigorous, executive, instructional, neutral, reassuring, urgent, exploratory, or other.

## Reader Types

Use these defaults only as starting points:

- Executive reader: needs the decision, tradeoffs, risk, cost, timing, and clear ask. Put proof after the point.
- Technical peer: needs precise terms, assumptions, constraints, edge cases, and reproducible details. Avoid oversimplifying.
- Newcomer: needs orientation, definitions, examples, and a low-friction path through unfamiliar concepts.
- Operator or maintainer: needs steps, failure modes, ownership, escalation paths, and what changes in practice.
- Skeptical reviewer: needs evidence, limits, alternatives considered, and acknowledgement of reasonable concerns.
- Busy collaborator: needs context, exact request, deadline, and next action with minimal ceremony.
- Public reader: needs plain language, context, credibility signals, and careful handling of claims that could be misread.

## Scientific Reader Types

Use these defaults for scientific articles and research communication:

- Journal reviewer: needs the research question, novelty, methodological adequacy, evidence for claims, limitations, and fit with the journal.
- Specialist reader: needs precise concepts, field-specific assumptions, technical detail, and careful positioning against prior work.
- Interdisciplinary researcher: needs more orientation, definitions, and explicit links between field-specific terms and broader concepts.
- Editor: needs scope fit, contribution, clarity of claims, ethical or reporting concerns, and likely reviewer objections.
- Student reader: needs the argument path, definitions, examples, and explicit explanations of why each section or result matters.
- Clinical, policy, or practice reader: needs the actionable implication, population or context limits, risk, uncertainty, and strength of evidence.
- Statistical or methods reader: needs design logic, variables, assumptions, model choices, uncertainty, robustness, and reproducibility details.

## Reader Fit Questions

Ask these internally while revising:

1. What is the reader trying to do after reading this?
2. What would make them stop reading?
3. What term, claim, or leap might they not understand?
4. What would they challenge, and does the text answer that challenge fairly?
5. What can move out of the way so the main point is easier to see?
6. What context is missing before the details make sense?
7. What detail is necessary for trust, even if it adds length?

For scientific writing, also ask:

1. Which claim is the reader being asked to believe?
2. What evidence supports that claim, and where is the evidence located?
3. What would a skeptical reviewer challenge first?
4. Does the text distinguish result, interpretation, implication, and speculation?
5. Does the section answer the reader question expected for that section?

## Multi-Reader Strategy

When one artifact serves multiple readers:

- Lead with the shared purpose or decision.
- Put audience-specific depth in clearly labeled sections.
- Use summaries for executives and appendices or implementation sections for specialists.
- Avoid a compromise voice that serves nobody. Prefer layered structure over averaging the audience.
- If reader needs conflict, call out the tradeoff and propose separate artifacts.
