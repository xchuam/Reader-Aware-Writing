# Academic Writing Patterns

Use this reference for academic drafts, research introductions, paragraph feedback, and cohesion/coherence work. For full scientific article sections, read [scientific-article.md](scientific-article.md). For sentence-by-sentence flow diagnostics, read [cohesion-coherence.md](cohesion-coherence.md).

## Research Introductions

Use a three-move introduction when adapting research writing:

1. Establish the territory.
   - Orient the reader to the research area.
   - Show why the area is important, interesting, problematic, or active.
   - Review the relevant literature enough to give background, show familiarity with the field, and position the new work in an existing research chain.

2. Establish the niche.
   - Identify the gap, unresolved conflict, extension, or new question.
   - Make the missing link explicit because it supports the research question and the contribution.
   - Use contrast language carefully: "however", "although", "despite", "few studies", "little information", or field-appropriate equivalents.

3. Occupy the niche.
   - State the aim, objective, purpose, or research question.
   - If writing from the paper's perspective, present tense usually fits: "This paper examines..."
   - If writing from the completed study's perspective, past tense may fit: "This study examined..."
   - Add findings, value, or paper structure only when the field or venue expects it.

## Paragraph Development

Treat a paragraph as a reader path through one governing idea:

- Use a topic sentence to state the main point, give the reader direction, and keep irrelevant material out.
- Put the topic sentence at or near the beginning unless there is a deliberate reason not to.
- Develop the topic sentence with examples, evidence, definitions, comparison, causes, effects, chronology, or consequences.
- Start a new paragraph for a new idea, contrast, complex pause, or new step in an extended argument.
- Arrange information by time, space, climax, importance, or another order the reader can predict.

## Cohesion and Coherence

Distinguish sentence flow from paragraph unity:

- Cohesion is sentence-by-sentence flow. Each sentence should connect to what came before and prepare what comes next.
- Coherence is unity. The paragraph or passage should have a clear governing point.
- For sentence beginnings, put familiar or old information first when possible.
- For sentence endings, put new, technical, difficult, or important information last when possible.
- Readers tend to remember sentence endings most, sentence beginnings next, and middles least.
- A passage can be cohesive but not coherent if each sentence links locally while the paragraph topic keeps changing.

## Revision Moves

When revising academic prose:

1. Name the paragraph's governing idea in one sentence.
2. Move or rewrite the topic sentence so the reader gets that idea early.
3. Remove or split sentences that serve another governing idea.
4. Reorder sentences so old information leads to new information.
5. Put the heaviest claim, term, or consequence where the reader is prepared for it.
6. Check that the introduction makes the research territory, niche, and contribution visible.

## Reader-Centered Academic Questions

Use these questions when an academic draft feels technically correct but difficult to follow:

- What does the reader need to know before this claim makes sense?
- Which term, finding, method, or population appears before it has been motivated?
- Does the paragraph answer one reader question or several?
- Does each citation serve a visible function, such as context, contrast, evidence, method, or gap?
- Is the author listing studies, or building a path toward the research problem?
- Are limitations and uncertainty placed where they help the reader judge the claim?
