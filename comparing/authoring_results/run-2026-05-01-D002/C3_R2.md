# Caspase 5c amplifies Wnt signaling through APC cleavage in intestinal epithelial models

## Abstract

Inflammatory caspases are best known for innate immune sensing, but the function of human caspase 5 has remained less clearly defined than that of related caspases. Here, studies in human intestinal epithelial models identify a CARD-less CASP5 isoform, CASP5C, as an amplifier of Wnt/beta-catenin signaling. CASP5 was enriched in intestinal tissues and organoids, where three isoforms, CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C, were expressed. CASP5C, but not CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 or CASP4, increased Wnt reporter activity, promoted beta-catenin redistribution, and elevated Wnt-associated transcripts. Proximity labeling and co-immunoprecipitation linked CASP5 isoforms to Dishevelled proteins, and functional assays indicated that CASP5A and CASP5B can restrain CASP5C by competing for DVL2. CASP5C catalytic activity was required for pathway activation. CASP5C cleaved APC, with D556 contributing to a major cleavage pattern, thereby weakening destruction-complex assembly and reducing beta-catenin ubiquitination. In organoids, CASP5C was associated with proliferation, Wnt-dependent recovery after irradiation, and delayed differentiation. These findings support a model in which CASP5C is a primate-associated epithelial caspase isoform that couples inflammatory or injury-responsive contexts to Wnt pathway amplification, while leaving disease associations as hypotheses for future testing.

## Introduction

Caspase-family proteases participate in cell death, inflammation and host defense, yet individual family members can have functions that are difficult to infer from sequence similarity alone. Human CASP5 is closely related to CASP4 and to mouse caspase 11, but its principal activity has been unclear. In the tested noncanonical inflammasome response to cytosolic lipopolysaccharide and bacteria, human CASP4 resembled mouse caspase 11 more closely than CASP5 and acted as the main effector in intestinal epithelial cells and macrophages. This result left an important question: if CASP5 is not the dominant mediator of that response, what cellular function does it serve?

The intestine provided a plausible setting in which to address this question. CASP5 expression was concentrated in intestinal tract and appendix, with signal in epithelial cells and in subsets of monocyte and macrophage populations. In colonic organoids, CASP5 was expressed at higher levels than CASP4, CASP1 and PYCARD when compared with macrophages, whereas CASP4 showed a broader distribution. These expression patterns suggested that CASP5 may have a specialized epithelial role rather than simply acting as a redundant inflammatory caspase.

An epithelial function for CASP5 would also be biologically consequential because the intestinal epithelium depends on carefully tuned Wnt/beta-catenin signaling. Wnt signaling supports intestinal development, crypt maintenance, tissue repair, stem-cell renewal and differentiation decisions. Within this pathway, Dishevelled proteins connect receptor-level events to downstream beta-catenin regulation, while APC functions as a scaffold and tumor suppressor within the beta-catenin destruction complex. The work summarized here tests whether intestinal CASP5 isoforms intersect with this pathway and identifies CASP5C as a selective amplifier of Wnt signaling.

## Results

Human intestinal organoids expressed three CASP5 isoforms: CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C, with approximate predicted sizes of 49.7 kDa, 43.2 kDa and 33.3 kDa. The isoforms differ in their N-terminal CARD content. CASP5B lacks part of the CARD region, whereas CASP5C lacks the CARD entirely. siRNA experiments indicated that CASP5C was not merely a cleavage product of CASP5A or CASP5B. CASP5 expression increased after Salmonella infection and after IFN-beta or IFN-gamma stimulation in organoids, whereas lipopolysaccharide alone had little effect in epithelium, consistent with low TLR4 expression in that context.

To identify candidate pathways linked to CASP5, proximity labeling was performed in human colonic organoids. BioID2 analysis placed DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3 near CASP5, and Wnt-related terms emerged in the interactor analysis. Co-immunoprecipitation supported physical association between CASP5 isoforms and DVL proteins. DVL2-containing complexes also included AXIN1 and beta-catenin, placing the interaction near the regulatory core of the Wnt pathway. Mapping experiments implicated the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain in binding, although the available data do not by themselves prove that the interaction is direct.

Functional assays then distinguished CASP5C from the other isoforms. In TOPFlash reporter experiments in HEK293T and HCT116 cells, CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity both with and without WNT3A stimulation. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 and CASP4 did not produce the same effect. CASP5C also increased expression of AXIN2 and TCF7 and promoted movement of beta-catenin into the nucleus and cytosol. By contrast, CASP5A and CASP5B could compete with CASP5C for DVL2 and reduce the CASP5C-driven reporter response. CARD-less variants of mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce this effect, suggesting that the activity may reflect a primate CASP5 specialization after gene duplication, although this evolutionary interpretation remains provisional.

The same isoform specificity appeared in human intestinal organoids. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity. Organoids expressing CASP5C contained more viable cells, were approximately 40% larger, and showed more Ki-67-positive and EdU-positive cells. They also contained fewer CK20-positive enterocytes, indicating a shift away from differentiated enterocyte features. At the transcript level, CASP5C increased Wnt and proliferation-associated markers, including AXIN2 by about threefold, TCF7 by 1.4-fold, MYC by 1.3-fold, LGR5 by 1.2-fold and MKI67 by 2.4-fold. Differentiation-associated markers were reduced, with ALPI and MUC2 decreasing by about 57% and 53%, respectively.

Organoid behavior under Wnt withdrawal and injury-like stress further supported a role for CASP5C in epithelial plasticity. CASP5C-expressing organoids survived longer in the absence of Wnt and were able to re-enter the cell cycle when Wnt was restored. In irradiated human colonic organoids, CASP5c transcript increased 42.4-fold within 24 hours, compared with 5.6-fold for CASP5a and 6.6-fold for CASP5b. After irradiation, LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7 and MYC rose faster in CASP5C-expressing organoids. These observations are consistent with a repair-supportive function in the model system, but they do not establish that CASP5C has the same effect in the human intestine in vivo.

Single-cell and differentiation analyses placed the isoforms in distinct epithelial states. CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent from LGR5-positive stem cells in one single-cell analysis, higher in transit-amplifying cells, and highest overall in enterocytes. Within that broader pattern, CASP5c was detected deeper in crypt regions and overlapped more strongly with MKI67 and AXIN2 than CASP5a or CASP5b. CASP5A and CASP5B were more associated with luminal, differentiated and CK20-positive compartments. Pseudotime analysis and CIBERSORTx supported the same division: CASP5c tracked with early-lineage or transit-amplifying states, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b tracked with mature or differentiating states. During differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, while CASP5a and CASP5b increased later with differentiation markers.

Loss-of-function experiments supported a competition model among isoforms. Knocking down CASP5a and CASP5b while retaining CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity, organoid size, ATP content, EdU incorporation and Ki-67 staining. When CASP5c was also knocked down, these effects were reduced. This pattern indicates that CASP5A and CASP5B are not simply inactive bystanders. Instead, they can restrain the CASP5C effect, at least in part by competing for DVL2-containing complexes.

The mechanism depended on CASP5C catalytic activity. The catalytic mutant CASP5C(C173A) lost the ability to activate the reporter and failed to produce the beta-catenin effects seen with wild-type CASP5C. CASP5C had higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and removal of the CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased enzymatic activity and conferred stronger Wnt reporter signaling. These findings suggest that the CARD-containing N terminus restrains the protease-dependent Wnt-amplifying activity of CASP5, whereas CASP5C is configured to act more readily.

APC emerged as a key substrate linking CASP5C catalysis to Wnt pathway control. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites were found mainly in the N-terminal region of APC. CASP5C generated APC cleavage products, whereas CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C(C173A) did not. Recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro, supporting a substrate relationship. Mutation of APC D556 removed the major cleavage products, identifying D556 as an important primary site, although other APC cleavage sites may also contribute.

APC cleavage explained how CASP5C altered destruction-complex behavior. After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced the amounts of DVL2, beta-catenin, phospho-beta-catenin, APC and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes and reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, reintroduction of wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt complex assembly and increase reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A) weakened this response. APC fragments resembling cleavage products also reduced beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased reporter activity. Together, these data support a model in which CASP5C cleaves APC and thereby loosens destruction-complex control of beta-catenin.

## Discussion

These findings assign a specific Wnt-regulatory function to CASP5C and clarify why CASP5 should not be treated as a simple equivalent of CASP4 or mouse caspase 11. In the tested noncanonical inflammasome settings, CASP4 was the dominant caspase. CASP5, by contrast, was enriched in intestinal epithelial contexts and separated into isoforms with distinct effects on Wnt signaling. The CARD-less CASP5C isoform amplified Wnt/beta-catenin output, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B did not and could instead restrain CASP5C through competition for DVL2.

The proposed mechanism is notable because it connects a protease class associated with inflammation to APC, a central Wnt destruction-complex scaffold. CASP5C did not merely correlate with Wnt target expression; its catalytic activity was required, it produced APC cleavage products, recombinant CASP5C cleaved APC in vitro, and APC mutations or fragments altered the downstream response. D556 accounted for a major cleavage pattern, but additional APC cleavage events may contribute because APC is a large scaffold with multiple functional regions.

The organoid data suggest that CASP5C can tune epithelial state under conditions relevant to regeneration. CASP5C promoted proliferation-associated markers, reduced differentiation markers, improved persistence during Wnt withdrawal, and accelerated Wnt-associated recovery after irradiation. These phenotypes fit a model in which CASP5C helps epithelial cells remain responsive to Wnt or re-engage proliferative programs after stress. However, organoid behavior should not be overextended to claims about human tissue repair without in vivo evidence.

The disease observations are similarly hypothesis-generating. CASP5 and CASP4 were increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while maintaining cell-type patterns. CASP5 mutations appeared in approximately 3% of colon or rectal cancers in one analysis, similar to AXIN1, and tended to co-occur with Wnt-pathway mutations while showing mutual exclusivity with APC mutations. In microsatellite instability-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were reported more often than APC mutations in one comparison. These associations are intriguing because they place CASP5 near settings where Wnt signaling and epithelial inflammation matter, but they do not show that CASP5C causes ulcerative colitis, colorectal cancer or any other disease. They also do not establish CASP5C as a validated therapeutic target.

Overall, the study supports a model in which CASP5C is an intestinal epithelial caspase isoform that amplifies Wnt/beta-catenin signaling by cleaving APC and weakening destruction-complex control of beta-catenin. The work also highlights isoform balance as an important regulatory layer: CASP5C promotes the Wnt response, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B can dampen it by competing for DVL2. The next questions are whether the same mechanism operates in human intestinal tissue, how inflammatory and injury signals tune CASP5 isoforms, and whether CASP5C-dependent APC cleavage has measurable consequences in disease contexts where Wnt signaling is already altered.
