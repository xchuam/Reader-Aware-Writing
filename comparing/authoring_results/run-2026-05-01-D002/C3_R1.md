# Caspase 5c amplifies epithelial Wnt signaling through APC cleavage in human intestinal models

## Abstract

Inflammatory caspases are best known for host defense and inflammasome-associated cell death, but the function of human caspase 5 has remained less defined than that of caspase 4 and mouse caspase 11. Here, evidence from human intestinal epithelial models indicates that caspase 5 has an isoform-specific role in Wnt pathway regulation. Human intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C, with CASP5C lacking the N-terminal CARD domain. CASP5C, but not CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 or CASP4, increased Wnt reporter activity, promoted beta-catenin redistribution, elevated Wnt target and proliferation markers, and supported organoid growth under Wnt-limiting or injury-associated conditions. Mechanistically, CASP5C associated with dishevelled proteins and required catalytic activity to cleave APC, thereby weakening destruction-complex assembly and reducing beta-catenin ubiquitination. CASP5A and CASP5B competed with CASP5C for DVL2 and restrained its signaling effect. These findings identify CASP5C as an amplifier of epithelial Wnt signaling in intestinal organoid systems and suggest that caspase 5 isoform balance helps tune proliferation and differentiation during epithelial renewal.

## Introduction

The intestinal epithelium depends on a controlled balance between renewal and differentiation. Wnt/beta-catenin signaling is central to this balance because it sustains crypt-associated progenitor programs, supports repair after injury, and coordinates the transition toward mature epithelial lineages. Dishevelled proteins relay receptor-proximal Wnt signals to the beta-catenin destruction complex, whereas APC acts as a scaffold and tumor suppressor that helps maintain beta-catenin turnover when Wnt input is low. Because excessive or insufficient Wnt activity can disrupt homeostasis, intestinal cells require mechanisms that adjust signaling strength in response to lineage state and stress.

Inflammatory caspases are candidates for such context-dependent regulation, but their individual functions are not interchangeable. CASP4 is more similar to mouse caspase 11 by sequence and phylogeny and was the principal mediator of the tested noncanonical inflammasome response to cytosolic lipopolysaccharide and bacteria in human intestinal epithelial cells and macrophages. CASP5 did not dominate that response, raising the possibility that it has a distinct function in tissues where it is enriched. CASP5 was concentrated in the intestinal tract and appendix and was detected in epithelial cells as well as selected monocyte and macrophage populations. In colonic organoids, CASP5 expression exceeded CASP4, CASP1 and PYCARD relative to macrophages, whereas CASP4 showed a broader distribution.

Human colon and small-intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C, with approximate sizes of 49.7, 43.2 and 33.3 kDa. CASP5B lacked part of the CARD region, and CASP5C lacked the CARD domain entirely. siRNA experiments indicated that CASP5C was not simply a cleavage product of CASP5A or CASP5B. CASP5 expression increased after Salmonella infection and after interferon-beta or interferon-gamma treatment, whereas lipopolysaccharide alone induced little epithelial CASP5, consistent with low epithelial TLR4 expression. Together, these data framed CASP5 as an intestinal epithelial factor whose isoforms might have separate functions.

## Results

Proximity labeling in human colonic organoids recovered DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3 near CASP5, and Wnt-related terms appeared among the enriched interactors. Co-immunoprecipitation experiments supported this result: CASP5 isoforms associated with DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3, and DVL2-containing complexes also included AXIN1 and beta-catenin. Binding mapped to the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain. Although these data did not prove a direct binary interaction, they placed CASP5 close to core Wnt signaling machinery.

The functional consequence of this association was strongly isoform-specific. In TOPFlash reporter assays in HEK293T and HCT116 cells, CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity both with and without WNT3A stimulation. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 and CASP4 did not reproduce this effect. CASP5C also increased expression of Wnt-associated genes including AXIN2 and TCF7 and promoted accumulation of beta-catenin in nuclear and cytosolic fractions. By contrast, CASP5A and CASP5B could reduce the CASP5C effect by competing for DVL2, suggesting that CASP5 isoforms form a regulatory set rather than a single functional unit. CARD-less mouse caspase 11 forms did not mimic CASP5C-induced Wnt activation, arguing against a generic consequence of CARD removal and suggesting, with appropriate caution, a primate CASP5 specialization after gene duplication.

The same isoform specificity was observed in human intestinal organoids. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity. Organoids expressing CASP5C contained more viable cells, were approximately 40% larger, and showed more Ki-67- and EdU-positive cells. They also contained fewer CK20-positive enterocytes, consistent with a shift away from terminal differentiation under these experimental conditions. Molecular markers followed the same direction: AXIN2 increased by about threefold, TCF7 by 1.4-fold, MYC by 1.3-fold, LGR5 by 1.2-fold and MKI67 by 2.4-fold, whereas the differentiation-associated markers ALPI and MUC2 decreased by about 57% and 53%, respectively.

CASP5C also altered organoid behavior during Wnt withdrawal and recovery. CASP5C-expressing organoids could continue growing for longer without Wnt and could re-enter the cell cycle after Wnt was restored. In irradiated human colonic organoids, CASP5c transcript increased 42.4-fold within 24 hours, compared with 5.6-fold for CASP5a and 6.6-fold for CASP5b. After irradiation, CASP5C-expressing organoids showed faster increases in LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7 and MYC. These findings support a role for CASP5C in repair-like responses in organoid systems, although they do not establish the same function in human tissue in vivo.

Mechanistic experiments connected CASP5C activity to the beta-catenin destruction complex. After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phospho-beta-catenin, APC and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes. It also reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination, consistent with impaired destruction-complex function. These effects required catalytic activity: the CASP5C(C173A) mutant lost reporter activation and failed to induce the beta-catenin effects observed with wild-type CASP5C. CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and removing the CARD region from CASP5A or CASP5B increased activity and conferred stronger Wnt reporter signaling. The CARD domain therefore appears to restrain catalytic signaling output.

APC emerged as a relevant CASP5C substrate. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites were concentrated mainly in the N-terminal region of APC. In cells, CASP5C produced APC cleavage products, whereas CASP5A, CASP5B and the catalytically inactive CASP5C(C173A) mutant did not. Recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro, supporting APC as a substrate rather than an indirect downstream marker. Mutation of APC residue D556 removed the major cleavage products, identifying D556 as a primary cleavage site. However, the data do not exclude contributions from other APC cleavage sites.

Functional APC experiments linked cleavage to Wnt activation. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, reintroduction of wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the destruction complex and increase Wnt reporter activity. Reintroduction of APC(D556A) weakened this effect. APC fragments resembling CASP5C cleavage products reduced beta-catenin association with the destruction complex and increased reporter activity. These results support a model in which CASP5C binds near dishevelled-containing Wnt complexes and, through catalytic cleavage of APC, reduces destruction-complex efficiency, lowers beta-catenin ubiquitination and amplifies beta-catenin-dependent transcription.

The cellular localization of CASP5 isoforms added an additional layer to this model. CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent from LGR5-positive stem cells in one single-cell RNA analysis, were higher in transit-amplifying cells, and were highest in enterocytes. Yet isoform-resolved analyses suggested that CASP5c was more crypt-associated than CASP5a or CASP5b and overlapped more with MKI67 and AXIN2. CASP5A and CASP5B were more luminal and more associated with differentiated CK20-positive cells. Pseudotime analysis and CIBERSORTx supported the same pattern: CASP5c aligned with early-lineage or transit-amplifying states, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b aligned with mature or differentiating states. During differentiation induced by Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, while CASP5a and CASP5b rose later with differentiation markers.

Perturbation of isoform balance further supported a competition model. Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity, organoid size, ATP levels, EdU incorporation and Ki-67 staining. When CASP5c was also knocked down, these effects decreased. This result is consistent with CASP5A and CASP5B restraining CASP5C by competing for DVL2, thereby limiting CASP5C access to the signaling context in which it cleaves APC and amplifies Wnt output.

## Discussion

These findings redefine CASP5 as more than a secondary inflammatory caspase in human intestinal epithelium. CASP4 resembled mouse caspase 11 and carried the principal noncanonical inflammasome role in the tested settings, whereas CASP5, especially CASP5C, was linked to epithelial Wnt signaling. The distinction matters because CASP5 isoforms did not behave equivalently. CASP5C promoted Wnt reporter activation, beta-catenin redistribution, proliferation-associated gene expression and organoid expansion. CASP5A and CASP5B lacked the same activating effect and, in some contexts, limited CASP5C output by competition. Thus, the relevant biological variable is not simply total CASP5 expression but the relative abundance, localization and catalytic availability of its isoforms.

The proposed mechanism provides a plausible route from isoform structure to epithelial behavior. CASP5C lacks the CARD domain, has higher catalytic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, associates with DVL-containing complexes, and cleaves APC. APC cleavage reduces the integrity or function of the beta-catenin destruction complex, leading to less beta-catenin ubiquitination and stronger beta-catenin-dependent transcription. The D556 site appears to account for major cleavage products and contributes to the signaling effect, but the available evidence leaves room for additional cleavage events. This qualification is important because APC is a large scaffold with multiple functional regions, and partial cleavage patterns may have distinct consequences depending on cellular context.

The organoid data place this mechanism in a biologically meaningful setting. CASP5C expression was associated with larger organoids, increased proliferative markers and reduced differentiation markers. After irradiation, CASP5c rose more sharply than CASP5a or CASP5b, and CASP5C-expressing organoids more rapidly induced genes associated with Wnt activity and epithelial renewal. These results support a model in which CASP5C helps epithelial cells sustain or regain proliferative competence after stress. However, the evidence comes from organoid and cell-line experiments, so the data should not be interpreted as proof of a repair mechanism operating identically in human intestine in vivo.

The disease-associated observations are suggestive but not causal. CASP5 and CASP4 were increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining their cell-type patterns. CASP5 mutations were reported in approximately 3% of colon or rectal cancers in one analysis, a frequency similar to AXIN1, and tended to co-occur with Wnt-pathway mutations while being mutually exclusive with APC mutations. In MSI-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were reported more often than APC in one comparison, approximately 68% versus 17%. These associations are compatible with a connection between CASP5 and Wnt pathway states, but they do not establish that CASP5C causes inflammatory bowel disease or colorectal cancer, nor do they validate CASP5C as a therapeutic target.

Overall, the evidence supports an isoform-specific model in which CASP5C amplifies Wnt/beta-catenin signaling in human intestinal epithelial systems by engaging DVL-associated complexes and cleaving APC. CASP5A and CASP5B appear to provide an opposing or buffering influence, at least partly through DVL2 competition. Future work will need to determine how CASP5C is regulated in vivo, whether APC cleavage occurs in native human intestinal tissue, and how inflammatory cues, epithelial lineage state and Wnt pathway mutations shape this caspase-dependent signaling axis.
