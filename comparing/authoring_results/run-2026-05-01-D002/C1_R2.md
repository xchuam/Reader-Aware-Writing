# Caspase 5c amplifies Wnt signalling through APC cleavage in human intestinal epithelial models

## Abstract

Inflammatory caspases are best known for innate immune sensing, but the function of human caspase 5 has remained less clear than that of related proteases. Here, the evidence supports a distinct intestinal epithelial role for the CASP5C isoform. In human intestinal organoids and epithelial cell systems, CASP5 was enriched in intestinal tissues and induced by bacterial and cytokine stimuli, yet CASP5 did not substitute for CASP4 in tested noncanonical inflammasome responses. Instead, proximity labelling and co-immunoprecipitation connected CASP5 isoforms to Dishevelled proteins, placing CASP5 near the Wnt pathway. CASP5C selectively increased beta-catenin-dependent reporter activity, Wnt target expression, beta-catenin redistribution, organoid growth, and proliferative markers. Mechanistically, CASP5C required catalytic activity and cleaved APC, weakening destruction-complex interactions and reducing beta-catenin ubiquitination. CASP5A and CASP5B did not produce the same response and could restrain CASP5C, probably by competing for Dishevelled binding. These findings identify CASP5C as an epithelial amplifier of Wnt signalling in human intestinal organoid models.

## Introduction

The intestinal epithelium must coordinate immune sensing with continuous tissue renewal. Wnt/beta-catenin signalling is central to that renewal because it supports intestinal development, crypt maintenance, stem-cell activity, repair, and differentiation decisions. The pathway is controlled partly through Dishevelled proteins, which transmit receptor-proximal Wnt signals, and through the beta-catenin destruction complex, in which APC acts as a scaffold and tumour suppressor.

Human inflammatory caspases occupy a less settled position in this epithelial context. CASP4 closely resembles mouse caspase 11 in sequence and phylogeny and functions as a principal mediator of the tested noncanonical inflammasome response to cytosolic lipopolysaccharide and bacteria. CASP5 is related to CASP4 and mouse caspase 11, but its biological role has been harder to define. In human intestinal epithelial cells and macrophages, CASP5 did not perform the main noncanonical inflammasome function assigned to CASP4. This mismatch raised a more specific question: whether CASP5 has a different role, particularly in the intestine, where its expression is concentrated.

This study examined that possibility by following CASP5 expression, isoform function, binding partners, and pathway consequences in human intestinal organoids and supporting cell models. The central finding is that CARD-less CASP5C acts separately from CASP5A and CASP5B to amplify Wnt/beta-catenin signalling through catalytic cleavage of APC.

## Results

CASP5 expression first pointed toward an intestinal epithelial function rather than a broad duplicate of CASP4. CASP5 was concentrated in the intestinal tract and appendix and was detected in epithelial cells as well as some monocyte and macrophage populations. In human colonic organoids, CASP5 expression exceeded CASP4, CASP1, and PYCARD relative to macrophages, whereas CASP4 showed a broader pattern. Human colon and small-intestinal organoids expressed three CASP5 isoforms: CASP5A, CASP5B, and CASP5C, with approximate sizes of 49.7, 43.2, and 33.3 kDa. CASP5B lacks part of the CARD region, CASP5C lacks the CARD entirely, and siRNA experiments indicated that CASP5C was not simply a cleavage product of longer isoforms.

Stimulus-response experiments placed CASP5 in an epithelial stress context. In organoids, CASP5 increased after Salmonella exposure and after IFN-beta or IFN-gamma treatment. By contrast, lipopolysaccharide alone had little effect in intestinal epithelium, consistent with low epithelial TLR4 expression. These results suggested that infection- and cytokine-associated conditions could alter CASP5 abundance in an epithelium where CASP5 might have another function.

To identify that function, BioID2 proximity labelling was performed in human colonic organoids. The analysis detected DVL1, DVL2, and DVL3 near CASP5, and Wnt-related terms appeared among the enriched interaction categories. Co-immunoprecipitation supported this connection: CASP5 isoforms associated with DVL proteins, and DVL2-containing complexes also included AXIN1 and beta-catenin. The interaction mapped to the DVL DEP domain and CASP5 catalytic domain, placing CASP5 close to Wnt signalling components without proving a direct biochemical interaction.

Functional tests then separated the CASP5 isoforms. In TOPFlash reporter assays, CASP5C increased Wnt/beta-catenin reporter activity in HEK293T and HCT116 cells, both with and without WNT3A stimulation. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1, and CASP4 did not reproduce this effect. CASP5C also increased the Wnt target genes AXIN2 and TCF7 and shifted beta-catenin into the cytosolic and nuclear fractions. In contrast, CASP5A and CASP5B could reduce the CASP5C effect, apparently by competing with CASP5C for DVL2 binding. CARD-less mouse caspase 11 forms did not mimic the Wnt response, which is consistent with, but does not prove, a primate-specific specialization of CASP5 after gene duplication.

Human organoids showed the same isoform specificity in a more intestinal setting. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity. CASP5C-expressing colonic organoids contained more viable cells, were about 40% larger, had more Ki-67- and EdU-positive cells, and contained fewer CK20-positive enterocytes. Wnt and proliferation markers rose, including AXIN2, TCF7, MYC, LGR5, and MKI67, whereas ALPI and MUC2 decreased by about 57% and 53%. Together, these findings indicate that CASP5C shifts organoid state toward Wnt-associated proliferation and away from some differentiated epithelial features.

Additional organoid experiments connected CASP5C to epithelial recovery capacity. CASP5C-expressing organoids could persist longer without Wnt and re-enter the cell cycle after Wnt was restored. After irradiation of human colonic organoids, CASP5c transcript increased 42.4-fold within 24 hours, compared with 5.6-fold for CASP5a and 6.6-fold for CASP5b. In this setting, LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7, and MYC rose faster in CASP5C-expressing organoids. These results support a model in which CASP5C helps epithelial organoids maintain or regain proliferative Wnt responsiveness after stress. They do not, by themselves, establish the magnitude or necessity of this process in human intestinal repair in vivo.

The mechanism required CASP5C catalytic activity. A catalytic mutant, CASP5C(C173A), lost the ability to activate the Wnt reporter and to produce beta-catenin effects. CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B. Conversely, removing the CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased their activity and produced more Wnt reporter signalling. These observations suggest that CARD loss exposes or enables a protease activity that is relevant to Wnt amplification.

The downstream substrate analysis identified APC as a key target. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites in APC were concentrated mostly in the N-terminal region. CASP5C generated APC cleavage products, whereas CASP5A, CASP5B, and CASP5C(C173A) did not. Recombinant CASP5C also directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. Mutation of APC aspartate 556 to alanine removed the major cleavage products, identifying D556 as an important cleavage site. However, the data leave room for additional APC cleavage sites to contribute to the full effect.

APC cleavage provided a plausible route from CASP5C activity to beta-catenin stabilization. After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced the association of DVL2, beta-catenin, phospho-beta-catenin, APC, and beta-TrCP with AXIN1-containing complexes, and it reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the complex and increase Wnt reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A) weakened this response. APC fragments resembling cleavage products also reduced beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased reporter activity.

The cellular distribution of CASP5 isoforms added a second layer to this model. In one single-cell RNA-sequencing analysis, CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent from LGR5-positive stem cells, higher in transit-amplifying cells, and highest in enterocytes. Yet CASP5c was detected deeper in crypt regions and overlapped more with MKI67 and AXIN2 than CASP5a or CASP5b, which were more luminal and associated with differentiated CK20-positive cells. Pseudotime, CIBERSORTx, and Wnt-withdrawal differentiation analyses similarly associated CASP5c with early-lineage or transit-amplifying states and CASP5a/b with mature or differentiating states.

Perturbing isoform balance directly tested whether the longer isoforms restrain CASP5C. Knocking down CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity, organoid size, ATP levels, EdU incorporation, and Ki-67 staining. When CASP5c was also knocked down, these effects decreased again. This pattern is consistent with a competition model: CASP5A and CASP5B can bind DVL2 and limit CASP5C-driven Wnt amplification, whereas CASP5C is the isoform that carries the pro-Wnt activity.

Disease-associated observations extended the relevance of the pathway but remained correlative. CASP5 and CASP4 were increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining their cell-type patterns. CASP5 mutations appeared in about 3% of colon or rectal cancers, tended to co-occur with Wnt-pathway mutations, and were mutually exclusive with APC mutations in the reported analysis. In microsatellite instability-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were reported more often than APC mutations in one comparison, about 68% versus 17%. These associations justify further study, but they do not show that CASP5C causes inflammatory bowel disease or colorectal cancer, nor do they establish CASP5C as a therapeutic target.

## Discussion

The principal conclusion is that CASP5C links an inflammatory caspase-family protein to epithelial Wnt regulation. CASP5 did not behave as the main CASP4-like noncanonical inflammasome protease in the tested human cells. Instead, CARD-less CASP5C amplified beta-catenin signalling, increased Wnt target expression, promoted proliferative features in organoids, and cleaved APC in a catalysis-dependent manner. The longer isoforms did not share this activity and could oppose it, making isoform identity central to the interpretation.

This isoform specificity prevents an overbroad conclusion about CASP5. The evidence does not support the claim that all CASP5 proteins promote Wnt signalling. CASP5A and CASP5B bind DVL proteins but lack the same Wnt-amplifying effect, and their presence can dampen CASP5C activity. The most coherent model is therefore a regulated balance between a catalytically active CARD-less isoform and longer isoforms that compete for the Wnt signalling machinery.

The proposed mechanism also fits the organization of the Wnt pathway. APC is a central destruction-complex scaffold, so cleavage of APC provides a direct way to loosen beta-catenin control. The D556A mutation reduced major APC cleavage products and weakened CASP5C-dependent reporter activation, supporting the importance of this site. At the same time, D556 should not be treated as the only relevant site, because the APC sequence contains additional predicted CASP5 cleavage sites and the available data do not exclude their contribution.

The organoid data suggest a role for CASP5C in epithelial adaptation to stress. CASP5c rose sharply after irradiation, and CASP5C-expressing organoids showed faster recovery of Wnt-associated transcripts and better persistence during Wnt withdrawal. These findings are compatible with a homeostatic or repair-supporting role in epithelial models. The boundary of the conclusion is equally important: organoids capture key features of human intestinal epithelium, but they do not prove that the same mechanism determines repair outcomes in vivo.

In disease, the findings are best viewed as a rationale for further study. Ulcerative colitis datasets and colorectal cancer mutation patterns show that CASP5 alterations can appear in contexts where epithelial inflammation, differentiation, and Wnt signalling are relevant. However, association is not causation, and the data do not justify a therapy claim. Future work should test whether CASP5C activity is necessary for epithelial repair in vivo, whether CASP5 isoform balance changes during disease, and how APC cleavage by CASP5C interacts with established Wnt-pathway mutations. For now, the study defines CASP5C as a Wnt-amplifying epithelial caspase isoform and places APC cleavage at the centre of that activity.
