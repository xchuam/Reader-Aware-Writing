# Caspase 5c amplifies Wnt signalling through APC cleavage in human intestinal epithelium

## Abstract

Inflammatory caspases are best known for innate immune responses, but individual family members may also have tissue-specific functions outside canonical inflammasome pathways. CASP5 is closely related to human CASP4 and mouse caspase 11, yet its function has remained unclear, particularly in intestinal epithelial cells where CASP5 expression is enriched. Here, analyses of human intestinal organoids, epithelial cell lines, macrophages and interaction assays identify the CARD-less isoform CASP5C as a positive regulator of Wnt/beta-catenin signalling. CASP5C, but not CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 or CASP4, increased Wnt reporter activity, promoted beta-catenin redistribution, induced Wnt-responsive genes and supported proliferative features in intestinal organoids. Mechanistically, CASP5C associated with DVL proteins and cleaved APC, weakening destruction-complex assembly and reducing beta-catenin ubiquitination. CASP5A and CASP5B competed with CASP5C for DVL2 and restrained this effect. These findings define an epithelial CASP5C-APC axis that links an inflammatory caspase family member to Wnt control in human intestinal homeostasis.

## Introduction

The intestinal epithelium must renew rapidly while maintaining differentiated barrier functions. Wnt/beta-catenin signalling is central to this balance because it supports intestinal development, repair, stem-cell renewal and lineage decisions. In this pathway, DVL proteins connect receptor-level Wnt inputs to downstream beta-catenin regulation, whereas APC functions as a scaffold and tumour suppressor within the beta-catenin destruction complex. Changes in the assembly or activity of this complex can therefore alter whether epithelial cells maintain a proliferative programme or progress toward differentiation.

Inflammatory caspases provide a less expected entry point into this epithelial signalling problem. CASP5 belongs to the inflammatory caspase family and is related to CASP4 and mouse caspase 11. CASP4 more closely resembles mouse caspase 11 in sequence and phylogeny, and in the tested human systems CASP4, rather than CASP5, acted as the principal mediator of noncanonical inflammasome responses to cytosolic LPS and bacteria. CASP5 did not perform that role in human intestinal epithelial cells or macrophages, raising the possibility that CASP5 has a different function.

Several observations focused attention on the intestine. CASP5 expression was concentrated in the intestinal tract and appendix and was detected in epithelial cells as well as in some monocyte and macrophage populations. In human colonic organoids, CASP5 was expressed at higher relative levels than CASP4, CASP1 and PYCARD compared with macrophages. Human colon and small-intestinal organoids expressed three CASP5 isoforms: CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C. CASP5B lacks part of the CARD region, CASP5C lacks the CARD entirely, and siRNA experiments indicated that CASP5C was not simply a cleavage product of CASP5A or CASP5B. Together, these features suggested that CASP5 isoforms might partition distinct epithelial functions.

The study therefore asked whether CASP5, and specifically one of its isoforms, contributes to intestinal epithelial signalling independently of the noncanonical inflammasome response. The findings identify CASP5C as a Wnt-amplifying isoform that acts through DVL-associated APC cleavage, while CASP5A and CASP5B appear to restrain this activity by competition for DVL2.

## Results

CASP5 expression in intestinal epithelium was responsive to inflammatory and microbial cues, but its pattern did not match a simple LPS-triggered inflammasome model. In organoids, CASP5 increased after Salmonella infection and after IFN-beta or IFN-gamma exposure, whereas LPS alone induced little epithelial CASP5, consistent with low TLR4 expression. This pattern placed CASP5 at an interface between inflammatory stimulation and epithelial biology.

Proximity labelling in human colonic organoids then identified proteins near CASP5. BioID2 analysis recovered DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3, and Wnt-related terms appeared in the interactor analysis. Co-immunoprecipitation supported association between CASP5 isoforms and DVL proteins. DVL2-containing complexes also included AXIN1 and beta-catenin, placing the interaction near the Wnt destruction complex. Mapping experiments implicated the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain in binding, although the available evidence did not fully establish whether the interaction was direct.

Functional tests then distinguished CASP5C from the other inflammatory caspases and CASP5 isoforms. In TOPFlash assays in HEK293T and HCT116 cells, CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity with or without WNT3A stimulation. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 and CASP4 did not produce the same effect. CASP5C also increased expression of Wnt-responsive markers, including AXIN2 and TCF7, and promoted beta-catenin accumulation in nuclear and cytosolic fractions. CARD-less forms of mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce the Wnt effect, suggesting a possible primate CASP5 specialization after gene duplication, but this comparison does not establish the evolutionary origin of the function.

The same isoform specificity was evident in human intestinal organoids. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity. CASP5C-expressing organoids contained more viable cells, were about 40% larger, and showed more Ki-67-positive and EdU-positive cells. At the same time, they contained fewer CK20-positive enterocytes, indicating a shift away from differentiated enterocyte features. Gene expression changes were consistent with this phenotype: CASP5C increased AXIN2 by about threefold, TCF7 by 1.4-fold, MYC by 1.3-fold, LGR5 by 1.2-fold and MKI67 by 2.4-fold, whereas ALPI and MUC2 decreased by about 57% and 53%, respectively.

The organoid phenotypes also suggested that CASP5C can support epithelial cells under Wnt-limited or injury-like conditions. CASP5C-expressing organoids survived longer after Wnt withdrawal and could re-enter the cell cycle when Wnt was restored. After irradiation, CASP5c transcript rose 42.4-fold within 24 hours, compared with 5.6-fold for CASP5a and 6.6-fold for CASP5b; LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7 and MYC then increased more rapidly in CASP5C-expressing organoids. These findings are consistent with regenerative signalling in organoids, but they do not prove repair in vivo.

Mechanistic experiments linked CASP5C activity to remodelling of the beta-catenin destruction complex. After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phosphorylated beta-catenin, APC and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes and decreased beta-catenin ubiquitination. These changes provide a plausible route from CASP5C-DVL association to increased beta-catenin signalling.

The catalytic activity of CASP5C was required for this pathway. A CASP5C(C173A) mutant lost reporter activation and beta-catenin effects. CASP5C also showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B. Consistent with the idea that the CARD constrains activity, removing the CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased enzymatic activity and Wnt reporter signalling. Thus, CASP5C's CARD-less architecture is closely linked to its signalling output.

APC emerged as a key substrate connecting CASP5C activity to Wnt amplification. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites in APC were concentrated mainly in the N-terminal region. CASP5C generated APC cleavage products, whereas CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C(C173A) did not. Recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro, supporting APC as a CASP5C substrate. Mutation of APC D556 removed the major cleavage products, identifying D556 as a primary site, although other APC cleavage sites may also contribute to the pathway.

APC rescue and fragment experiments connected cleavage to destruction-complex function. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, expression of wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the complex and increase Wnt reporter activity. APC(D556A) weakened this effect. APC fragments resembling cleavage products reduced beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased Wnt reporter activity. These results support a model in which CASP5C cleaves APC, with D556 serving as a major site, and thereby compromises APC-dependent beta-catenin control.

CASP5 isoforms also differed across epithelial states. CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent in LGR5-positive stem cells in one single-cell analysis, higher in transit-amplifying cells, and highest in enterocytes. Within that broad pattern, CASP5c was detected deeper in crypt regions and overlapped more with MKI67 and AXIN2 than CASP5a or CASP5b. CASP5A and CASP5B were more luminal and associated with differentiated or CK20-positive populations. Pseudotime and CIBERSORTx analyses similarly linked CASP5c with early-lineage or transit-amplifying states. During differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, whereas CASP5a/b peaked later with differentiation markers.

The opposing isoform patterns were functionally relevant. CASP5A and CASP5B could compete with CASP5C for DVL2 and reduce CASP5C-dependent Wnt activation. Conversely, knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity, organoid size, ATP content, EdU incorporation and Ki-67 positivity. When CASP5c was also knocked down, these effects decreased. This genetic relationship supports a competition model in which CASP5A and CASP5B restrain CASP5C by limiting its access to DVL-associated Wnt complexes.

Disease-associated data were consistent with relevance to intestinal pathology but did not establish causality. CASP5 and CASP4 were increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining their cell-type patterns. CASP5 mutations occurred in approximately 3% of colon or rectal cancers, tended to co-occur with Wnt-pathway mutations, and were mutually exclusive with APC mutations. In MSI-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were reported more often than APC mutations in one comparison, approximately 68% versus 17%. These associations do not show that CASP5C causes ulcerative colitis, colorectal cancer or therapeutic vulnerability.

## Discussion

This study defines a noncanonical role for a human inflammatory caspase isoform in intestinal epithelial Wnt signalling. The central finding is isoform-specific: CASP5C amplifies Wnt/beta-catenin activity, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B do not share this activity and can instead restrain CASP5C through DVL2 competition. This distinction is essential because CASP5 cannot be treated as a single functional entity in the intestinal epithelium.

The proposed mechanism links CASP5C to the Wnt destruction complex through DVL proteins and APC cleavage. CASP5C associates with DVL-containing complexes, requires catalytic activity for reporter and beta-catenin effects, directly cleaves APC in vitro, and depends in part on a major APC cleavage site at D556. Cleavage of APC provides a mechanistic explanation for reduced destruction-complex components, decreased beta-catenin ubiquitination and increased Wnt target expression. At the same time, D556 should be viewed as a primary site rather than the only possible site, and the directness of CASP5-DVL binding remains incompletely resolved.

The biological meaning of the pathway is most strongly supported in organoids. CASP5C promoted Wnt-responsive transcription, proliferation markers, organoid growth and recovery after Wnt restoration or irradiation-like stress. These observations fit a model in which CASP5C helps transit-amplifying or early-lineage epithelial cells sustain a regenerative Wnt programme. The expression data reinforce that model because CASP5c aligned more closely with crypt, MKI67-positive and AXIN2-positive states than did CASP5a/b. However, the organoid and dataset findings should not be extended to claims of proven human tissue repair without additional in vivo evidence.

The disease observations are best interpreted as hypotheses for future work. Increased CASP5 and CASP4 in ulcerative colitis datasets, recurrent CASP5 mutations in colorectal cancer subsets, and relationships with Wnt-pathway mutation patterns all suggest that CASP5 isoform biology may matter in inflamed or transformed intestinal tissue. Yet these data are associative. They do not establish CASP5C as a driver of disease or a validated drug target. A useful next step would be to determine how CASP5 isoform abundance, catalytic activity and APC cleavage vary across defined epithelial cell states during injury, inflammation and tumorigenesis.

Overall, CASP5C expands the functional range of inflammatory caspase-family proteins beyond microbial sensing and inflammasome execution. In human intestinal epithelial models, the CARD-less CASP5C isoform uses catalytic activity to cleave APC and amplify Wnt/beta-catenin signalling, while related CASP5 isoforms can temper this activity. This isoform-resolved view provides a framework for studying how inflammatory cues and epithelial renewal programmes intersect without assuming that all CASP5 products share the same function.
