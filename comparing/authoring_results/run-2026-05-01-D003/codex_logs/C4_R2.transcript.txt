# Scientific Writing

## Overview

Scientific writing is a process for communicating research with precision and clarity. Write manuscripts using IMRAD structure, citations (APA/AMA/Vancouver), figures/tables, and reporting guidelines (CONSORT/STROBE/PRISMA). Apply this skill for research papers and journal submissions.

## When to Use This Skill

This skill should be used when:
- Writing or revising any section of a scientific manuscript (abstract, introduction, methods, results, discussion)
- Structuring a research paper using IMRAD or other standard formats
- Formatting citations and references in specific styles (APA, AMA, Vancouver, Chicago, IEEE)
- Creating, formatting, or improving figures, tables, and data visualizations
- Applying study-specific reporting guidelines (CONSORT for trials, STROBE for observational studies, PRISMA for reviews)
- Drafting abstracts that meet journal requirements (structured or unstructured)
- Preparing manuscripts for submission to specific journals
- Improving writing clarity, conciseness, and precision
- Ensuring proper use of field-specific terminology and nomenclature
- Addressing reviewer comments and revising manuscripts

## Core Capabilities

### 1. Manuscript Structure and Organization

**IMRAD Format**: Guide papers through the standard Introduction, Methods, Results, And Discussion structure used across most scientific disciplines. This includes:
- **Introduction**: Establish research context, identify gaps, state objectives
- **Methods**: Detail study design, populations, procedures, and analysis approaches
- **Results**: Present findings objectively without interpretation
- **Discussion**: Interpret results, acknowledge limitations, propose future directions

For detailed guidance on IMRAD structure, refer to `references/imrad_structure.md`.

**Alternative Structures**: Support discipline-specific formats including:
- Review articles (narrative, systematic, scoping)
- Case reports and case series
- Meta-analyses and pooled analyses
- Theoretical/modeling papers
- Methods papers and protocols

### 2. Section-Specific Writing Guidance

**Abstract Composition**: Craft concise, standalone summaries (100-250 words) that capture the paper's purpose, methods, results, and conclusions. Support both structured abstracts (with labeled sections) and unstructured single-paragraph formats.

**Introduction Development**: Build compelling introductions that:
- Establish the research problem's importance
- Review relevant literature systematically
- Identify knowledge gaps or controversies
- State clear research questions or hypotheses
- Explain the study's novelty and significance

**Methods Documentation**: Ensure reproducibility through:
- Detailed participant/sample descriptions
- Clear procedural documentation
- Statistical methods with justification
- Equipment and materials specifications
- Ethical approval and consent statements

**Results Presentation**: Present findings with:
- Logical flow from primary to secondary outcomes
- Integration with figures and tables
- Statistical significance with effect sizes
- Objective reporting without interpretation

**Discussion Construction**: Synthesize findings by:
- Relating results to research questions
- Comparing with existing literature
- Acknowledging limitations honestly
- Proposing mechanistic explanations
- Suggesting practical implications and future research

### 3. Citation and Reference Management

Apply citation styles correctly across disciplines. For comprehensive style guides, refer to `references/citation_styles.md`.

**Major Citation Styles:**
- **AMA (American Medical Association)**: Numbered superscript citations, common in medicine
- **Vancouver**: Numbered citations in square brackets, biomedical standard
- **APA (American Psychological Association)**: Author-date in-text citations, common in social sciences
- **Chicago**: Notes-bibliography or author-date, humanities and sciences
- **IEEE**: Numbered square brackets, engineering and computer science

**Best Practices:**
- Cite primary sources when possible
- Include recent literature (last 5-10 years for active fields)
- Balance citation distribution across introduction and discussion
- Verify all citations against original sources
- Use reference management software (Zotero, Mendeley, EndNote)

### 4. Figures and Tables

Create effective data visualizations that enhance comprehension. For detailed best practices, refer to `references/figures_tables.md`.

**When to Use Tables vs. Figures:**
- **Tables**: Precise numerical data, complex datasets, multiple variables requiring exact values
- **Figures**: Trends, patterns, relationships, comparisons best understood visually

**Design Principles:**
- Make each table/figure self-explanatory with complete captions
- Use consistent formatting and terminology across all display items
- Label all axes, columns, and rows with units
- Include sample sizes (n) and statistical annotations
- Follow the "one table/figure per 1000 words" guideline
- Avoid duplicating information between text, tables, and figures

**Common Figure Types:**
- Bar graphs: Comparing discrete categories
- Line graphs: Showing trends over time
- Scatterplots: Displaying correlations
- Box plots: Showing distributions and outliers
- Heatmaps: Visualizing matrices and patterns

### 5. Reporting Guidelines by Study Type

Ensure completeness and transparency by following established reporting standards. For comprehensive guideline details, refer to `references/reporting_guidelines.md`.

**Key Guidelines:**
- **CONSORT**: Randomized controlled trials
- **STROBE**: Observational studies (cohort, case-control, cross-sectional)
- **PRISMA**: Systematic reviews and meta-analyses
- **STARD**: Diagnostic accuracy studies
- **TRIPOD**: Prediction model studies
- **ARRIVE**: Animal research
- **CARE**: Case reports
- **SQUIRE**: Quality improvement studies
- **SPIRIT**: Study protocols for clinical trials
- **CHEERS**: Economic evaluations

Each guideline provides checklists ensuring all critical methodological elements are reported.

### 6. Writing Principles and Style

Apply fundamental scientific writing principles. For detailed guidance, refer to `references/writing_principles.md`.

**Clarity**:
- Use precise, unambiguous language
- Define technical terms and abbreviations at first use
- Maintain logical flow within and between paragraphs
- Use active voice when appropriate for clarity

**Conciseness**:
- Eliminate redundant words and phrases
- Favor shorter sentences (15-20 words average)
- Remove unnecessary qualifiers
- Respect word limits strictly

**Accuracy**:
- Report exact values with appropriate precision
- Use consistent terminology throughout
- Distinguish between observations and interpretations
- Acknowledge uncertainty appropriately

**Objectivity**:
- Present results without bias
- Avoid overstating findings or implications
- Acknowledge conflicting evidence
- Maintain professional, neutral tone

### 7. Journal-Specific Formatting

Adapt manuscripts to journal requirements:
- Follow author guidelines for structure, length, and format
- Apply journal-specific citation styles
- Meet figure/table specifications (resolution, file formats, dimensions)
- Include required statements (funding, conflicts of interest, data availability, ethical approval)
- Adhere to word limits for each section
- Format according to template requirements when provided

### 8. Field-Specific Language and Terminology

Adapt language, terminology, and conventions to match the specific scientific discipline. Each field has established vocabulary, preferred phrasings, and domain-specific conventions that signal expertise and ensure clarity for the target audience.

**Identify Field-Specific Linguistic Conventions:**
- Review terminology used in recent high-impact papers in the target journal
- Note field-specific abbreviations, units, and notation systems
- Identify preferred terms (e.g., "participants" vs. "subjects," "compound" vs. "drug," "specimens" vs. "samples")
- Observe how methods, organisms, or techniques are typically described

**Biomedical and Clinical Sciences:**
- Use precise anatomical and clinical terminology (e.g., "myocardial infarction" not "heart attack" in formal writing)
- Follow standardized disease nomenclature (ICD, DSM, SNOMED-CT)
- Specify drug names using generic names first, brand names in parentheses if needed
- Use "patients" for clinical studies, "participants" for community-based research
- Follow Human Genome Variation Society (HGVS) nomenclature for genetic variants
- Report lab values with standard units (SI units in most international journals)

**Molecular Biology and Genetics:**
- Use italics for gene symbols (e.g., *TP53*), regular font for proteins (e.g., p53)
- Follow species-specific gene nomenclature (uppercase for human: *BRCA1*; sentence case for mouse: *Brca1*)
- Specify organism names in full at first mention, then use accepted abbreviations (e.g., *Escherichia coli*, then *E. coli*)
- Use standard genetic notation (e.g., +/+, +/-, -/- for genotypes)
- Employ established terminology for molecular techniques (e.g., "quantitative PCR" or "qPCR," not "real-time PCR")

**Chemistry and Pharmaceutical Sciences:**
- Follow IUPAC nomenclature for chemical compounds
- Use systematic names for novel compounds, common names for well-known substances
- Specify chemical structures using standard notation (e.g., SMILES, InChI for databases)
- Report concentrations with appropriate units (mM, μM, nM, or % w/v, v/v)
- Describe synthesis routes using accepted reaction nomenclature
- Use terms like "bioavailability," "pharmacokinetics," "IC50" consistently with field definitions

**Ecology and Environmental Sciences:**
- Use binomial nomenclature for species (italicized: *Homo sapiens*)
- Specify taxonomic authorities at first species mention when relevant
- Employ standardized habitat and ecosystem classifications
- Use consistent terminology for ecological metrics (e.g., "species richness," "Shannon diversity index")
- Describe sampling methods with field-standard terms (e.g., "transect," "quadrat," "mark-recapture")

**Physics and Engineering:**
- Follow SI units consistently unless field conventions dictate otherwise
- Use standard notation for physical quantities (scalars vs. vectors, tensors)
- Employ established terminology for phenomena (e.g., "quantum entanglement," "laminar flow")
- Specify equipment with model numbers and manufacturers when relevant
- Use mathematical notation consistent with field standards (e.g., ℏ for reduced Planck constant)

**General Principles:**

**Match Audience Expertise:**
- For specialized journals: Use field-specific terminology freely, define only highly specialized or novel terms
- For broad-impact journals (e.g., *Nature*, *Science*): Define more technical terms, provide context for specialized concepts

**Define Technical Terms Strategically:**
- Define abbreviations at first use: "messenger RNA (mRNA)"
- Provide brief explanations for specialized techniques when writing for broader audiences
- Avoid over-defining terms well-known to the target audience (signals unfamiliarity with field)
- Create a glossary if numerous specialized terms are unavoidable

**Maintain Consistency:**
- Use the same term for the same concept throughout (don't alternate between "medication," "drug," and "pharmaceutical")
- Follow a consistent system for abbreviations (decide on "PCR" or "polymerase chain reaction" after first definition)
- Apply the same nomenclature system throughout (especially for genes, species, chemicals)

**Avoid Field Mixing Errors:**
- Don't use clinical terminology for basic science (e.g., don't call mice "patients")
- Avoid colloquialisms or overly general terms in place of precise field terminology
- Don't import terminology from adjacent fields without ensuring proper usage

**Verify Terminology Usage:**
- Consult field-specific style guides and nomenclature resources
- Check how terms are used in recent papers from the target journal
- Use domain-specific databases and ontologies (e.g., Gene Ontology, MeSH terms)
- When uncertain, cite a key reference that establishes terminology

### 9. Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**Top Rejection Reasons:**
1. Inappropriate, incomplete, or insufficiently described statistics
2. Over-interpretation of results or unsupported conclusions
3. Poorly described methods affecting reproducibility
4. Small, biased, or inappropriate samples
5. Poor writing quality or difficult-to-follow text
6. Inadequate literature review or context
7. Figures and tables that are unclear or poorly designed
8. Failure to follow reporting guidelines

**Writing Quality Issues:**
- Mixing tenses inappropriately (use past tense for methods/results, present for established facts)
- Excessive jargon or undefined acronyms
- Paragraph breaks that disrupt logical flow
- Missing transitions between sections
- Inconsistent notation or terminology

## Workflow for Manuscript Development

**Stage 1: Planning**
1. Identify target journal and review author guidelines
2. Determine applicable reporting guideline (CONSORT, STROBE, etc.)
3. Outline manuscript structure (usually IMRAD)
4. Plan figures and tables as the backbone of the paper

**Stage 2: Drafting**
1. Start with figures and tables (the core data story)
2. Write Methods (often easiest to draft first)
3. Draft Results (describing figures/tables objectively)
4. Compose Discussion (interpreting findings)
5. Write Introduction (setting up the research question)
6. Craft Abstract (synthesizing the complete story)
7. Create Title (concise and descriptive)

**Stage 3: Revision**
1. Check logical flow and "red thread" throughout
2. Verify consistency in terminology and notation
3. Ensure figures/tables are self-explanatory
4. Confirm adherence to reporting guidelines
5. Verify all citations are accurate and properly formatted
6. Check word counts for each section
7. Proofread for grammar, spelling, and clarity

**Stage 4: Final Preparation**
1. Format according to journal requirements
2. Prepare supplementary materials
3. Write cover letter highlighting significance
4. Complete submission checklists
5. Gather all required statements and forms

+# CASP5C amplifies epithelial Wnt signalling through APC cleavage in human intestinal organoids
+
+## Abstract
+
+Inflammatory caspases are best known as mediators of innate immune responses, yet the functions of human CASP5 have remained less defined than those of CASP4 and mouse caspase 11. Here we describe a noncanonical role for a CARD-less CASP5 isoform, CASP5C, in human intestinal epithelial Wnt signalling. Human intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C, with CASP5 enriched in intestinal epithelial contexts and inducible by infection-associated and interferon signals. Proximity labelling and co-immunoprecipitation placed CASP5 isoforms near dishevelled proteins DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3. Functional assays showed that CASP5C, but not CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 or CASP4, increased Wnt reporter activity, promoted beta-catenin redistribution, and induced Wnt target genes. In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased organoid size, viability and proliferation while reducing differentiation markers. Mechanistically, CASP5C required catalytic activity and cleaved APC, with D556 contributing to major cleavage products but not excluding additional sites. CASP5A and CASP5B competed with CASP5C for DVL2 and restrained its effect. These findings identify CASP5C as an epithelial amplifier of Wnt signalling and suggest that alternative CASP5 isoforms partition inflammatory caspase family functions in the human intestine.
+
+## Introduction
+
+Human inflammatory caspases participate in host defence and inflammatory signalling, but individual family members have not been functionally resolved to the same extent. CASP4 is closely related to mouse caspase 11 and acts as a principal human mediator of noncanonical inflammasome responses to cytosolic lipopolysaccharide and intracellular bacteria in the tested epithelial and macrophage systems. By contrast, CASP5 did not carry this dominant function in these assays, raising the possibility that CASP5 has diverged toward a distinct role.
+
+The intestine is a plausible setting for such specialization. CASP5 expression is concentrated in the intestinal tract and appendix and is detected in epithelial cells as well as selected monocyte and macrophage populations. In human colonic organoids, CASP5 expression exceeded that of CASP4, CASP1 and PYCARD when compared with macrophages, whereas CASP4 showed broader expression. These patterns suggested that CASP5 might have a cell-state-specific epithelial function rather than serving only as a duplicate of CASP4.
+
+Intestinal epithelial renewal depends on Wnt/beta-catenin signalling. Wnt activity maintains stem and proliferative compartments, supports repair, and regulates the balance between renewal and differentiation. Dishevelled proteins connect receptor-proximal Wnt events to downstream beta-catenin control, whereas APC is a scaffold and tumour suppressor within the beta-catenin destruction complex. Because inappropriate Wnt signalling is closely linked to colorectal tumorigenesis, any new modulator of this pathway requires careful functional and mechanistic definition.
+
+Here we investigate whether CASP5 isoforms contribute to intestinal epithelial signalling. The data identify CASP5C, a CARD-less isoform, as a selective enhancer of Wnt/beta-catenin activity. CASP5C acts through its catalytic domain, associates with DVL-containing complexes, and cleaves APC to weaken beta-catenin destruction complex assembly. CASP5A and CASP5B do not share this activity and instead can restrain CASP5C, supporting an isoform competition model in which CASP5 products exert opposing effects on epithelial Wnt tone.
+
+## Methods
+
+Human intestinal expression patterns were assessed across tissue, organoid and single-cell datasets described in the source studies. Human colonic and small-intestinal organoids were used to examine CASP5 isoform expression and function. CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C corresponded approximately to 49.7 kDa, 43.2 kDa and 33.3 kDa proteins. CASP5B lacked part of the CARD region, whereas CASP5C lacked the CARD domain. Small interfering RNA experiments distinguished CASP5C from a cleavage product of CASP5A or CASP5B.
+
+Inducible expression was examined after Salmonella exposure and interferon stimulation. Lipopolysaccharide alone was evaluated in epithelial contexts, where limited response was consistent with low TLR4 expression. CASP5-interacting proteins were identified by BioID2 proximity labelling in human colonic organoids and by co-immunoprecipitation of CASP5 isoforms with DVL proteins. DVL2-containing complexes were further assessed for AXIN1 and beta-catenin association.
+
+Wnt signalling was measured with TOPFlash reporter assays in HEK293T, HCT116 and intestinal organoid systems, with and without WNT3A stimulation. CASP5 isoforms were compared with CASP1 and CASP4. Wnt target gene expression, beta-catenin localization, organoid viability, organoid size, Ki-67 staining, EdU incorporation, ATP content and differentiation markers were used as functional readouts. Catalytic dependence was tested with CASP5C(C173A). CARD deletion and mouse caspase 11 constructs were used to evaluate structural and species-related specificity.
+
+Mechanistic experiments examined AXIN1-containing destruction complexes, beta-catenin ubiquitination and APC cleavage. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites in APC were tested using cellular expression, recombinant CASP5C cleavage of truncated APC in vitro, APC knockout HEK293T rescue, APC(D556A), and APC fragments resembling cleavage products. Disease-associated observations from ulcerative colitis and colorectal cancer datasets were treated as associations, not causal evidence.
+
+## Results
+
+CASP5 showed a distinctive intestinal epithelial profile. CASP5 expression was enriched in intestinal tract and appendix, and human intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C. The three isoforms differed structurally: CASP5A retained the canonical architecture, CASP5B lacked part of the CARD, and CASP5C lacked the CARD entirely. CASP5C was not explained by proteolytic processing of longer isoforms, because isoform-targeted knockdown experiments supported its identity as a transcript-derived product.
+
+CASP5 expression responded to microbial and cytokine cues in organoids. Salmonella, interferon-beta and interferon-gamma increased CASP5, whereas lipopolysaccharide alone produced little induction in epithelial cells, consistent with limited epithelial TLR4 responsiveness. These observations placed CASP5 at the intersection of epithelial state and inflammatory stimulation, but did not support assigning CASP5 the same primary cytosolic lipopolysaccharide-sensing role as CASP4.
+
+BioID2 proximity labelling in human colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3 near CASP5, and Wnt-related terms were enriched among candidate interactors. Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed association of CASP5 isoforms with DVL proteins. DVL2 complexes also contained AXIN1 and beta-catenin, placing CASP5 near the machinery that regulates beta-catenin stability. The interaction involved the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain. Although these data strongly support close physical association, they do not by themselves prove a direct binary interaction.
+
+Functional testing separated CASP5C from the other isoforms. CASP5C increased TOPFlash reporter activity in HEK293T and HCT116 cells, both with and without WNT3A. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 and CASP4 did not produce the same response. CASP5C also increased AXIN2 and TCF7 expression and redistributed beta-catenin into cytosolic and nuclear pools. In contrast, CASP5A and CASP5B could compete with CASP5C for DVL2 binding and reduce CASP5C-driven Wnt activation. CARD-less mouse caspase 11 constructs did not reproduce the Wnt effect, suggesting that this activity may reflect a primate CASP5 specialization after gene duplication, although this evolutionary interpretation remains provisional.
+
+In human intestinal organoids, CASP5C again selectively enhanced Wnt activity. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter signalling. CASP5C-expressing colonic organoids contained more viable cells, were approximately 40% larger, and showed increased Ki-67 and EdU positivity. Differentiated enterocyte features were reduced, including fewer CK20-positive cells. At the transcript level, CASP5C increased AXIN2 by about threefold, TCF7 by 1.4-fold, MYC by 1.3-fold, LGR5 by 1.2-fold and MKI67 by 2.4-fold. Differentiation-associated ALPI and MUC2 decreased by approximately 57% and 53%, respectively.
+
+CASP5C also altered organoid behaviour during Wnt limitation and recovery. CASP5C-expressing organoids persisted longer after Wnt withdrawal and re-entered the cell cycle more readily when Wnt was restored. After irradiation of human colonic organoids, CASP5c transcript increased 42.4-fold within 24 hours, compared with 5.6-fold for CASP5a and 6.6-fold for CASP5b. Following this induction, LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7 and MYC rose more rapidly in CASP5C-expressing organoids. These data are consistent with a role in epithelial recovery programmes in organoid systems, but they do not establish repair efficacy in the human body.
+
+Single-cell and differentiation analyses suggested that CASP5 isoforms occupy distinct epithelial states. In one single-cell analysis, total CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent in LGR5-positive stem cells, higher in transit-amplifying cells and highest in enterocytes. However, isoform-resolved patterns placed CASP5c deeper in crypt regions, where it overlapped more with MKI67 and AXIN2 than did CASP5a or CASP5b. CASP5A and CASP5B were more luminal and associated with differentiated or CK20-positive states. Pseudotime and CIBERSORTx analyses similarly linked CASP5c with early-lineage or transit-amplifying compartments, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b aligned with mature or differentiating states. During differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, while CASP5a and CASP5b rose later with differentiation markers.
+
+Perturbation experiments supported an isoform competition model. Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while retaining CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity, organoid size, ATP content, EdU incorporation and Ki-67 positivity. When CASP5c was also depleted, these effects decreased. Thus, the longer isoforms appear able to restrain CASP5C, at least in part by competing for DVL2-containing complexes.
+
+CASP5C required catalytic activity to activate Wnt signalling. The catalytic mutant CASP5C(C173A) lost reporter activation and beta-catenin effects. CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A and CASP5B, and removal of the CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased activity and conferred stronger Wnt reporter signalling. These results connect the absence of the CARD domain to increased catalytic availability or activity, although the precise structural basis remains to be defined.
+
+Mechanistically, CASP5C disrupted beta-catenin destruction complex organization after Wnt stimulation. CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phospho-beta-catenin, APC and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes and decreased beta-catenin ubiquitination. APC emerged as a relevant substrate. CASP5C generated APC cleavage products, whereas CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C(C173A) did not. Recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. Mutation of APC D556 removed the major cleavage products, indicating that D556 is a primary cleavage site. However, additional APC sites may also contribute. In APC knockout HEK293T cells, wild-type APC restored the ability of CASP5C to disrupt the complex and increase Wnt reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A) weakened this effect. APC fragments resembling cleavage products reduced beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased reporter activity.
+
+Disease datasets provided context but not proof of pathogenesis. CASP5 and CASP4 were increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining their cell-type patterns. CASP5 mutations occurred in approximately 3% of colon or rectal cancers in the reported analysis, similar to AXIN1. CASP5 mutations tended to co-occur with Wnt-pathway mutations but were mutually exclusive with APC mutations. In microsatellite instability-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were reported more often than APC mutations in one comparison, approximately 68% versus 17%. These associations raise questions about CASP5 and Wnt pathway genetics, but they do not show that CASP5C causes inflammatory bowel disease, ulcerative colitis or colorectal cancer, nor do they establish CASP5C as a validated therapeutic target.
+
+## Discussion
+
+These findings define CASP5C as a selective enhancer of Wnt/beta-catenin signalling in human intestinal epithelial models. The activity is isoform specific: CASP5C promotes Wnt reporter activity, beta-catenin redistribution, Wnt target gene expression and organoid proliferation, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B do not. Indeed, the longer isoforms can oppose CASP5C by competing for DVL2, providing a mechanism by which alternative CASP5 products tune epithelial Wnt output.
+
+The work also separates CASP5 from CASP4 functionally. CASP4 more closely resembles mouse caspase 11 in sequence, phylogeny and tested noncanonical inflammasome activity. CASP5, and especially CASP5C, instead shows an epithelial Wnt-regulatory function in these systems. This distinction helps explain why CASP5 conservation does not necessarily imply redundancy with CASP4.
+
+The proposed mechanism connects CASP5C catalytic activity to APC cleavage. By cleaving APC, CASP5C weakens assembly or function of the beta-catenin destruction complex, reduces beta-catenin ubiquitination and increases downstream transcription. D556 is an important APC cleavage site, but the evidence leaves room for additional sites and for other substrate contributions. The interaction with DVL-containing complexes may localize CASP5C to the relevant signalling machinery and provide specificity.
+
+Several limitations temper the interpretation. Much of the functional evidence comes from organoids and engineered cell systems. These models are powerful for epithelial mechanisms but do not establish organism-level repair, inflammation or cancer outcomes. The physical interaction data support close proximity and co-complex formation, but direct binding remains incompletely proven. Disease associations in ulcerative colitis and colorectal cancer are hypothesis-generating rather than causal.
+
+Overall, the data reveal an unexpected link between an inflammatory caspase-family isoform and epithelial Wnt control. CASP5C appears to amplify Wnt signalling during proliferative or recovery-associated states, while CASP5A and CASP5B are more aligned with differentiated compartments and can restrain CASP5C activity. This isoform-resolved view provides a framework for studying how intestinal epithelial cells integrate inflammatory cues with renewal programmes.

+## Introduction
+
+Human inflammatory caspases participate in host defence and inflammatory signalling, but individual family members have not been functionally resolved to the same extent. CASP4 is closely related to mouse caspase 11 and acts as a principal human mediator of noncanonical inflammasome responses to cytosolic lipopolysaccharide and intracellular bacteria in the tested epithelial and macrophage systems. By contrast, CASP5 did not carry this dominant function in these assays, raising the possibility that CASP5 has diverged toward a distinct role.
+
+The intestine is a plausible setting for such specialization. CASP5 expression is concentrated in the intestinal tract and appendix and is detected in epithelial cells as well as selected monocyte and macrophage populations. In human colonic organoids, CASP5 expression exceeded that of CASP4, CASP1 and PYCARD when compared with macrophages, whereas CASP4 showed broader expression. These patterns suggested that CASP5 might have a cell-state-specific epithelial function rather than serving only as a duplicate of CASP4.
+
+Intestinal epithelial renewal depends on Wnt/beta-catenin signalling. Wnt activity maintains stem and proliferative compartments, supports repair, and regulates the balance between renewal and differentiation. Dishevelled proteins connect receptor-proximal Wnt events to downstream beta-catenin control, whereas APC is a scaffold and tumour suppressor within the beta-catenin destruction complex. Because inappropriate Wnt signalling is closely linked to colorectal tumorigenesis, any new modulator of this pathway requires careful functional and mechanistic definition.
+
+Here we investigate whether CASP5 isoforms contribute to intestinal epithelial signalling. The data identify CASP5C, a CARD-less isoform, as a selective enhancer of Wnt/beta-catenin activity. CASP5C acts through its catalytic domain, associates with DVL-containing complexes, and cleaves APC to weaken beta-catenin destruction complex assembly. CASP5A and CASP5B do not share this activity and instead can restrain CASP5C, supporting an isoform competition model in which CASP5 products exert opposing effects on epithelial Wnt tone.
+
+## Methods
+
+Human intestinal expression patterns were assessed across tissue, organoid and single-cell datasets described in the source studies. Human colonic and small-intestinal organoids were used to examine CASP5 isoform expression and function. CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C corresponded approximately to 49.7 kDa, 43.2 kDa and 33.3 kDa proteins. CASP5B lacked part of the CARD region, whereas CASP5C lacked the CARD domain. Small interfering RNA experiments distinguished CASP5C from a cleavage product of CASP5A or CASP5B.
+
+Inducible expression was examined after Salmonella exposure and interferon stimulation. Lipopolysaccharide alone was evaluated in epithelial contexts, where limited response was consistent with low TLR4 expression. CASP5-interacting proteins were identified by BioID2 proximity labelling in human colonic organoids and by co-immunoprecipitation of CASP5 isoforms with DVL proteins. DVL2-containing complexes were further assessed for AXIN1 and beta-catenin association.
+
+Wnt signalling was measured with TOPFlash reporter assays in HEK293T, HCT116 and intestinal organoid systems, with and without WNT3A stimulation. CASP5 isoforms were compared with CASP1 and CASP4. Wnt target gene expression, beta-catenin localization, organoid viability, organoid size, Ki-67 staining, EdU incorporation, ATP content and differentiation markers were used as functional readouts. Catalytic dependence was tested with CASP5C(C173A). CARD deletion and mouse caspase 11 constructs were used to evaluate structural and species-related specificity.
+
+Mechanistic experiments examined AXIN1-containing destruction complexes, beta-catenin ubiquitination and APC cleavage. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites in APC were tested using cellular expression, recombinant CASP5C cleavage of truncated APC in vitro, APC knockout HEK293T rescue, APC(D556A), and APC fragments resembling cleavage products. Disease-associated observations from ulcerative colitis and colorectal cancer datasets were treated as associations, not causal evidence.
+
+## Results
+
+CASP5 showed a distinctive intestinal epithelial profile. CASP5 expression was enriched in intestinal tract and appendix, and human intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C. The three isoforms differed structurally: CASP5A retained the canonical architecture, CASP5B lacked part of the CARD, and CASP5C lacked the CARD entirely. CASP5C was not explained by proteolytic processing of longer isoforms, because isoform-targeted knockdown experiments supported its identity as a transcript-derived product.
+
+CASP5 expression responded to microbial and cytokine cues in organoids. Salmonella, interferon-beta and interferon-gamma increased CASP5, whereas lipopolysaccharide alone produced little induction in epithelial cells, consistent with limited epithelial TLR4 responsiveness. These observations placed CASP5 at the intersection of epithelial state and inflammatory stimulation, but did not support assigning CASP5 the same primary cytosolic lipopolysaccharide-sensing role as CASP4.
+
+BioID2 proximity labelling in human colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3 near CASP5, and Wnt-related terms were enriched among candidate interactors. Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed association of CASP5 isoforms with DVL proteins. DVL2 complexes also contained AXIN1 and beta-catenin, placing CASP5 near the machinery that regulates beta-catenin stability. The interaction involved the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain. Although these data strongly support close physical association, they do not by themselves prove a direct binary interaction.
+
+Functional testing separated CASP5C from the other isoforms. CASP5C increased TOPFlash reporter activity in HEK293T and HCT116 cells, both with and without WNT3A. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 and CASP4 did not produce the same response. CASP5C also increased AXIN2 and TCF7 expression and redistributed beta-catenin into cytosolic and nuclear pools. In contrast, CASP5A and CASP5B could compete with CASP5C for DVL2 binding and reduce CASP5C-driven Wnt activation. CARD-less mouse caspase 11 constructs did not reproduce the Wnt effect, suggesting that this activity may reflect a primate CASP5 specialization after gene duplication, although this evolutionary interpretation remains provisional.
+
+In human intestinal organoids, CASP5C again selectively enhanced Wnt activity. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter signalling. CASP5C-expressing colonic organoids contained more viable cells, were approximately 40% larger, and showed increased Ki-67 and EdU positivity. Differentiated enterocyte features were reduced, including fewer CK20-positive cells. At the transcript level, CASP5C increased AXIN2 by about threefold, TCF7 by 1.4-fold, MYC by 1.3-fold, LGR5 by 1.2-fold and MKI67 by 2.4-fold. Differentiation-associated ALPI and MUC2 decreased by approximately 57% and 53%, respectively.
+
+CASP5C also altered organoid behaviour during Wnt limitation and recovery. CASP5C-expressing organoids persisted longer after Wnt withdrawal and re-entered the cell cycle more readily when Wnt was restored. After irradiation of human colonic organoids, CASP5c transcript increased 42.4-fold within 24 hours, compared with 5.6-fold for CASP5a and 6.6-fold for CASP5b. Following this induction, LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7 and MYC rose more rapidly in CASP5C-expressing organoids. These data are consistent with a role in epithelial recovery programmes in organoid systems, but they do not establish repair efficacy in the human body.
+
+Single-cell and differentiation analyses suggested that CASP5 isoforms occupy distinct epithelial states. In one single-cell analysis, total CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent in LGR5-positive stem cells, higher in transit-amplifying cells and highest in enterocytes. However, isoform-resolved patterns placed CASP5c deeper in crypt regions, where it overlapped more with MKI67 and AXIN2 than did CASP5a or CASP5b. CASP5A and CASP5B were more luminal and associated with differentiated or CK20-positive states. Pseudotime and CIBERSORTx analyses similarly linked CASP5c with early-lineage or transit-amplifying compartments, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b aligned with mature or differentiating states. During differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, while CASP5a and CASP5b rose later with differentiation markers.
+
+Perturbation experiments supported an isoform competition model. Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while retaining CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity, organoid size, ATP content, EdU incorporation and Ki-67 positivity. When CASP5c was also depleted, these effects decreased. Thus, the longer isoforms appear able to restrain CASP5C, at least in part by competing for DVL2-containing complexes.
+
+CASP5C required catalytic activity to activate Wnt signalling. The catalytic mutant CASP5C(C173A) lost reporter activation and beta-catenin effects. CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A and CASP5B, and removal of the CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased activity and conferred stronger Wnt reporter signalling. These results connect the absence of the CARD domain to increased catalytic availability or activity, although the precise structural basis remains to be defined.
+
+Mechanistically, CASP5C disrupted beta-catenin destruction complex organization after Wnt stimulation. CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phospho-beta-catenin, APC and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes and decreased beta-catenin ubiquitination. APC emerged as a relevant substrate. CASP5C generated APC cleavage products, whereas CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C(C173A) did not. Recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. Mutation of APC D556 removed the major cleavage products, indicating that D556 is a primary cleavage site. However, additional APC sites may also contribute. In APC knockout HEK293T cells, wild-type APC restored the ability of CASP5C to disrupt the complex and increase Wnt reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A) weakened this effect. APC fragments resembling cleavage products reduced beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased reporter activity.
+
+Disease datasets provided context but not proof of pathogenesis. CASP5 and CASP4 were increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining their cell-type patterns. CASP5 mutations occurred in approximately 3% of colon or rectal cancers in the reported analysis, similar to AXIN1. CASP5 mutations tended to co-occur with Wnt-pathway mutations but were mutually exclusive with APC mutations. In microsatellite instability-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were reported more often than APC mutations in one comparison, approximately 68% versus 17%. These associations raise questions about CASP5 and Wnt pathway genetics, but they do not show that CASP5C causes inflammatory bowel disease, ulcerative colitis or colorectal cancer, nor do they establish CASP5C as a validated therapeutic target.
+
+## Discussion
+
+These findings define CASP5C as a selective enhancer of Wnt/beta-catenin signalling in human intestinal epithelial models. The activity is isoform specific: CASP5C promotes Wnt reporter activity, beta-catenin redistribution, Wnt target gene expression and organoid proliferation, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B do not. Indeed, the longer isoforms can oppose CASP5C by competing for DVL2, providing a mechanism by which alternative CASP5 products tune epithelial Wnt output.
+
+The work also separates CASP5 from CASP4 functionally. CASP4 more closely resembles mouse caspase 11 in sequence, phylogeny and tested noncanonical inflammasome activity. CASP5, and especially CASP5C, instead shows an epithelial Wnt-regulatory function in these systems. This distinction helps explain why CASP5 conservation does not necessarily imply redundancy with CASP4.
+
+The proposed mechanism connects CASP5C catalytic activity to APC cleavage. By cleaving APC, CASP5C weakens assembly or function of the beta-catenin destruction complex, reduces beta-catenin ubiquitination and increases downstream transcription. D556 is an important APC cleavage site, but the evidence leaves room for additional sites and for other substrate contributions. The interaction with DVL-containing complexes may localize CASP5C to the relevant signalling machinery and provide specificity.
+
+Several limitations temper the interpretation. Much of the functional evidence comes from organoids and engineered cell systems. These models are powerful for epithelial mechanisms but do not establish organism-level repair, inflammation or cancer outcomes. The physical interaction data support close proximity and co-complex formation, but direct binding remains incompletely proven. Disease associations in ulcerative colitis and colorectal cancer are hypothesis-generating rather than causal.
+
+Overall, the data reveal an unexpected link between an inflammatory caspase-family isoform and epithelial Wnt control. CASP5C appears to amplify Wnt signalling during proliferative or recovery-associated states, while CASP5A and CASP5B are more aligned with differentiated compartments and can restrain CASP5C activity. This isoform-resolved view provides a framework for studying how intestinal epithelial cells integrate inflammatory cues with renewal programmes.

+# CASP5C amplifies epithelial Wnt signalling through APC cleavage in human intestinal organoids
+
+## Abstract
+
+Inflammatory caspases are best known as mediators of innate immune responses, yet the functions of human CASP5 have remained less defined than those of CASP4 and mouse caspase 11. Here we describe a noncanonical role for a CARD-less CASP5 isoform, CASP5C, in human intestinal epithelial Wnt signalling. Human intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C, with CASP5 enriched in intestinal epithelial contexts and inducible by infection-associated and interferon signals. Proximity labelling and co-immunoprecipitation placed CASP5 isoforms near dishevelled proteins DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3. Functional assays showed that CASP5C, but not CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 or CASP4, increased Wnt reporter activity, promoted beta-catenin redistribution, and induced Wnt target genes. In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased organoid size, viability and proliferation while reducing differentiation markers. Mechanistically, CASP5C required catalytic activity and cleaved APC, with D556 contributing to major cleavage products but not excluding additional sites. CASP5A and CASP5B competed with CASP5C for DVL2 and restrained its effect. These findings identify CASP5C as an epithelial amplifier of Wnt signalling and suggest that alternative CASP5 isoforms partition inflammatory caspase family functions in the human intestine.
+
+## Introduction
+
+Human inflammatory caspases participate in host defence and inflammatory signalling, but individual family members have not been functionally resolved to the same extent. CASP4 is closely related to mouse caspase 11 and acts as a principal human mediator of noncanonical inflammasome responses to cytosolic lipopolysaccharide and intracellular bacteria in the tested epithelial and macrophage systems. By contrast, CASP5 did not carry this dominant function in these assays, raising the possibility that CASP5 has diverged toward a distinct role.
+
+The intestine is a plausible setting for such specialization. CASP5 expression is concentrated in the intestinal tract and appendix and is detected in epithelial cells as well as selected monocyte and macrophage populations. In human colonic organoids, CASP5 expression exceeded that of CASP4, CASP1 and PYCARD when compared with macrophages, whereas CASP4 showed broader expression. These patterns suggested that CASP5 might have a cell-state-specific epithelial function rather than serving only as a duplicate of CASP4.
+
+Intestinal epithelial renewal depends on Wnt/beta-catenin signalling. Wnt activity maintains stem and proliferative compartments, supports repair, and regulates the balance between renewal and differentiation. Dishevelled proteins connect receptor-proximal Wnt events to downstream beta-catenin control, whereas APC is a scaffold and tumour suppressor within the beta-catenin destruction complex.
+
+Here we investigate whether CASP5 isoforms contribute to intestinal epithelial signalling. The data identify CASP5C, a CARD-less isoform, as a selective enhancer of Wnt/beta-catenin activity. CASP5C acts through its catalytic domain, associates with DVL-containing complexes, and cleaves APC to weaken beta-catenin destruction complex assembly. CASP5A and CASP5B instead can restrain CASP5C, supporting an isoform competition model.
+
+## Methods
+
+Human intestinal expression patterns were assessed across tissue, organoid and single-cell datasets. Human colonic and small-intestinal organoids were used to examine CASP5 isoform expression and function. CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C corresponded approximately to 49.7 kDa, 43.2 kDa and 33.3 kDa proteins. CASP5B lacked part of the CARD region, whereas CASP5C lacked the CARD domain. Small interfering RNA experiments distinguished CASP5C from a cleavage product of CASP5A or CASP5B.
+
+Inducible expression was examined after Salmonella exposure and interferon stimulation. CASP5-interacting proteins were identified by BioID2 proximity labelling in human colonic organoids and by co-immunoprecipitation of CASP5 isoforms with DVL proteins. DVL2-containing complexes were further assessed for AXIN1 and beta-catenin association.
+
+Wnt signalling was measured with TOPFlash reporter assays in HEK293T, HCT116 and intestinal organoid systems, with and without WNT3A. CASP5 isoforms were compared with CASP1 and CASP4. Wnt target gene expression, beta-catenin localization, organoid viability, organoid size, Ki-67 staining, EdU incorporation, ATP content and differentiation markers were used as readouts. Catalytic dependence was tested with CASP5C(C173A). CARD deletion and mouse caspase 11 constructs evaluated structural and species-related specificity.
+
+Mechanistic experiments examined AXIN1-containing destruction complexes, beta-catenin ubiquitination and APC cleavage. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites in APC were tested using recombinant CASP5C cleavage of truncated APC in vitro, APC knockout HEK293T rescue, APC(D556A), and APC fragments resembling cleavage products. Disease-associated observations from ulcerative colitis and colorectal cancer datasets were treated as associations, not causal evidence.
