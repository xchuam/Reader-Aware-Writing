# A CARD-less CASP5 isoform amplifies epithelial Wnt signaling through APC cleavage

## Abstract

Human CASP5 resembles CASP4, the principal human inflammatory caspase for noncanonical inflammasome responses, but its biological role has remained unclear. We investigated whether CASP5 has a distinct function in intestinal epithelial cells, where CASP5 expression is enriched and Wnt/beta-catenin signaling controls renewal. Comparative epithelial and macrophage assays showed that bacterial noncanonical inflammasome activation and clearance depended on CASP4 rather than CASP5. Human intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B, and a CARD-less isoform, CASP5C. Proximity labeling and co-immunoprecipitation linked CASP5 isoforms to dishevelled proteins, or DVL proteins, which connect Wnt receptor activation to downstream beta-catenin regulation. Functional assays showed that CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased beta-catenin/TCF reporter activity, nuclear beta-catenin, Wnt-responsive gene expression, organoid growth, proliferation, and repair-associated responses after irradiation. CASP5C required catalytic activity, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination, disrupted AXIN1-containing destruction complexes, and cleaved APC at D556. CASP5A and CASP5B competed with CASP5C for DVL binding and restrained this activity. These data support a model in which CASP5C locally amplifies Wnt signaling in transit-amplifying intestinal epithelial cells.

## Introduction

Inflammatory caspases link innate immune sensing to inflammatory cell death and antimicrobial defense. Human CASP4 and CASP5 are orthologues of mouse caspase 11 and can bind lipopolysaccharide through caspase activation and recruitment domains, or CARDs. CASP4, however, is closer to mouse caspase 11 and retains the main human noncanonical inflammasome role after cytosolic lipopolysaccharide detection. CASP5 arose after primate-specific gene duplication, leaving open whether it simply overlaps with CASP4 or acquired a different function.

The intestine offers a plausible setting for such a function. CASP5 transcripts are concentrated mainly in intestinal tract and appendix samples and, at single-cell resolution, appear largely in intestinal epithelial cells and monocytes/macrophages. Intestinal epithelial renewal depends on Wnt/beta-catenin signaling. At the crypt base, Wnt maintains stem and progenitor states; farther from this source, transit-amplifying cells, the proliferative progeny between stem cells and differentiated enterocytes, must sustain enough Wnt activity to expand before differentiation.

Wnt output is controlled in part by beta-catenin stability. In low-Wnt conditions, the beta-catenin destruction complex, a multiprotein assembly including AXIN1 and APC, promotes beta-catenin phosphorylation, ubiquitination, and degradation. APC is a scaffold protein in this complex and a tumor suppressor frequently altered in colorectal cancer. Dishevelled proteins, abbreviated DVL, are Wnt signaling hubs that link receptor activation to downstream beta-catenin regulation. We asked whether CASP5 has a non-inflammasome role in this epithelial pathway, which isoform mediates it, and how the mechanism connects to intestinal renewal.

## Methods

The study integrated computational, cellular, biochemical, organoid, tissue, and public-dataset analyses. Sequence and phylogenetic comparisons evaluated human CASP4, human CASP5, and mouse caspase 11. Human Protein Atlas and single-cell datasets defined tissue and cell-type expression. Human colonic and small intestinal organoids modeled epithelial CASP5 isoform expression and function, while monocyte-derived macrophages provided a comparator for inflammatory caspase activity.

Functional assays used organoid-derived epithelial cells, macrophages, HEK293T, HCT116, MODE-K, APC-knockout HEK293T cells, and primary human organoids. TOPFlash measured beta-catenin/TCF-dependent Wnt transcriptional activity, with FOPFlash as a mutated binding-site control. Beta-catenin localization, Wnt-responsive transcripts, beta-catenin ubiquitination, organoid viability, size, Ki-67, EdU incorporation, ATP concentration, and differentiation markers measured pathway and epithelial outcomes.

CASP5-associated proteins were identified by BioID2 proximity labeling and mass spectrometry, followed by co-immunoprecipitation, domain mapping, and immunoblotting. Catalytically inactive CASP5C(C173A), CARD-deleted CASP5 constructs, APC D556A mutation, APC fragments, recombinant CASP5C, and APC-knockout rescue assays tested enzymatic mechanism. Irradiated colonic organoids modeled epithelial injury and recovery. Human intestinal tissue, ulcerative colitis datasets, colorectal cancer mutation and methylation datasets, and mouse intestinal material assessed localization and disease-associated patterns.

## Results

CASP4, not CASP5, mediated the tested noncanonical inflammasome responses. Although CASP4 and CASP5 share sequence similarity, CASP4 was more similar to mouse caspase 11. In colonic organoid-derived epithelial cells, noncanonical inflammasome activation and clearance of intracellular Salmonella Typhimurium and Shigella flexneri depended on CASP4 rather than CASP5. Monocyte-derived macrophages showed the same CASP4 dependence during phagocytosis of virulent and avirulent live bacteria. These findings motivated the search for a CASP5 function outside the tested inflammasome pathway.

Expression and isoform analyses placed CASP5 in the intestinal epithelium. CASP4 transcripts were broadly distributed, whereas CASP5 transcripts were concentrated in intestinal tissues and limited mainly to epithelial cells and monocytes/macrophages. Healthy colonic epithelial fractions and colonic organoids expressed more CASP5 than monocyte-derived macrophages, while CASP4, CASP1, and PYCARD showed the reverse pattern. Human colonic and small intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B, and CASP5C. CASP5A was about 49.7 kDa, CASP5B about 43.2 kDa, and CASP5C about 33.3 kDa. CASP5B lacks part of the CASP5A CARD region, whereas CASP5C lacks the entire CARD. siRNA experiments supported CASP5C as a distinct isoform rather than a cleavage product.

CASP5 isoforms associated with Wnt signaling machinery. BioID2 proximity labeling in human colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2, and DVL3 among leading CASP5-associated proteins, and enriched CASP5 interactors included Wnt-related processes. Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed endogenous CASP5 interactions with DVL proteins and detected AXIN1 and beta-catenin in CASP5-DVL2 complexes. Domain mapping implicated the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain, although the experiments did not establish whether binding was direct.

CASP5C was the Wnt-promoting isoform. In HEK293T and HCT116 cells lacking endogenous CASP5, CASP5C increased TOPFlash activity with or without WNT3A, while CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1, and CASP4 did not. CASP5C also increased AXIN2 and TCF7 expression and promoted nuclear beta-catenin accumulation. Co-expression of CASP5A or CASP5B reduced CASP5C-mediated reporter activity and competed with CASP5C for DVL2 interaction. Tested CARD-less mouse caspase 11 isoforms did not regulate Wnt in MODE-K or HEK293T cells, suggesting specialization after primate CASP5 duplication.

Primary organoids connected CASP5C to epithelial growth. In human colonic and small intestinal organoids, CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity, cytosolic and nuclear beta-catenin, and Wnt-responsive and stem/proliferation-associated markers. In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased AXIN2 about 3-fold, TCF7 about 1.4-fold, MYC about 1.3-fold, LGR5 about 1.2-fold, and MKI67 about 2.4-fold, while reducing ALPI and MUC2 by about 57% and 53%. CASP5C-expressing organoids had more viable cells, were about 40% larger, contained more Ki-67-positive and EdU-positive cells, and had fewer CK20-positive enterocytes. CASP5C also supported prolonged Wnt-independent growth and allowed cell-cycle re-entry after Wnt restoration. After irradiation, CASP5c transcript rose about 42.4-fold within 24 hours, compared with 5.6-fold for CASP5a and 6.6-fold for CASP5b, and CASP5C-expressing organoids rapidly increased LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7, and MYC.

Mechanistic assays showed that CASP5C acts through catalytic disruption of the beta-catenin destruction complex. After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phosphorylated beta-catenin, APC, and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes and reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. CASP5C(C173A) lost the ability to promote Wnt reporter activity, beta-catenin relocalization, and Wnt-responsive gene expression. CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and deleting the CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased enzymatic activity and Wnt reporter signaling. Thus, the absence of the inhibitory CARD enables CASP5C-dependent Wnt amplification.

APC cleavage linked CASP5C protease activity to pathway output. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites in APC clustered mainly in the N-terminal region. CASP5C, but not CASP5A, CASP5B, or CASP5C(C173A), generated APC cleavage products in cells, and recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. Mutation of APC D556 to alanine abolished the major CASP5C-dependent cleavage products. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, reintroduced wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the destruction complex and enhance Wnt reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A) reduced this effect. APC fragments mimicking cleavage products decreased beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased Wnt reporter activity, although additional CASP5C cleavage sites may contribute.

Localization and knockdown experiments supported a transit-amplifying-cell model. In healthy human colon single-cell data, CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent in LGR5-positive stem cells, increased in transit-amplifying cells, and were most enriched in enterocytes; CASP4 was more uniform. In situ hybridization showed CASP5a and CASP5b more luminally, whereas CASP5c localized deeper in crypts and overlapped strongly with MKI67-positive and AXIN2-positive zones. Immunohistochemistry, CIBERSORTx, and pseudotime analyses similarly associated CASP5C with proliferative crypt states and CASP5A/B with mature or differentiating states. During organoid differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, while CASP5a and CASP5b increased later. Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C enhanced Wnt reporter activity, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination by about 50%, increased organoid size by about 60%, and increased EdU-positive and Ki-67-positive cells; concurrent CASP5c knockdown returned these effects toward baseline.

## Discussion

The evidence identifies CASP5C as an epithelial Wnt amplifier rather than a duplicate of CASP4 in the tested noncanonical inflammasome settings. The study first separated CASP5 from CASP4-dependent bacterial inflammasome activation, then connected epithelial CASP5C to DVL proteins, APC cleavage, beta-catenin stabilization, and organoid proliferation. CASP5C does not replace canonical Wnt control. Instead, it modulates pathway strength by acting near the destruction complex.

The isoform logic explains how CASP5 activity may be tuned across the crypt. CASP5C lacks the CARD region, displays higher enzymatic activity, binds DVL through its catalytic domain, and cleaves APC at D556. CASP5A and CASP5B retain all or part of the CARD, bind DVL, but do not promote Wnt to the same extent. Their ability to compete with CASP5C for DVL2, together with their later expression in differentiating compartments, supports a model in which isoform balance helps turn down CASP5C-dependent Wnt amplification as cells mature.

Several limits constrain interpretation. The work is mechanistically strong across cell-line, organoid, biochemical, tissue, and dataset analyses, but many functional experiments rely on overexpression, knockdown, and organoid systems. Human tissue and public datasets support localization and association, not direct in vivo causality in human intestine. Mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce CASP5C Wnt activity in the tested systems, limiting mouse-to-human inference. APC D556 is strongly supported as a primary cleavage site, but other cleavage sites may also contribute.

Disease-associated findings remain hypothesis-generating. CASP5 and CASP4 expression increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining cell-type distributions. CASP5 mutations appeared in about 3% of colon or rectal adenocarcinomas, tended to co-occur with other Wnt pathway mutations, and were mutually exclusive with APC mutations in the reported analysis. In microsatellite instability-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were more common than APC mutations in the cited dataset comparison. These observations suggest disease-relevant questions, but they do not show that CASP5C causes inflammatory bowel disease or colorectal cancer, and they do not validate CASP5C as a therapeutic target.

## Conclusion

CASP5C defines a non-inflammasome role for human CASP5 in intestinal epithelial biology. By lacking the CARD region, retaining high catalytic activity, binding DVL proteins, and cleaving APC at D556, CASP5C weakens beta-catenin destruction and amplifies Wnt-dependent transcription. This activity supports organoid growth and proliferation and is enriched in transit-amplifying cells, where local Wnt amplification may help sustain epithelial renewal and repair. CASP5A and CASP5B restrain this activity by competing for DVL binding, suggesting that CASP5 isoform balance tunes Wnt output across epithelial differentiation.
