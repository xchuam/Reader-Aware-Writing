# Topic

CASP5C, a CARD-less isoform of human caspase 5, promotes Wnt signaling in intestinal epithelial transit-amplifying cells by binding dishevelled and cleaving APC, thereby supporting epithelial homeostasis and repair.

# Target Reader

The intended reader is a broadly trained life-science researcher, such as a cell biologist, immunologist, gastroenterology researcher, or senior graduate student. The reader probably knows that Wnt/beta-catenin signaling controls intestinal epithelial renewal, and that inflammatory caspases mediate innate immune responses, but may not know why CASP5 isoforms or transit-amplifying cells matter.

The article should help the reader understand:

- why CASP5 function was unclear despite its similarity to CASP4;
- why intestinal epithelial cells are the relevant biological setting;
- how CASP5C differs from CASP5A and CASP5B;
- how dishevelled, APC, beta-catenin, and the destruction complex connect mechanistically;
- how the organoid, cell-line, tissue, and dataset evidence fit into one model.

# Article Goal

Draft a concise research article that reports the study as a coherent mechanistic investigation. The article should move from the biological puzzle to the experimental strategy, then to the major findings and their implications for intestinal epithelial renewal.

Do not mention that the article was drafted from a dossier or extracted from a Nature paper. Do not reuse the source paper title verbatim.

# Background Facts

- CASP5 is an inflammatory caspase-family cysteine protease related to inflammation and cell death.
- Human CASP4 and CASP5 are orthologues of mouse caspase 11, but CASP4 is closer to mouse caspase 11 and is the main human mediator of noncanonical inflammasome activation after cytosolic lipopolysaccharide detection.
- CASP5 shares high sequence similarity with CASP4, but its biological function has remained unclear.
- CASP5 arose after primate-specific gene duplication, whereas CASP4 retained the ancestral noncanonical inflammasome role.
- Wnt/beta-catenin signaling is required for intestinal epithelial development, regeneration, and the balance between stem-cell renewal and differentiation.
- Dishevelled proteins link Wnt receptors to downstream signaling components, including the beta-catenin destruction complex.
- APC is a central scaffold protein in the beta-catenin destruction complex and a tumor suppressor frequently altered in colorectal cancer.
- Transit-amplifying cells are proliferative intestinal epithelial progeny that depend on Wnt signaling as they move away from the Wnt-rich crypt base.

# Research Question

Does CASP5 have a non-inflammasome function in human intestinal epithelial cells, and if so, which isoform mediates that function and by what mechanism?

# Central Claim

CASP5C is a human intestinal epithelial amplifier of Wnt signaling. It binds dishevelled proteins through the CASP5 catalytic domain, cleaves APC at D556, disrupts the beta-catenin destruction complex, reduces beta-catenin ubiquitination, and supports proliferation of transit-amplifying cells. CASP5A and CASP5B bind dishevelled but restrain CASP5C activity by competition rather than amplifying Wnt.

# Study Design / Evidence Base

The study combined computational, biochemical, cellular, organoid, tissue, and public-dataset analyses.

- Sequence and phylogenetic analyses compared human CASP4, human CASP5, and mouse caspase 11.
- Human Protein Atlas and single-cell expression datasets were used to compare CASP4 and CASP5 tissue and cell-type expression.
- Human colonic and small intestinal organoids were used to model intestinal epithelial CASP5 expression and function.
- Human monocyte-derived macrophages were used as a comparator for inflammatory caspase expression and bacterial inflammasome responses.
- HEK293T, HCT116, MODE-K, APC-knockout HEK293T cells, and human organoids were used for reporter assays, overexpression, knockdown, cleavage assays, and mechanistic rescue tests.
- BioID2 proximity labeling, mass spectrometry, co-immunoprecipitation, domain mapping, and immunoblotting tested CASP5 binding partners and complex composition.
- TOPFlash/FOPFlash Wnt reporter assays, beta-catenin localization, Wnt-responsive gene expression, beta-catenin ubiquitination assays, and organoid growth/proliferation assays tested Wnt pathway function.
- Catalytically inactive CASP5C(C173A), CARD-deleted CASP5 constructs, APC D556A mutation, APC fragments, and recombinant CASP5C were used to test enzymatic mechanism.
- Irradiated human colonic organoids were used as an epithelial injury and recovery model.
- Human intestinal tissue, ulcerative colitis datasets, colorectal cancer mutation/methylation datasets, and mouse intestinal material were used to evaluate cell-type localization and disease associations.

# Main Findings

## 1. CASP4, not CASP5, mediates the tested noncanonical inflammasome responses.

- CASP4 and CASP5 share sequence similarity and both bind LPS through CARD regions, but CASP4 is more similar to mouse caspase 11.
- In human intestinal epithelial cells derived from colonic organoids, noncanonical inflammasome activation and clearance of intracellular Salmonella Typhimurium and Shigella flexneri depended on CASP4, not CASP5.
- In monocyte-derived macrophages, phagocytosis of virulent and avirulent live bacteria activated the noncanonical inflammasome and bacterial clearance, again depending on CASP4 rather than CASP5.
- These results motivated the search for a different CASP5 function.

## 2. CASP5 is concentrated in the human intestinal epithelium and exists as three isoforms.

- CASP4 transcripts were broadly distributed across tissues and cell types.
- CASP5 transcripts were concentrated mainly in the intestinal tract and appendix, and at single-cell level were limited largely to intestinal epithelial cells and monocytes/macrophages.
- CASP5 transcripts were more abundant in healthy colonic epithelial fractions and colonic organoids than in monocyte-derived macrophages, while CASP4, CASP1, and PYCARD showed the reverse pattern.
- Human colonic and small intestinal organoids expressed three CASP5 isoforms: CASP5A, CASP5B, and CASP5C.
- CASP5A is about 49.7 kDa, CASP5B about 43.2 kDa, and CASP5C about 33.3 kDa.
- CASP5B lacks part of the CASP5A CARD region; CASP5C lacks the entire CARD.
- siRNA experiments supported the conclusion that CASP5C is a distinct isoform rather than a cleavage product of CASP5A or CASP5B.
- In organoids, CASP5 increased after Salmonella Typhimurium, IFN-beta, or IFN-gamma stimulation; LPS alone did not increase CASP5 in epithelial cells, consistent with low TLR4 expression.

## 3. CASP5 isoforms interact with dishevelled proteins.

- BioID2 proximity labeling in human colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2, and DVL3 among the leading CASP5-associated proteins.
- Gene ontology enrichment of CASP5 interactors highlighted Wnt-related biological processes.
- Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed that endogenous CASP5 isoforms interact with DVL1, DVL2, and DVL3 in colonic organoids.
- Wnt components including AXIN1 and beta-catenin were present in CASP5-DVL2 complexes.
- Domain mapping indicated that the interaction involves the dishevelled DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain. The study notes that the interaction may be direct or indirect.

## 4. CASP5C uniquely promotes Wnt signaling.

- In HEK293T and HCT116 cells that lack endogenous CASP5, CASP5C increased TOPFlash Wnt reporter activity, with or without WNT3A.
- CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1, and CASP4 did not produce the same Wnt reporter activation.
- CASP5C increased expression of Wnt-responsive genes such as AXIN2 and TCF7 and promoted nuclear beta-catenin accumulation.
- Co-expression of CASP5A or CASP5B reduced CASP5C-mediated Wnt activity and competed with CASP5C for DVL2 interaction.
- Tested CARD-less mouse caspase 11 isoforms did not regulate Wnt in MODE-K or HEK293T cells, suggesting that CASP5C specialization emerged after primate CASP5 duplication.

## 5. CASP5C promotes organoid growth, proliferation, and injury recovery.

- In primary human colonic and small intestinal organoids, CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity.
- CASP5C caused cytosolic and nuclear beta-catenin accumulation and increased Wnt-responsive and stem/proliferation-associated markers.
- In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased AXIN2 about 3-fold, TCF7 about 1.4-fold, MYC about 1.3-fold, LGR5 about 1.2-fold, and MKI67 about 2.4-fold.
- CASP5C reduced differentiated epithelial markers ALPI and MUC2 by about 57% and 53%, respectively.
- CASP5C-expressing colonic organoids had more viable cells, about 40% larger size, more Ki-67-positive and EdU-positive cells, and fewer CK20-positive enterocytes than controls.
- CASP5C enabled prolonged Wnt-independent growth and allowed organoids to re-enter the cell cycle after Wnt was restored.
- In irradiated colonic organoids, CASP5c transcript increased about 42.4-fold within 24 hours, compared with about 5.6-fold for CASP5a and 6.6-fold for CASP5b.
- After irradiation, CASP5C-expressing organoids showed rapid increases in LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7, and MYC compared with non-CASP5C organoids, consistent with enhanced Wnt-dependent repair.

## 6. CASP5C enzymatic activity disrupts the beta-catenin destruction complex.

- CASP5C reduced levels of DVL2, beta-catenin, phosphorylated beta-catenin, APC, and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes after Wnt stimulation.
- CASP5C reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination.
- The catalytic mutant CASP5C(C173A) lost the ability to promote Wnt reporter activity, beta-catenin localization, and Wnt-responsive gene expression.
- CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and CARD deletion from CASP5A or CASP5B increased activity and Wnt reporter signaling.
- These experiments support the idea that absence of the inhibitory CARD enables CASP5C enzymatic activity.

## 7. CASP5C cleaves APC at D556 to activate Wnt signaling.

- Bioinformatic prediction identified candidate CASP5 cleavage sites in APC, mostly in the N-terminal region.
- In cell experiments, CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, produced APC cleavage products; the catalytically inactive CASP5C(C173A) did not.
- Recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro.
- Mutation of APC D556 to alanine abolished the major CASP5C-mediated cleavage products, establishing D556 as a primary cleavage site.
- In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, reintroduced wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the destruction complex and enhance Wnt reporter activity; APC(D556A) reduced this effect.
- APC fragments mimicking the cleavage products decreased beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased Wnt reporter activity.
- The study allows that other APC cleavage sites may also contribute.

## 8. CASP5C is enriched in transit-amplifying cells.

- In healthy human colon single-cell data, CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent in LGR5-positive stem cells, increased in transit-amplifying cells, and were most enriched in enterocytes.
- CASP4 was more uniformly expressed across epithelial cell types.
- In situ hybridization showed that CASP5a and CASP5b were more luminal, whereas CASP5c localized more deeply in crypts and overlapped strongly with MKI67-positive transit-amplifying zones.
- CASP5c overlapped more with Wnt-responsive AXIN2 than CASP5a or CASP5b.
- Immunohistochemistry supported enrichment of CASP5C with proliferative crypt markers, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B were more associated with differentiated CK20-positive enterocytes.
- CIBERSORTx and pseudotime analyses supported CASP5c association with early-lineage or transit-amplifying cells, while CASP5a and CASP5b associated more with mature or differentiating epithelial states.

## 9. Endogenous CASP5C promotes Wnt signaling in organoids, while CASP5A and CASP5B restrain it.

- During colonic organoid differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, consistent with transit-amplifying enrichment.
- CASP5a and CASP5b increased later with differentiation and mature epithelial markers.
- Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C enhanced Wnt reporter activity in colonic and small intestinal organoids.
- Concurrent CASP5c knockdown restored reporter activity toward baseline.
- Preserving CASP5C while reducing CASP5A and CASP5B reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination by about 50% in colonic organoids.
- Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b increased organoid size by about 60%, increased EdU-positive and Ki-67-positive proliferating cells, and increased ATP concentration; these growth effects were prevented by simultaneous CASP5c knockdown.
- The data support a competitive model: in transit-amplifying cells, high CASP5C preferentially binds DVL2 and amplifies Wnt; in differentiated cells, CASP5A and CASP5B compete for DVL2 and restrain CASP5C.

## 10. Disease-associated findings are suggestive but not definitive.

- CASP5 and CASP4 expression increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining their cell-type distributions.
- Colon or rectal adenocarcinomas carried CASP5 mutations in about 3% of cases, comparable to the proportion carrying AXIN1 mutations in the analyzed context.
- CASP5 mutations tended to co-occur with other Wnt pathway mutations but were mutually exclusive with APC mutations in the reported analysis.
- In microsatellite instability-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were more common than APC mutations in the cited dataset comparison: about 68% versus about 17%.
- The authors frame the cancer relevance as a hypothesis requiring further investigation, not as a demonstrated clinical mechanism or treatment target.

# Interpretation

The study argues that inflammatory caspases can have epithelial homeostatic functions beyond innate immune activation. CASP5C appears to provide a local Wnt-amplifying mechanism in transit-amplifying cells, helping sustain proliferation as cells move away from the Wnt-rich stem-cell zone. CASP5A and CASP5B may help turn down this signal in more differentiated enterocytes by competing with CASP5C for dishevelled binding.

# Limitations and Caveats

- The study is mechanistically strong but relies heavily on cell-line, organoid, overexpression, knockdown, and biochemical assays.
- Human tissue and public dataset analyses support localization and disease association, but they do not by themselves prove in vivo causal function in human intestine.
- Mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce CASP5C Wnt activity in the tested systems, limiting direct mouse-to-human inference.
- Cancer and inflammatory bowel disease observations are associative and hypothesis-generating.
- The APC D556 cleavage site is strongly supported as a primary site, but the study does not rule out additional CASP5C cleavage sites.
- Some numerical results come from specific assay contexts; do not generalize them as universal biological constants.
- Do not claim that CASP5C is a validated therapeutic target.
- Do not claim that CASP5C causes inflammatory bowel disease or colorectal cancer.
- Do not claim that CASP5C replaces canonical Wnt regulation; describe it as an amplifier or modulator.

# A CARD-less CASP5 Isoform Amplifies Intestinal Epithelial Wnt Signaling

## Abstract

Human CASP5 is closely related to CASP4, the principal human mediator of tested noncanonical inflammasome responses, but the specific function of CASP5 has remained unclear. This study identifies CASP5C, a CARD-less CASP5 isoform, as an epithelial amplifier of Wnt/beta-catenin signaling. CASP5C was enriched in human intestinal epithelial transit-amplifying cells, associated with dishevelled proteins, cleaved APC at D556, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination, and promoted Wnt-responsive transcription. CASP5A and CASP5B bound dishevelled but did not activate Wnt signaling; instead, they restrained CASP5C by competition. In human colonic and small intestinal organoids, CASP5C increased beta-catenin accumulation, proliferation, growth, and injury-associated recovery markers. These findings support a model in which primate CASP5 acquired an isoform-specific, non-inflammasome role in epithelial homeostasis and repair.

## Introduction

Inflammatory caspases are cysteine proteases best known for innate immune functions. Human CASP4 and CASP5 are orthologues of mouse caspase 11, but CASP4 is more similar to mouse caspase 11 and retained the ancestral role in noncanonical inflammasome activation after cytosolic lipopolysaccharide detection. CASP5 arose after primate-specific gene duplication, shares high sequence similarity with CASP4, and includes CARD-containing isoforms that can bind lipopolysaccharide. Its distinct biological role, however, has remained unresolved.

The intestinal epithelium offered a plausible setting for a CASP5-specific function because CASP5 transcripts are enriched in intestinal tissue and epithelial fractions. Intestinal renewal depends on Wnt/beta-catenin signaling, which maintains stem-cell and progenitor proliferation while permitting differentiation along the crypt axis. Transit-amplifying cells are proliferative epithelial progenitors located between stem cells and mature enterocytes; they continue to require Wnt input as they move away from the Wnt-rich crypt base.

Wnt signaling is coordinated by dishevelled proteins (DVL1, DVL2, and DVL3), hub proteins that link receptor activation to downstream beta-catenin control. In low-Wnt conditions, the beta-catenin destruction complex, containing AXIN1 and the APC scaffold and tumor suppressor, promotes beta-catenin phosphorylation, ubiquitination, and degradation. Wnt activation stabilizes beta-catenin, allowing nuclear beta-catenin/TCF transcription measured by TOPFlash reporter assays. This study asked whether CASP5 has a non-inflammasome function in human intestinal epithelial cells and, if so, which isoform and mechanism are responsible.

## Methods

The investigation combined computational, biochemical, cellular, organoid, tissue, and public-dataset approaches. Sequence and phylogenetic analyses compared human CASP4, human CASP5, and mouse caspase 11. Human Protein Atlas and single-cell expression datasets were used to map CASP4 and CASP5 distribution across tissues and epithelial cell states.

Human colonic and small intestinal organoids modeled epithelial CASP5 expression and function. Human monocyte-derived macrophages served as inflammatory comparators. Noncanonical inflammasome activation and bacterial clearance were tested in organoid-derived epithelial cells and macrophages after exposure to Salmonella Typhimurium, Shigella flexneri, or live virulent and avirulent bacteria.

Mechanistic assays used HEK293T, HCT116, MODE-K, APC-knockout HEK293T cells, and human organoids. Wnt output was measured using TOPFlash/FOPFlash reporters, beta-catenin localization, Wnt-responsive gene expression, beta-catenin ubiquitination, and organoid growth and proliferation assays. BioID2 proximity labeling, mass spectrometry, co-immunoprecipitation, domain mapping, and immunoblotting tested CASP5-associated proteins and destruction-complex composition. CASP5C(C173A), CARD-deleted CASP5 constructs, APC(D556A), APC fragments, and recombinant CASP5C tested catalytic requirements and APC cleavage. Irradiated colonic organoids modeled epithelial injury and recovery. Human intestinal tissue, ulcerative colitis datasets, colorectal cancer datasets, CIBERSORTx, and pseudotime analyses evaluated localization and disease-associated patterns.

## Results

In human intestinal epithelial cells derived from colonic organoids, noncanonical inflammasome activation and clearance of intracellular Salmonella Typhimurium and Shigella flexneri depended on CASP4. Human monocyte-derived macrophages showed the same CASP4 dependence during bacterial phagocytosis and clearance. These data argued against simple redundancy between CASP4 and CASP5 and prompted analysis of epithelial CASP5 function.

CASP5 was concentrated in the human intestinal epithelium and expressed as three isoforms. CASP4 transcripts were broadly distributed, whereas CASP5 transcripts were mainly enriched in intestinal tract and appendix and, at single-cell resolution, largely in intestinal epithelial cells and monocytes/macrophages. CASP5 transcripts were more abundant in healthy colonic epithelial fractions and colonic organoids than in monocyte-derived macrophages, while CASP4, CASP1, and PYCARD showed the opposite pattern. Human intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B, and CASP5C. CASP5A is approximately 49.7 kDa, CASP5B approximately 43.2 kDa, and CASP5C approximately 33.3 kDa. CASP5B lacks part of the CARD, the caspase activation and recruitment domain, whereas CASP5C lacks the entire CARD. siRNA experiments supported CASP5C as a distinct isoform rather than a cleavage product.

CASP5-associated proteins pointed to Wnt signaling. BioID2 proximity labeling in human colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2, and DVL3 among leading CASP5-associated proteins, and gene ontology analysis enriched Wnt-related processes. Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed endogenous CASP5 interactions with all three dishevelled proteins. AXIN1 and beta-catenin were present in CASP5-DVL2 complexes. Domain mapping implicated the dishevelled DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain, although the interaction may be direct or indirect.

CASP5C uniquely promoted Wnt signaling. In HEK293T and HCT116 cells lacking endogenous CASP5, CASP5C increased TOPFlash activity with or without WNT3A, increased AXIN2 and TCF7 expression, and promoted nuclear beta-catenin accumulation. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1, and CASP4 did not produce comparable activation. Co-expression of CASP5A or CASP5B reduced CASP5C-mediated reporter activity and competed with CASP5C for DVL2 binding. Tested CARD-less mouse caspase 11 isoforms did not regulate Wnt in MODE-K or HEK293T cells, consistent with a primate CASP5C specialization.

Human organoids confirmed the epithelial consequence. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity in primary colonic and small intestinal organoids. CASP5C caused cytosolic and nuclear beta-catenin accumulation and increased Wnt-responsive and proliferation-associated markers. In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased AXIN2 about 3-fold, TCF7 about 1.4-fold, MYC about 1.3-fold, LGR5 about 1.2-fold, and MKI67 about 2.4-fold, while reducing differentiated markers ALPI and MUC2 by about 57% and 53%. CASP5C-expressing colonic organoids had more viable cells, were about 40% larger, contained more Ki-67-positive and EdU-positive cells, and showed fewer CK20-positive enterocytes. CASP5C also enabled prolonged Wnt-independent growth and cell-cycle re-entry after Wnt restoration. After irradiation, CASP5c transcript increased about 42.4-fold within 24 hours, exceeding CASP5a and CASP5b induction, and CASP5C-expressing organoids showed rapid increases in LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7, and MYC.

Mechanistically, CASP5C disrupted the beta-catenin destruction complex through catalytic APC cleavage. CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phosphorylated beta-catenin, APC, and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes after Wnt stimulation and reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. The catalytically inactive CASP5C(C173A) lost Wnt reporter, beta-catenin localization, and gene-expression effects. CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, while CARD deletion from CASP5A or CASP5B increased activity and Wnt reporter signaling, supporting an inhibitory role for the CARD.

APC was a direct functional substrate. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites clustered mainly in the APC N-terminal region. CASP5C, but not CASP5A, CASP5B, or CASP5C(C173A), generated APC cleavage products in cells, and recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. Mutation of APC D556 to alanine abolished the major CASP5C-mediated cleavage products. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, reintroduced wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the destruction complex and enhance TOPFlash activity, whereas APC(D556A) reduced this effect. APC fragments mimicking cleavage products lowered beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased reporter activity, although additional cleavage sites may contribute.

CASP5C localized to the epithelial compartment where Wnt amplification would be useful. In healthy human colon single-cell data, CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent in LGR5-positive stem cells, increased in transit-amplifying cells, and were most enriched in enterocytes, while CASP4 was more uniform. In situ hybridization showed CASP5a and CASP5b more luminally, whereas CASP5c localized deeper in crypts and overlapped with MKI67-positive transit-amplifying zones and AXIN2. Immunohistochemistry, CIBERSORTx, and pseudotime analyses similarly associated CASP5C with proliferative early-lineage states and CASP5A/B with mature or differentiating states.

Endogenous knockdown experiments supported a competitive isoform model. During differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b rose later with differentiated markers. Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C enhanced Wnt reporter activity, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination by about 50%, increased organoid size by about 60%, increased EdU-positive and Ki-67-positive cells, and increased ATP concentration. Concurrent CASP5c knockdown returned these effects toward baseline.

## Discussion

The data identify CASP5C as a non-inflammasome amplifier of Wnt/beta-catenin signaling in human intestinal epithelial cells. CASP5C differs from CASP5A and CASP5B by lacking the CARD region, displaying higher enzymatic activity, and requiring catalytic function to promote Wnt output. # A CARD-less CASP5 Isoform Amplifies Epithelial Wnt Signaling Through APC Cleavage

## Abstract

CASP5 is a human inflammatory caspase-family protease related to CASP4, but its function has remained unclear because CASP4 is the closer counterpart of mouse caspase 11 and mediates the tested noncanonical inflammasome responses. We examined whether CASP5 has a distinct function in intestinal epithelial cells. Sequence, expression, organoid, cell-line, biochemical, tissue, and public-dataset analyses showed that intestinal CASP5 is expressed as three isoforms with divergent activities. CASP5A and CASP5B contain all or part of an N-terminal caspase activation and recruitment domain (CARD), whereas CASP5C lacks the CARD. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, amplified beta-catenin/TCF-dependent Wnt transcription in cell lines and human intestinal organoids. CASP5C associated with dishevelled proteins (DVL), Wnt signaling hubs linking receptor activation to beta-catenin control, and cleaved APC, a scaffold and tumor suppressor in the beta-catenin destruction complex, principally at D556. This disrupted destruction-complex composition, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination, promoted nuclear beta-catenin accumulation, and increased organoid proliferation. CASP5C was enriched in transit-amplifying epithelial cells, while CASP5A and CASP5B restrained CASP5C by competing for DVL binding. These findings identify CASP5C as a human intestinal epithelial amplifier of Wnt signaling.

## Introduction

Inflammatory caspases are best known for linking microbial sensing to innate immune responses and inflammatory cell death. Human CASP4 and CASP5 are related to mouse caspase 11, but they are not equivalent products of this evolutionary relationship. CASP4 is more similar to mouse caspase 11 and retained the main noncanonical inflammasome role after cytosolic lipopolysaccharide detection. CASP5 arose after primate-specific gene duplication, shares high sequence similarity with CASP4, and can bind lipopolysaccharide through a CARD region, yet its biological role has been uncertain.

The intestinal epithelium is a relevant setting for resolving this question because it is both an immune barrier and a rapidly renewing tissue. Renewal depends on Wnt/beta-catenin signaling, which controls the balance between stem-cell maintenance, proliferation, and differentiation. Wnt signals are strongest near the crypt base. Transit-amplifying cells, the proliferative progeny between stem cells and differentiated enterocytes, move away from this niche but still require Wnt activity.

Canonical Wnt signaling is constrained by the beta-catenin destruction complex, a multiprotein assembly that includes AXIN1 and APC. APC is a central scaffold protein and tumor suppressor that helps promote beta-catenin phosphorylation and ubiquitination. When Wnt signaling is active, dishevelled proteins, or DVL proteins, connect receptor activation to downstream inhibition of this complex, allowing beta-catenin to accumulate and enter the nucleus. Nuclear beta-catenin drives TCF-dependent transcription, measured experimentally by TOPFlash reporter activity. We asked whether CASP5 has a non-inflammasome function in human intestinal epithelial cells and which CASP5 isoform mediates that function.

## Methods

The study combined comparative, cellular, biochemical, organoid, tissue, and dataset approaches. Sequence and phylogenetic analyses compared human CASP4, human CASP5, and mouse caspase 11. Human Protein Atlas and single-cell datasets defined tissue and cell-type distributions. Human intestinal organoids modeled epithelial CASP5 expression and function, while monocyte-derived macrophages provided an inflammatory comparator.

Mechanistic studies used HEK293T, HCT116, MODE-K, APC-knockout HEK293T cells, and human organoids. Wnt activity was assessed by TOPFlash/FOPFlash reporter assays, beta-catenin localization, Wnt-responsive gene expression, beta-catenin ubiquitination, and organoid growth. CASP5-associated proteins were identified by BioID2 proximity labeling and mass spectrometry, then tested by co-immunoprecipitation, domain mapping, and immunoblotting. Catalytically inactive CASP5C(C173A), CARD-deleted constructs, APC D556A mutation, APC fragments, and recombinant CASP5C tested enzymatic mechanism. Irradiated colonic organoids modeled injury and recovery, and human tissue plus disease datasets evaluated localization and associations.

## Results

CASP4, not CASP5, mediated the tested noncanonical inflammasome responses. In human intestinal epithelial cells derived from colonic organoids, noncanonical inflammasome activation and clearance of intracellular Salmonella Typhimurium and Shigella flexneri depended on CASP4. Human monocyte-derived macrophages showed the same CASP4 dependence during responses to virulent and avirulent live bacteria. These results argued that CASP5 does not simply duplicate the CASP4 antibacterial inflammasome function in the tested systems.

Expression analyses instead pointed to intestinal epithelial specialization. CASP4 transcripts were broadly distributed, whereas CASP5 transcripts were concentrated mainly in the intestinal tract and appendix and, at single-cell resolution, largely in intestinal epithelial cells and monocytes or macrophages. CASP5 transcripts were more abundant in healthy colonic epithelial fractions and organoids than in monocyte-derived macrophages, while CASP4, CASP1, and PYCARD showed the reverse pattern. Human colonic and small intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B, and CASP5C. CASP5A is approximately 49.7 kDa and contains the CARD, CASP5B is approximately 43.2 kDa and lacks part of this domain, and CASP5C is approximately 33.3 kDa and lacks the entire CARD. siRNA experiments supported CASP5C as a distinct isoform rather than a cleavage product. CASP5 increased after Salmonella Typhimurium, IFN-beta, or IFN-gamma stimulation, but lipopolysaccharide alone did not, consistent with low epithelial TLR4 expression.

BioID2 proximity labeling connected CASP5 to Wnt signaling by identifying DVL1, DVL2, and DVL3 among leading CASP5-associated proteins. Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed endogenous CASP5 interactions with DVL proteins in colonic organoids, and AXIN1 and beta-catenin were present in CASP5-DVL2 complexes. Domain mapping implicated the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain, although the interaction may be direct or indirect.

Despite shared DVL binding, only CASP5C promoted Wnt signaling. In HEK293T and HCT116 cells lacking endogenous CASP5, CASP5C increased TOPFlash activity with or without WNT3A. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1, and CASP4 did not produce comparable activation. CASP5C increased AXIN2 and TCF7 expression and promoted nuclear beta-catenin accumulation. Co-expression of CASP5A or CASP5B reduced CASP5C-mediated reporter activity and competed with CASP5C for DVL2 interaction. Tested CARD-less mouse caspase 11 isoforms did not regulate Wnt in MODE-K or HEK293T cells, suggesting primate-specific CASP5C specialization.

Primary human organoids reproduced this isoform specificity. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity in colonic and small intestinal organoids, caused cytosolic and nuclear beta-catenin accumulation, and increased Wnt-responsive and proliferation-associated markers. In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased AXIN2 about threefold, TCF7 about 1.4-fold, MYC about 1.3-fold, LGR5 about 1.2-fold, and MKI67 about 2.4-fold, while reducing differentiated markers ALPI and MUC2 by about 57% and 53%. CASP5C-expressing organoids contained more viable cells, were about 40% larger, had more Ki-67-positive and EdU-positive cells, and had fewer CK20-positive enterocytes. CASP5C also supported prolonged Wnt-independent growth and cell-cycle re-entry after Wnt restoration. After irradiation, CASP5c transcript rose about 42.4-fold within 24 hours, exceeding CASP5a and CASP5b induction, and CASP5C-expressing organoids rapidly increased LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7, and MYC during recovery.

CASP5C acted through catalytic disruption of the beta-catenin destruction complex. After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phosphorylated beta-catenin, APC, and beta-TrCP within AXIN1-containing complexes and reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. The catalytic mutant CASP5C(C173A) lost the ability to promote Wnt reporter activity, beta-catenin localization, and Wnt-responsive gene expression. CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and deleting the CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased enzymatic activity and Wnt reporter signaling, supporting the interpretation that CARD absence releases catalytic activity.

APC was a key CASP5C substrate. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites clustered mainly in the APC N terminus. In cells, CASP5C, but not CASP5A, CASP5B, or CASP5C(C173A), produced APC cleavage products, and recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. Mutation of APC D556 to alanine abolished the major cleavage products, establishing D556 as a primary site. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, reintroduced wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the destruction complex and enhance TOPFlash activity; APC(D556A) reduced this effect. APC fragments mimicking cleavage products decreased beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased reporter activity, while not excluding additional CASP5C cleavage sites.

CASP5C localized to the epithelial compartment where Wnt amplification would be useful. In healthy human colon single-cell data, CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent from LGR5-positive stem cells, increased in transit-amplifying cells, and were most enriched in enterocytes; CASP4 was more uniform. In situ hybridization showed that CASP5a and CASP5b were more luminal, whereas CASP5c localized deeper in crypts and overlapped strongly with MKI67-positive transit-amplifying zones and AXIN2. Immunohistochemistry, CIBERSORTx, and pseudotime analyses likewise linked CASP5C with proliferative early-lineage states and CASP5A/B with differentiated epithelial states.

Endogenous perturbations supported a competitive model. During differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, while CASP5a and CASP5b increased later with mature epithelial markers. Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C enhanced reporter activity in colonic and small intestinal organoids; simultaneous CASP5c knockdown restored activity toward baseline. Preserving CASP5C while reducing CASP5A/B reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination by about 50%, increased organoid size by about 60%, increased EdU-positive and Ki-67-positive cells, and raised ATP concentration. These effects were prevented by simultaneous CASP5c knockdown.

Disease-associated observations were suggestive but not definitive. CASP5 and CASP4 expression increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining cell-type distributions. Colon or rectal adenocarcinomas carried CASP5 mutations in about 3% of cases, and CASP5 mutations tended to co-occur with other Wnt pathway mutations while being mutually exclusive with APC mutations. In microsatellite instability-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were more common than APC mutations in the cited comparison, about 68% versus 17%. These findings raise hypotheses but do not establish CASP5C as a cause of inflammatory bowel disease or colorectal cancer.

## Discussion

These results define CASP5C as an epithelial Wnt amplifier distinct from CASP4-mediated noncanonical inflammasome activation. The isoform structure explains the functional divergence: CASP5C lacks the CARD region, displays higher enzymatic activity, and requires catalytic activity to enhance Wnt signaling. CASP5A and CASP5B bind DVL proteins but do not amplify Wnt, and instead can restrain CASP5C by competing for DVL binding.

The proposed mechanism places CASP5C at the interface between DVL-associated receptor signaling and proteolytic remodeling of the destruction complex. By cleaving APC principally at D556, CASP5C weakens beta-catenin retention and ubiquitination in AXIN1-containing complexes, increasing nuclear beta-catenin and Wnt-responsive transcription. This is best interpreted as pathway amplification, not replacement of canonical Wnt regulation.

Transit-amplifying cells provide a coherent biological setting for this mechanism. These proliferative progenitors have left the Wnt-rich stem-cell zone but remain dependent on Wnt signaling. CASP5C enrichment in MKI67-positive and AXIN2-positive crypt regions suggests a local mechanism for sustaining proliferation, whereas later CASP5A/B expression may help turn down this signal as epithelial cells mature.

The evidence is mechanistically strong but has limits. Much of it comes from cell-line, organoid, overexpression, knockdown, and biochemical assays. Human tissue and public datasets support localization and disease associations, but they do not prove causal in vivo function in human intestine. Mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce CASP5C activity in the tested systems, limiting mouse-to-human inference. Disease associations remain future directions rather than clinical mechanisms or therapeutic validation.

## Conclusion

CASP5 has a human intestinal epithelial role that is distinct from CASP4-driven noncanonical inflammasome activation. CASP5C binds DVL-associated Wnt complexes, cleaves APC at D556, disrupts the beta-catenin destruction complex, reduces beta-catenin ubiquitination, and promotes organoid proliferation. CASP5A and CASP5B do not share this Wnt-amplifying activity and can restrain CASP5C through DVL competition. Together, the data support CASP5C as a transit-amplifying-cell Wnt amplifier that contributes to epithelial homeostasis and repair in the tested systems.

CASP4 transcripts were broadly distributed, whereas CASP5 transcripts were concentrated mainly in the intestinal tract and appendix and, at single-cell resolution, largely limited to intestinal epithelial cells and monocytes/macrophages. Healthy colonic epithelial fractions and organoids expressed more CASP5 than macrophages, while CASP4, CASP1, and PYCARD showed the reverse pattern. Human organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B, and CASP5C. CASP5B lacks part of the CASP5A CARD region, and CASP5C lacks the entire CARD. siRNA experiments supported CASP5C as a distinct isoform rather than a cleavage product. In organoids, CASP5 increased after Salmonella Typhimurium, interferon-beta, or interferon-gamma stimulation, but not after lipopolysaccharide alone.

BioID2 proximity labeling in colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2, and DVL3 among leading CASP5-associated proteins, and gene ontology enrichment pointed to Wnt-related processes. Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed that endogenous CASP5 isoforms interacted with DVL proteins, and AXIN1 and beta-catenin were detected in CASP5-DVL2 complexes. Domain mapping localized the interaction to the dishevelled DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain, although the data did not show whether binding was direct.

Among the isoforms, CASP5C uniquely promoted Wnt signaling. In HEK293T and HCT116 cells lacking endogenous CASP5, CASP5C increased TOPFlash reporter activity with or without WNT3A, whereas CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1, and CASP4 did not. CASP5C also increased AXIN2 and TCF7 expression and promoted nuclear beta-catenin accumulation. CASP5A or CASP5B co-expression reduced CASP5C-mediated Wnt activity and competed with CASP5C for DVL2 binding. Tested CARD-less mouse caspase 11 isoforms did not regulate Wnt signaling in MODE-K or HEK293T cells, suggesting primate CASP5 specialization.

Primary human organoids reproduced these findings. In colonic and small intestinal organoids, CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity, cytosolic and nuclear beta-catenin, and Wnt-responsive and proliferation-associated markers. In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased AXIN2 about 3-fold, TCF7 about 1.4-fold, MYC about 1.3-fold, LGR5 about 1.2-fold, and MKI67 about 2.4-fold, while reducing ALPI and MUC2 by about 57% and 53%. CASP5C-expressing organoids were about 40% larger, had more viable, Ki-67-positive, and EdU-positive cells, and had fewer CK20-positive enterocytes than controls. CASP5C also enabled prolonged Wnt-independent growth and cell-cycle re-entry after Wnt restoration. In irradiated organoids, CASP5c transcript increased about 42.4-fold within 24 hours, and CASP5C-expressing organoids rapidly increased LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7, and MYC.

Mechanistically, CASP5C acted on the beta-catenin destruction complex. After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phosphorylated beta-catenin, APC, and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes and reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. Catalytic activity was required: CASP5C(C173A) lost the ability to promote Wnt reporter activity, beta-catenin localization, and Wnt-responsive gene expression. CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and deleting the CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased enzymatic activity and Wnt reporter signaling. These experiments support a model in which CARD absence permits CASP5C catalytic activity.

APC was the critical substrate linking CASP5C activity to Wnt amplification. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites clustered mainly in the APC N-terminal region. In cells, CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, produced APC cleavage products, and recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. Mutation of APC D556 to alanine abolished the major cleavage products, establishing D556 as a primary cleavage site. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt destruction-complex assembly and enhance Wnt reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A) reduced this effect. APC fragments mimicking the cleavage products decreased beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased Wnt reporter activity, although additional cleavage sites may also contribute.

The cellular localization of CASP5 isoforms aligned with their functional differences. In healthy human colon single-cell data, CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent from LGR5-positive stem cells, increased in transit-amplifying cells, and were most enriched in enterocytes, whereas CASP4 was more uniform. In situ hybridization showed that CASP5a and CASP5b were more luminal, while CASP5c localized deeper in crypts and overlapped strongly with MKI67-positive transit-amplifying zones and AXIN2. Immunohistochemistry, CIBERSORTx, and pseudotime analyses similarly linked CASP5C to proliferative crypt or early-lineage states and CASP5A/B to mature epithelial states.

Endogenous knockdown experiments supported a competitive isoform model. During colonic organoid differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b increased later with differentiation markers. Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C enhanced Wnt reporter activity in colonic and small intestinal organoids, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination by about 50%, increased organoid size by about 60%, increased EdU-positive and Ki-67-positive cells, and increased ATP concentration. These effects were prevented by simultaneous CASP5c knockdown.

## Discussion

These findings define a CASP5 function distinct from CASP4-mediated noncanonical inflammasome activation. Rather than acting as the principal bacterial lipopolysaccharide sensor in the tested systems, CASP5 operates in human intestinal epithelial cells as an isoform-specific modulator of Wnt signaling. CASP5C lacks the CARD found in CASP5A and partially retained in CASP5B, has higher enzymatic activity, requires its catalytic cysteine for Wnt amplification, and can be restrained by CASP5A/B through competition for dishevelled binding.

The proposed mechanism connects CASP5C to a central node of epithelial renewal. By binding dishevelled proteins through the CASP5 catalytic domain and the dishevelled DEP domain, CASP5C is positioned near Wnt pathway machinery. Its cleavage of APC at D556 weakens destruction-complex function, reduces beta-catenin ubiquitination, and increases beta-catenin-dependent transcription. CASP5C therefore acts as an amplifier, not a replacement for canonical Wnt regulation.

Transit-amplifying cells provide a coherent biological context. These cells proliferate after leaving the Wnt-rich stem-cell zone, and CASP5C enrichment in MKI67-positive, AXIN2-positive crypt regions suggests that CASP5C helps sustain Wnt responsiveness during this transition. The later rise of CASP5A and CASP5B during differentiation suggests an isoform switch that may help tune epithelial maturation.

Several boundaries are important. The study relies heavily on organoids, cell lines, overexpression, knockdown, and biochemical assays. Human tissue and public datasets support localization and disease-associated patterns, but they do not establish in vivo causal function in human intestine. Mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce CASP5C Wnt activity in the tested systems. Disease observations also remain suggestive: CASP5 and CASP4 expression increased in ulcerative colitis datasets, and CASP5 mutations appeared in colorectal cancer subsets. These associations do not show that CASP5C causes inflammatory bowel disease or colorectal cancer, and they do not validate CASP5C as a therapeutic target.

## Conclusion

CASP5C is a CARD-less human CASP5 isoform that amplifies Wnt/beta-catenin signaling in intestinal epithelial progenitor contexts. It binds dishevelled proteins, depends on catalytic activity, cleaves APC at D556, disrupts beta-catenin destruction-complex function, and supports organoid growth and proliferation. CASP5A and CASP5B do not share this Wnt-promoting activity and can restrain CASP5C by competing for dishevelled binding. Together, the data support a model in which CASP5 isoform balance helps tune Wnt signaling across the human intestinal epithelial lineage, especially in transit-amplifying cells, while leaving disease relevance as an important but unresolved direction for future study.

# A CARD-less CASP5 Isoform Tunes Wnt Signaling in Human Intestinal Progenitors

## Abstract

Human CASP5 is closely related to CASP4, yet its biological role has remained unclear because CASP4 is the principal human mediator of noncanonical inflammasome activation after cytosolic lipopolysaccharide detection. We asked whether CASP5 has a distinct function in human intestinal epithelial cells, where Wnt/beta-catenin signaling controls renewal and differentiation. Using expression datasets, human intestinal organoids, macrophage comparators, cell-line reconstitution, proximity labeling, biochemical assays, tissue localization, and injury models, we found that CASP5 is concentrated in intestinal epithelial settings and is expressed as three isoforms. CASP5C, a short isoform lacking the caspase activation and recruitment domain (CARD), uniquely promoted beta-catenin/TCF-dependent TOPFlash reporter activity, nuclear beta-catenin accumulation, Wnt-responsive gene expression, organoid growth, and epithelial proliferation. Mechanistically, CASP5C associated with dishevelled proteins, Wnt signaling hubs that link receptor activation to downstream beta-catenin regulation, and cleaved APC, a scaffold and tumor suppressor in the beta-catenin destruction complex, primarily at D556. This activity reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. CASP5C was enriched in transit-amplifying cells, proliferative progenitors between intestinal stem cells and differentiated enterocytes, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B restrained CASP5C by competing for dishevelled binding. These findings define CASP5C as a human epithelial Wnt amplifier distinct from CASP4-mediated inflammasome signaling.

## Introduction

Inflammatory caspases are usually discussed as innate immune proteases, but human CASP5 has not fit neatly into this framework. CASP4 and CASP5 are human orthologues of mouse caspase 11, and both share sequence similarity and CARD regions capable of binding lipopolysaccharide. However, CASP4 is closer to mouse caspase 11 and carries the main human noncanonical inflammasome function. CASP5 arose after a primate-specific duplication, raising the possibility that it acquired a different role rather than simply duplicating CASP4 activity.

The intestinal epithelium offered a focused setting for testing this possibility. Wnt/beta-catenin signaling is required for intestinal development, regeneration, and the balance between stem-cell renewal and differentiation. Dishevelled proteins, abbreviated DVL, are Wnt signaling hubs that connect activated receptors to downstream control of the beta-catenin destruction complex. This complex includes AXIN1 and APC; APC acts as a scaffold and tumor suppressor that helps promote beta-catenin phosphorylation and ubiquitination. When destruction-complex activity falls, beta-catenin accumulates and drives TCF-dependent transcription. Transit-amplifying cells, the proliferative epithelial progeny between stem cells and differentiated enterocytes, depend on Wnt signaling as they move away from the Wnt-rich crypt base.

We investigated whether CASP5 has a non-inflammasome epithelial function and which isoform mediates it. The central finding is that CASP5C, not CASP5A or CASP5B, amplifies Wnt signaling by binding DVL proteins and cleaving APC at D556. CASP5A and CASP5B retain DVL binding but limit CASP5C activity through competition.

## Methods

The study combined computational, cellular, organoid, tissue, and biochemical approaches. Sequence and phylogenetic analyses compared human CASP4, human CASP5, and mouse caspase 11. Human Protein Atlas and single-cell datasets were used to compare CASP4 and CASP5 expression across tissues and cell types. Human colonic and small intestinal organoids modeled intestinal epithelial CASP5 expression and function, while human monocyte-derived macrophages served as inflammatory comparators.

Noncanonical inflammasome function was tested in organoid-derived epithelial cells and macrophages exposed to Salmonella Typhimurium, Shigella flexneri, or other live bacterial challenges. Wnt function was tested in HEK293T, HCT116, MODE-K, APC-knockout HEK293T cells, and human organoids using overexpression, knockdown, rescue, beta-catenin localization, Wnt-responsive gene expression, beta-catenin ubiquitination, organoid growth, Ki-67 staining, EdU incorporation, and TOPFlash/FOPFlash reporter assays. TOPFlash measures beta-catenin/TCF-dependent transcriptional activity, whereas FOPFlash is the specificity control.

CASP5-associated proteins were identified by BioID2 proximity labeling and mass spectrometry in human colonic organoids. Co-immunoprecipitation, domain mapping, and immunoblotting assessed interactions among CASP5 isoforms, DVL1, DVL2, DVL3, AXIN1, beta-catenin, APC, and beta-TrCP. Catalytically inactive CASP5C(C173A), CARD-deleted CASP5 constructs, APC(D556A), APC fragments, and recombinant CASP5C tested enzymatic mechanism. Irradiated colonic organoids modeled epithelial injury and recovery. Human tissue and public disease datasets evaluated localization and associations.

## Results

CASP4, not CASP5, mediated the tested noncanonical inflammasome responses. In human colonic organoid-derived epithelial cells, inflammasome activation and clearance of intracellular Salmonella Typhimurium and Shigella flexneri depended on CASP4. In monocyte-derived macrophages, phagocytosis of virulent and avirulent live bacteria again supported bacterial clearance through CASP4 rather than CASP5. These results argued that CASP5 is not simply a redundant CASP4-like inflammasome protease in the tested human systems.

Expression analyses instead pointed to intestinal epithelial biology. CASP4 transcripts were broadly distributed, whereas CASP5 transcripts were concentrated mainly in the intestinal tract and appendix and, at single-cell level, largely in intestinal epithelial cells and monocytes/macrophages. CASP5 increased after Salmonella Typhimurium, IFN-beta, or IFN-gamma stimulation in organoids, but lipopolysaccharide alone did not increase CASP5, consistent with low epithelial TLR4 expression.

Proximity labeling linked CASP5 to Wnt signaling. Domain mapping implicated the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain, although the interaction may be direct or indirect.

Functional experiments showed that CASP5C uniquely promotes Wnt signaling. In CASP5-negative HEK293T and HCT116 cells, CASP5C increased TOPFlash activity with or without WNT3A. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1, and CASP4 did not produce comparable activation. CASP5C increased AXIN2, TCF7, and nuclear beta-catenin. Tested CARD-less mouse caspase 11 isoforms did not regulate Wnt in MODE-K or HEK293T cells, consistent with CASP5C specialization after primate CASP5 duplication.

The same isoform specificity was observed in primary intestinal organoids. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity in human colonic and small intestinal organoids. In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased AXIN2 about threefold, TCF7 about 1.4-fold, MYC about 1.3-fold, LGR5 about 1.2-fold, and MKI67 about 2.4-fold, while reducing differentiated markers ALPI and MUC2 by about 57% and 53%. APC was the relevant substrate. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites in APC clustered mainly in the N-terminal region. CASP5C, but not CASP5A, CASP5B, or CASP5C(C173A), produced APC cleavage products in cells, and recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. APC(D556A) abolished the major CASP5C-mediated cleavage products. APC fragments mimicking cleavage products decreased beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased reporter activity, although additional cleavage sites may also contribute.

In healthy human colon single-cell data, CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent in LGR5-positive stem cells, increased in transit-amplifying cells, and were most enriched in enterocytes; CASP4 was more uniform. Simultaneous CASP5c knockdown prevented these effects. Co-expression of CASP5A or CASP5B reduced CASP5C-mediated Wnt activity and competed with CASP5C for DVL2 interaction.

Disease-associated findings were suggestive but not definitive. CASP5 and CASP4 expression increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining their cell-type distributions. Colon or rectal adenocarcinomas carried CASP5 mutations in about 3% of cases in the analyzed context. CASP5 mutations tended to co-occur with Wnt pathway mutations but were mutually exclusive with APC mutations. In microsatellite instability-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were more common than APC mutations in the cited comparison, about 68% versus about 17%. These observations motivate future work but remain associative.

## Discussion

The findings separate CASP5 biology from the CASP4-centered view of human noncanonical inflammasome activation. CASP5C is not an inflammasome substitute in these assays; it is a CARD-less epithelial protease that amplifies Wnt signaling. Its mechanism is coherent across molecular and cellular scales: DVL binding positions CASP5C near Wnt pathway complexes, catalytic activity cleaves APC at D556, destruction-complex function weakens, beta-catenin ubiquitination falls, and beta-catenin-dependent transcription increases.

The isoform contrast is central. CASP5A and CASP5B bind DVL but do not activate Wnt signaling like CASP5C. Their later expression in differentiating organoids and their ability to compete with CASP5C for DVL2 suggest a built-in attenuation mechanism. In transit-amplifying cells, high CASP5C may help sustain Wnt output as cells leave the crypt base; in more differentiated cells, CASP5A and CASP5B may restrain this signal.

Several caveats limit the scope of the model. The study relies heavily on cell lines, organoids, overexpression, knockdown, and biochemical assays, while human tissue and dataset analyses mainly support localization and association. Mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce CASP5C Wnt activity, limiting direct mouse-to-human inference. APC D556 is strongly supported as a primary cleavage site, but other sites may contribute. The ulcerative colitis and colorectal cancer observations are hypothesis-generating, not causal or clinical evidence.

## Conclusion

CASP5C provides a non-inflammasome function for human CASP5 in intestinal epithelial biology. By lacking the CARD region, displaying high catalytic activity, binding DVL proteins, and cleaving APC at D556, CASP5C amplifies Wnt/beta-catenin signaling in transit-amplifying cells and supports organoid growth and proliferation. CASP5A and CASP5B counterbalance this activity by competing for DVL binding, linking CASP5 isoform usage to epithelial renewal, differentiation, and repair.

The mechanism fits the architecture of canonical Wnt signaling. DVL proteins connect receptor-proximal Wnt activation to destruction-complex regulation. APC helps maintain beta-catenin phosphorylation and ubiquitination when Wnt signaling is low. CASP5C binding to DVL proteins positions the protease near this regulatory machinery, and APC cleavage at D556 weakens beta-catenin degradation. CASP5C therefore modulates Wnt output rather than replacing canonical regulation.

The cell-type distribution provides biological context. CASP5C enrichment in transit-amplifying cells suggests a local means of sustaining Wnt responsiveness as epithelial progenitors move through the crypt. The later rise of CASP5A and CASP5B during differentiation, together with their ability to compete for DVL2 binding, suggests an isoform switch that dampens CASP5C activity as cells mature.

The evidence is mechanistically strong but has limits. It relies heavily on organoids, cell lines, overexpression, knockdown, and biochemical assays. Human tissue and public datasets support localization and association, but do not prove in vivo causality. Mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce CASP5C Wnt activity, limiting direct mouse-to-human inference. The APC D556 site is strongly supported as a primary cleavage site, but additional sites may contribute. Ulcerative colitis and colorectal cancer observations remain associative and hypothesis-generating.

## Conclusion

CASP5C is a CARD-less human CASP5 isoform that amplifies Wnt/beta-catenin signaling in intestinal epithelial transit-amplifying cells. By binding dishevelled proteins and cleaving APC at D556, CASP5C disrupts the beta-catenin destruction complex, reduces beta-catenin ubiquitination, and promotes organoid proliferation. CASP5A and CASP5B counter this activity by competing for dishevelled binding. These findings expand inflammatory caspase biology beyond innate immune activation and identify an isoform-specific mechanism for tuning intestinal epithelial renewal and repair.

# A CARD-less caspase 5 isoform amplifies epithelial Wnt signaling through APC cleavage

## Abstract

Human CASP5 is an inflammatory caspase-family protease related to CASP4, but its function has been difficult to define because CASP4 is the dominant human mediator of noncanonical inflammasome responses to cytosolic lipopolysaccharide. We investigated whether CASP5 has a distinct role in human intestinal epithelial biology. Sequence analyses, bacterial challenge experiments, organoid models, cell-line assays, proximity labeling, biochemical reconstitution, and human tissue datasets showed that CASP4, not CASP5, mediated the tested bacterial noncanonical inflammasome responses. CASP5 instead showed epithelial enrichment and was expressed as three isoforms: CASP5A, CASP5B, and CASP5C. CASP5A and CASP5B contain all or part of the N-terminal caspase activation and recruitment domain (CARD), whereas CASP5C lacks this domain. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, amplified beta-catenin/TCF-dependent Wnt signaling, promoted nuclear beta-catenin accumulation, and supported organoid proliferation. Mechanistically, CASP5 isoforms associated with dishevelled proteins, Wnt signaling hubs that link receptor activation to downstream beta-catenin regulation. CASP5C cleaved APC, a scaffold and tumor suppressor in the beta-catenin destruction complex, primarily at D556. This weakened destruction-complex function and reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. CASP5C was enriched in transit-amplifying cells, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B were more associated with differentiated epithelial states and restrained CASP5C by competing for dishevelled binding. These findings identify CASP5C as a human epithelial amplifier of Wnt signaling rather than an inflammasome effector in the tested intestinal contexts.

## Introduction

The intestinal epithelium is renewed continuously by stem cells, proliferative transit-amplifying cells, and differentiating epithelial lineages. Wnt/beta-catenin signaling is central to this hierarchy because it supports epithelial development, regeneration, stem-cell renewal, and progenitor proliferation. In the absence of sufficient Wnt input, beta-catenin is restrained by the beta-catenin destruction complex, a multiprotein assembly that includes AXIN1 and APC. APC is both a scaffold in this complex and a tumor suppressor frequently altered in colorectal cancer. When Wnt signaling is active, dishevelled proteins, also called DVL proteins, connect receptors to downstream regulation of the destruction complex, allowing beta-catenin to accumulate, enter the nucleus, and drive TCF-dependent transcription.

Inflammatory caspases are usually understood as mediators of innate immune activation and cell death. Human CASP4 and CASP5 are orthologues of mouse caspase 11, but CASP4 is closer to mouse caspase 11 and has retained the main ancestral role in noncanonical inflammasome activation. CASP5 arose after primate-specific gene duplication and shares high sequence similarity with CASP4, yet its biological role has remained uncertain. This uncertainty is sharpened by CASP5 expression in the human intestinal epithelium, where tissue renewal depends on carefully tuned Wnt signaling.

The study asked whether CASP5 has a non-inflammasome function in human intestinal epithelial cells and, if so, which isoform mediates it. Particular attention was given to CASP5C, a shorter isoform lacking the CARD, or caspase activation and recruitment domain. CARDs are N-terminal interaction domains present in CASP5A and CASP5B that participate in inflammatory caspase signaling. The central hypothesis was that CASP5 isoform structure might determine whether CASP5 acts as an innate immune protease or as a modulator of epithelial Wnt signaling in transit-amplifying cells, the proliferative progenitors located between stem cells and differentiated enterocytes.

## Methods

The study integrated computational, biochemical, cellular, organoid, tissue, and public-dataset analyses. Sequence and phylogenetic comparisons placed human CASP5 in relation to human CASP4 and mouse caspase 11, while Human Protein Atlas and single-cell datasets compared tissue distribution and epithelial cell-type localization.

Human colonic and small intestinal organoids modeled epithelial CASP5 expression and function, and monocyte-derived macrophages served as inflammatory comparators. Organoid-derived epithelial cells and macrophages were challenged with intracellular or phagocytosed bacteria to test CASP4 and CASP5 contributions to noncanonical inflammasome activation and bacterial clearance.

Mechanistic experiments used HEK293T, HCT116, MODE-K, APC-knockout HEK293T cells, and human organoids. Wnt activity was measured with TOPFlash, which detects beta-catenin/TCF-dependent transcription, and FOPFlash control reporters. Additional readouts included beta-catenin localization, Wnt-responsive gene expression, beta-catenin ubiquitination, organoid growth, EdU incorporation, Ki-67 staining, differentiation markers, and viability. BioID2 proximity labeling, mass spectrometry, co-immunoprecipitation, domain mapping, and immunoblotting identified CASP5-associated proteins. Catalytically inactive CASP5C(C173A), CARD-deleted CASP5 constructs, APC D556A mutation, APC fragments, recombinant CASP5C, and APC-knockout rescue assays tested the enzymatic mechanism. Irradiated colonic organoids modeled epithelial injury and recovery.

## Results

CASP5 did not account for the tested CASP4-like noncanonical inflammasome responses. Although CASP4 and CASP5 share sequence similarity and their longer forms can bind LPS through CARD regions, CASP4 was more similar to mouse caspase 11. In organoid-derived intestinal epithelial cells, noncanonical inflammasome activation and clearance of intracellular Salmonella Typhimurium and Shigella flexneri depended on CASP4 rather than CASP5. Monocyte-derived macrophages showed the same CASP4 dependence after phagocytosis of live bacteria.

Expression analyses pointed to the intestinal epithelium. CASP4 transcripts were broadly distributed, whereas CASP5 transcripts were concentrated mainly in the intestinal tract and appendix and, at single-cell level, were largely limited to intestinal epithelial cells and monocytes or macrophages. CASP5 transcripts were more abundant in healthy colonic epithelial fractions and organoids than in macrophages, while CASP4, CASP1, and PYCARD showed the reverse pattern. Human colonic and small intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B, and CASP5C, with approximate sizes of 49.7, 43.2, and 33.3 kDa, respectively. CASP5B lacks part of the CASP5A CARD, and CASP5C lacks the entire CARD. siRNA experiments supported CASP5C as a distinct isoform rather than a cleavage product. In organoids, CASP5 increased after Salmonella Typhimurium, IFN-beta, or IFN-gamma stimulation, but not after LPS alone, consistent with low epithelial TLR4 expression.

CASP5 isoforms associated with Wnt pathway components. BioID2 proximity labeling in human colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2, and DVL3 among leading CASP5-associated proteins, and gene ontology enrichment highlighted Wnt-related processes. Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed endogenous CASP5 interactions with DVL proteins, and CASP5-DVL2 complexes contained AXIN1 and beta-catenin. Domain mapping implicated the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain, while leaving open whether the interaction is direct or indirect.

Wnt activation was specific to CASP5C. In HEK293T and HCT116 cells lacking endogenous CASP5, CASP5C increased TOPFlash activity with or without WNT3A. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1, and CASP4 did not produce the same response. CASP5C increased AXIN2 and TCF7 expression and promoted nuclear beta-catenin accumulation. Co-expression of CASP5A or CASP5B reduced CASP5C-mediated Wnt activity and competed with CASP5C for DVL2 interaction. Tested CARD-less mouse caspase 11 isoforms did not regulate Wnt in MODE-K or HEK293T cells, suggesting that CASP5C specialization emerged after primate CASP5 duplication.

Primary human organoids supported the epithelial relevance of this activity. In colonic and small intestinal organoids, CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity, cytosolic and nuclear beta-catenin, and Wnt-responsive and stem/proliferation-associated markers. In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased AXIN2 by about threefold, TCF7 by about 1.4-fold, MYC by about 1.3-fold, LGR5 by about 1.2-fold, and MKI67 by about 2.4-fold, while ALPI and MUC2 decreased by about 57% and 53%. CASP5C-expressing organoids had more viable cells, were about 40% larger, contained more Ki-67-positive and EdU-positive cells, and had fewer CK20-positive enterocytes. CASP5C also supported prolonged Wnt-independent growth and cell-cycle re-entry after Wnt restoration. After irradiation, CASP5c transcript increased about 42.4-fold within 24 hours, and CASP5C-expressing organoids rapidly increased LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7, and MYC.

CASP5C acted through catalytic activity and APC cleavage. CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phosphorylated beta-catenin, APC, and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes after Wnt stimulation and reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. Catalytically inactive CASP5C(C173A) failed to promote Wnt reporter activity, beta-catenin localization, or Wnt-responsive gene expression. CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and CARD deletion from CASP5A or CASP5B increased activity and Wnt reporter signaling. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, produced APC cleavage products in cells, and recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. APC D556A abolished the major CASP5C-mediated cleavage products, establishing D556 as a primary site. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the destruction complex and enhance reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A) reduced this effect.

Human tissue and dataset analyses placed CASP5C in transit-amplifying cells. In healthy colon single-cell data, CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent from LGR5-positive stem cells, increased in transit-amplifying cells, and were most enriched in enterocytes; CASP4 was more uniform. In situ hybridization showed luminal localization of CASP5a and CASP5b, whereas CASP5c localized deeper in crypts and overlapped with MKI67-positive transit-amplifying zones and AXIN2. Immunohistochemistry supported CASP5C enrichment with proliferative crypt markers, while CASP5A and CASP5B were more associated with CK20-positive enterocytes. During organoid differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b rose later with mature epithelial markers. Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C enhanced Wnt reporter activity, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination by about 50%, and increased organoid size by about 60%; simultaneous CASP5c knockdown prevented these effects. These data support a competitive model in which CASP5C preferentially binds DVL2 and amplifies Wnt in transit-amplifying cells, while CASP5A and CASP5B restrain CASP5C as cells differentiate.

## Discussion

These findings identify a CASP5 function distinct from CASP4-mediated noncanonical inflammasome activation. CASP5C is not presented as the main driver of intestinal Wnt signaling, but as a local amplifier that can tune beta-catenin activity in human epithelial progenitors. The mechanism links isoform structure to signaling output: loss of the CARD increases CASP5C catalytic activity, DVL-associated localization connects the protease to Wnt pathway machinery, and APC cleavage at D556 weakens the destruction complex that normally promotes beta-catenin phosphorylation and ubiquitination.

The model helps explain why CASP5 expression is positioned along the intestinal crypt axis. Transit-amplifying cells remain proliferative while moving away from the Wnt-rich stem-cell zone, and CASP5C could help sustain Wnt responsiveness in this intermediate state. Later accumulation of CASP5A and CASP5B provides a potential brake, because these isoforms bind DVL without comparably activating Wnt and can compete with CASP5C for DVL2 binding.

Several caveats remain. The study is mechanistically strong across organoid, cell-line, knockdown, overexpression, rescue, and biochemical assays, but human tissue and public datasets mainly support localization and association rather than proving in vivo causality in human intestine. Mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce CASP5C Wnt activity in the tested systems, limiting direct mouse-to-human inference. APC D556 is strongly supported as a primary cleavage site, but additional CASP5C cleavage sites may contribute. Disease-associated observations are also suggestive rather than definitive: CASP5 and CASP4 expression increased in ulcerative colitis datasets, and CASP5 mutations appeared in subsets of colorectal cancer, including microsatellite instability-high tumors. These patterns motivate future work but do not show that CASP5C causes inflammatory bowel disease or colorectal cancer, and they do not establish CASP5C as a therapeutic target.

## Conclusion

CASP5C is a CARD-less human CASP5 isoform that amplifies Wnt signaling in intestinal epithelial transit-amplifying cells. By binding DVL-associated complexes and cleaving APC primarily at D556, CASP5C disrupts the beta-catenin destruction complex, reduces beta-catenin ubiquitination, and promotes Wnt-responsive transcription, organoid proliferation, and repair-associated responses. CASP5A and CASP5B counterbalance this activity by competing for dishevelled binding, linking CASP5 isoform composition to epithelial renewal and differentiation.

## Results

Initial experiments separated CASP5 from CASP4-mediated noncanonical inflammasome function. Although CASP4 and CASP5 share sequence similarity, CASP4 was more similar to mouse caspase 11. In intestinal epithelial cells derived from colonic organoids, noncanonical inflammasome activation and clearance of intracellular Salmonella Typhimurium and Shigella flexneri depended on CASP4 rather than CASP5. In monocyte-derived macrophages, bacterial phagocytosis likewise activated CASP4-dependent clearance. These results argued against CASP5 as the dominant mediator of the tested bacterial inflammasome responses.

CASP5 expression pointed toward the intestinal epithelium. CASP4 transcripts were broadly distributed, whereas CASP5 was concentrated in intestinal tissues and appendix. Human colonic and small intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C. In organoids, CASP5 increased after Salmonella Typhimurium, IFN-beta or IFN-gamma stimulation, but lipopolysaccharide alone did not, consistent with low epithelial TLR4 expression.

Proteomic analysis connected CASP5 to Wnt signaling. BioID2 proximity labeling in colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3 among leading CASP5-associated proteins, and gene ontology enrichment highlighted Wnt-related processes. Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed endogenous CASP5-DVL interactions. AXIN1 and beta-catenin were present in CASP5-DVL2 complexes. Domain mapping implicated the dishevelled DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain, although the interaction may be direct or indirect.

Functional assays showed that Wnt amplification was specific to CASP5C. In HEK293T and HCT116 cells lacking endogenous CASP5, CASP5C increased TOPFlash activity with or without WNT3A. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 and CASP4 did not. CASP5C increased AXIN2 and TCF7 expression and promoted nuclear beta-catenin accumulation. Co-expression of CASP5A or CASP5B reduced CASP5C-driven reporter activity and competed with CASP5C for DVL2 binding. Tested CARD-less mouse caspase 11 isoforms did not regulate Wnt activity in MODE-K or HEK293T cells.

In primary human organoids, CASP5C again acted as the Wnt-promoting isoform. CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity, caused cytosolic and nuclear beta-catenin accumulation, and induced Wnt-responsive and proliferation-associated markers. In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased AXIN2 by about 3-fold, TCF7 by about 1.4-fold, MYC by about 1.3-fold, LGR5 by about 1.2-fold and MKI67 by about 2.4-fold. ALPI and MUC2 decreased by about 57% and 53%, respectively. CASP5C-expressing colonic organoids contained more viable cells, were about 40% larger, included more Ki-67-positive and EdU-positive cells, and contained fewer CK20-positive enterocytes. CASP5C also allowed prolonged Wnt-independent growth and cell-cycle re-entry after Wnt restoration.

An injury model supported a role in repair-associated Wnt responsiveness. After irradiation of colonic organoids, CASP5c transcript increased about 42.4-fold within 24 hours, compared with about 5.6-fold for CASP5a and 6.6-fold for CASP5b. CASP5C-expressing organoids showed rapid increases in LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7 and MYC after irradiation.

Mechanistic experiments placed CASP5C at the beta-catenin destruction complex. After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced levels of DVL2, beta-catenin, phosphorylated beta-catenin, APC and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes, and reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. These effects required enzymatic activity: CASP5C(C173A) failed to promote Wnt reporter activity, beta-catenin relocalization or Wnt-responsive gene expression. CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and deleting the CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased enzymatic activity and Wnt reporter signaling.

APC was identified as a CASP5C substrate. Predicted cleavage sites clustered mainly in the N-terminal region of APC. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, produced APC cleavage products in cells, whereas CASP5C(C173A) did not. Recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. Mutation of APC D556 to alanine abolished the major CASP5C-mediated cleavage products, establishing D556 as a primary cleavage site. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, reintroduced wild-type APC permitted CASP5C to disrupt the destruction complex and enhance Wnt reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A) reduced this effect. Additional cleavage sites may also contribute.

CASP5C localized to the epithelial compartment where Wnt amplification would be useful. In healthy human colon single-cell data, CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent from LGR5-positive stem cells, increased in transit-amplifying cells, and were most enriched in enterocytes. Transit-amplifying cells are proliferative progenitors between stem cells and differentiated enterocytes. In situ hybridization showed that CASP5a and CASP5b were more luminal, whereas CASP5c localized deeper in crypts and overlapped strongly with MKI67-positive transit-amplifying zones and AXIN2 expression. Immunohistochemistry, CIBERSORTx and pseudotime analyses supported this distribution.

Endogenous isoform knockdown supported a competitive model. During organoid differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b rose later with differentiated epithelial markers. Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C enhanced Wnt reporter activity; concurrent CASP5c knockdown returned reporter activity toward baseline. Preserving CASP5C while reducing CASP5A and CASP5B decreased beta-catenin ubiquitination by about 50%, increased organoid size by about 60%, increased EdU-positive and Ki-67-positive cells, and increased ATP concentration. These growth effects were prevented by simultaneous CASP5c knockdown.

## Discussion

These results define CASP5C as an epithelial Wnt amplifier rather than a redundant CASP4-like inflammasome protease in the tested contexts. The mechanism links a primate-specific inflammatory caspase paralogue to a central epithelial renewal pathway: CASP5C binds dishevelled-containing Wnt complexes, cleaves APC at D556, weakens beta-catenin destruction complex function, reduces beta-catenin ubiquitination and increases Wnt-dependent transcription. The higher activity of CASP5C and CARD-deleted constructs suggests that the CARD restrains catalytic output.

The cellular distribution provides a plausible homeostatic role. Wnt signaling is strongest near the crypt base but must remain active in transit-amplifying cells as they proliferate outside the most Wnt-rich niche. CASP5C enrichment in MKI67-positive, AXIN2-positive transit-amplifying zones suggests a local mechanism for sustaining Wnt responsiveness. CASP5A and CASP5B may help restrain this signal in differentiating cells by competing with CASP5C for dishevelled binding.

The disease-associated findings are suggestive but not definitive. CASP5 and CASP4 expression increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining cell-type distributions. Colorectal cancer datasets showed CASP5 mutations in about 3% of colon or rectal adenocarcinomas, with co-occurrence involving other Wnt pathway mutations and mutual exclusivity with APC mutations in the reported analysis. In a microsatellite instability-high comparison, CASP5 mutations were more common than APC mutations. These observations raise hypotheses about CASP5-Wnt interactions, but they do not establish CASP5C as a cause of disease or as a validated therapeutic target.

Several limitations temper the interpretation. The evidence relies on cell lines, organoids, overexpression, knockdown and biochemical assays. Human tissue and public datasets support localization and association but do not prove causal in vivo function in human intestine. Mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce CASP5C Wnt activity in tested systems, limiting direct mouse modeling. APC D556 is well supported as a primary cleavage site, but additional CASP5C targets or APC cleavage sites may contribute.

## Conclusion

CASP5 has a human intestinal epithelial function distinct from CASP4-mediated noncanonical inflammasome activation. The CARD-less isoform CASP5C binds dishevelled proteins, uses its catalytic activity to cleave APC at D556, disrupts the beta-catenin destruction complex and amplifies Wnt signaling in transit-amplifying epithelial cells. CASP5A and CASP5B do not share this Wnt-promoting activity and can restrain CASP5C by competing for dishevelled binding. Together, these findings reveal an isoform-specific mechanism by which an inflammatory caspase-family protease supports epithelial proliferation and repair while leaving canonical Wnt control intact.

# A CARD-less CASP5 isoform amplifies epithelial Wnt signaling through APC cleavage

## Abstract

Human CASP5 is an inflammatory caspase-family protease related to CASP4, yet its function has remained unclear because CASP4 is the principal human mediator of noncanonical inflammasome activation after cytosolic lipopolysaccharide detection. We asked whether CASP5 has a distinct role in human intestinal epithelial biology, where CASP5 expression is concentrated. Computational, organoid, cell-line, biochemical, tissue, and public-dataset analyses separated CASP5 from CASP4. CASP4, not CASP5, mediated tested noncanonical inflammasome responses to intracellular bacteria in epithelial cells and macrophages. Human intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B, and a shorter CARD-less isoform, CASP5C. All three associated with dishevelled proteins, Wnt signaling hubs that connect receptor activation to beta-catenin regulation, but only CASP5C amplified beta-catenin/TCF transcription, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination, and enhanced organoid growth. CASP5C required catalytic activity, disrupted AXIN1-containing beta-catenin destruction complexes, and cleaved APC at D556. CASP5C was enriched in transit-amplifying cells, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B restrained CASP5C by competing for dishevelled binding. These findings identify CASP5C as an epithelial Wnt amplifier rather than an inflammasome effector in the tested systems.

## Introduction

Inflammatory caspases are usually considered in innate immune detection and cell death. Human CASP4 and CASP5 are both orthologues of mouse caspase 11 and retain lipopolysaccharide-binding CARD regions. A CARD, or caspase activation and recruitment domain, is an N-terminal interaction module present in CASP5A and CASP5B. CASP4 is closer to mouse caspase 11 and retains the ancestral noncanonical inflammasome role. CASP5 arose after primate-specific duplication, shares high sequence similarity with CASP4, and has remained harder to assign to a specific biological function.

The human intestinal epithelium provides a relevant setting. Wnt/beta-catenin signaling maintains epithelial development, regeneration, and the balance between stem-cell renewal and differentiation. In the crypt, Wnt signals are strongest near the stem-cell compartment and remain important for transit-amplifying cells, the proliferative progeny that move away from the crypt base before differentiating. These cells must sustain proliferation while experiencing changing Wnt exposure, making them sensitive to local pathway modulators.

The Wnt pathway offers a mechanistic entry point. Dishevelled proteins, or DVLs, are hub proteins that link activated Wnt receptors to downstream beta-catenin regulation. Without sufficient Wnt input, beta-catenin is restrained by a destruction complex that includes AXIN1 and APC. APC is a scaffold in this complex and a tumor suppressor frequently altered in colorectal cancer. By promoting beta-catenin phosphorylation and ubiquitination, the destruction complex limits beta-catenin accumulation and beta-catenin/TCF transcription. We asked whether CASP5 has a non-inflammasome epithelial function in this pathway, which isoform mediates it, and how that isoform acts.

## Methods

The study combined evolutionary, cellular, biochemical, organoid, tissue, and public-dataset analyses. Sequence and phylogenetic comparisons related human CASP4, human CASP5, and mouse caspase 11. Human Protein Atlas information and single-cell datasets defined tissue distribution and epithelial localization. Human colonic and small intestinal organoids modeled epithelial CASP5 function, while monocyte-derived macrophages served as an inflammatory comparator. Functional separation of CASP4 and CASP5 was tested in epithelial cells and macrophages exposed to intracellular Salmonella Typhimurium and Shigella flexneri.

CASP5 isoform expression was examined in organoids under basal and stimulated conditions. CASP5-interacting proteins were identified using BioID2 proximity labeling and mass spectrometry, then tested by co-immunoprecipitation, domain mapping, and immunoblotting. Wnt signaling was evaluated in cell lines and human organoids using TOPFlash, a beta-catenin/TCF transcriptional reporter, with FOPFlash as a control. Additional readouts included beta-catenin localization, Wnt-responsive gene expression, beta-catenin ubiquitination, organoid growth, proliferation, and differentiation markers. Mechanistic tests used catalytically inactive CASP5C(C173A), CARD-deleted CASP5 constructs, APC D556A mutation, APC fragments, recombinant CASP5C, and destruction-complex assays. Irradiated colonic organoids modeled injury and recovery.

## Results

CASP4 and CASP5 first separated at the level of inflammatory caspase function. Although both proteins share sequence similarity and retain CARD-mediated lipopolysaccharide-binding capacity, CASP4 was closer to mouse caspase 11. In organoid-derived intestinal epithelial cells, noncanonical inflammasome activation and clearance of intracellular Salmonella Typhimurium and Shigella flexneri depended on CASP4 rather than CASP5. Macrophages showed the same pattern after phagocytosis of live bacteria. These results argued against CASP5 being a redundant CASP4-like inflammasome effector in the tested systems.

Expression analyses pointed to the intestine as the relevant setting. CASP4 transcripts were broadly distributed, whereas CASP5 transcripts were concentrated mainly in the intestinal tract and appendix and largely limited to intestinal epithelial cells and monocytes or macrophages. In healthy colonic epithelial fractions and colonic organoids, CASP5 transcripts were more abundant than in macrophages, while CASP4, CASP1, and PYCARD showed the reverse pattern. Organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B, and CASP5C. CASP5B lacks part of the CASP5A CARD region, whereas CASP5C lacks the entire CARD, and siRNA experiments supported CASP5C as a distinct isoform. In organoids, CASP5 increased after Salmonella Typhimurium, IFN-beta, or IFN-gamma stimulation, but not after lipopolysaccharide alone.

Proximity labeling connected CASP5 to Wnt machinery. BioID2 in colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2, and DVL3 among leading CASP5-associated proteins, and gene ontology enrichment highlighted Wnt-related processes. Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed that endogenous CASP5 isoforms associated with DVL1, DVL2, and DVL3. Wnt components including AXIN1 and beta-catenin were present in CASP5-DVL2 complexes. Domain mapping localized the interaction to the dishevelled DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain, although the data did not require direct binding.

Despite shared DVL binding, the isoforms differed sharply in Wnt output. In HEK293T and HCT116 cells lacking endogenous CASP5, CASP5C increased TOPFlash activity with or without WNT3A, whereas CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1, and CASP4 did not. CASP5C increased Wnt-responsive genes including AXIN2 and TCF7 and promoted nuclear beta-catenin accumulation. CASP5A or CASP5B reduced CASP5C-mediated reporter activation and competed with CASP5C for DVL2 interaction. Tested CARD-less mouse caspase 11 isoforms did not regulate Wnt, indicating that CASP5C activity was not a general feature of caspase 11-like proteins in these assays.

Human organoids showed that this pathway effect translated into epithelial growth. In primary colonic and small intestinal organoids, CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity, caused cytosolic and nuclear beta-catenin accumulation, increased Wnt-responsive and proliferation-associated markers, and reduced differentiated epithelial markers. In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased AXIN2 approximately 3-fold and MKI67 approximately 2.4-fold, while reducing ALPI and MUC2 by approximately 57% and 53%. CASP5C-expressing organoids had more viable cells, were approximately 40% larger, contained more Ki-67-positive and EdU-positive cells, and had fewer CK20-positive enterocytes. In irradiated organoids, CASP5c transcript increased approximately 42.4-fold within 24 hours, and CASP5C-expressing organoids rapidly increased repair-associated Wnt targets.

Mechanistically, CASP5C acted on the beta-catenin destruction complex. After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phosphorylated beta-catenin, APC, and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes and reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. The catalytic mutant CASP5C(C173A) lost the ability to promote Wnt reporter activity, beta-catenin relocalization, and Wnt-responsive gene expression. CASP5C displayed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and CARD deletion increased enzymatic activity and Wnt reporter signaling.

APC was the critical substrate linking CASP5C enzymatic activity to Wnt signaling. Candidate CASP5 cleavage sites in APC were predicted mainly in the N-terminal region. In cells, CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, produced APC cleavage products; catalytically inactive CASP5C(C173A) did not. Recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. Mutation of APC D556 to alanine abolished the major CASP5C-mediated cleavage products, establishing D556 as a primary cleavage site. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the destruction complex and enhance Wnt reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A) reduced this effect. APC fragments mimicking cleavage products decreased beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased Wnt reporter activity, although additional cleavage sites may contribute.

Cell-type analyses placed CASP5C in the transit-amplifying compartment. In healthy human colon single-cell data, CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent from LGR5-positive stem cells, increased in transit-amplifying cells, and were most enriched in enterocytes, whereas CASP4 was more uniform. In situ hybridization showed CASP5a and CASP5b in more luminal regions, while CASP5c localized more deeply in crypts and overlapped with MKI67-positive transit-amplifying zones and Wnt-responsive AXIN2. Immunohistochemistry, CIBERSORTx, and pseudotime analyses supported enrichment of CASP5C with proliferative crypt markers and association of CASP5A and CASP5B with more mature epithelial states. During organoid differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b increased later. Reducing CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C enhanced Wnt reporter activity, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination by about 50%, and increased organoid growth and proliferation. Simultaneous CASP5c knockdown prevented these effects.

## Discussion

These findings identify CASP5C as a non-inflammasome amplifier of Wnt signaling in human intestinal epithelial cells. The study begins from a paradox: CASP5 resembles CASP4, yet CASP4 accounts for the tested noncanonical inflammasome functions in both epithelial cells and macrophages. CASP5C resolves this paradox by acting in a different epithelial pathway. It lacks the CARD found in CASP5A and CASP5B, displays higher enzymatic activity, and uses that activity to modulate Wnt signaling through DVL-associated destruction-complex remodeling.

The proposed model is local and isoform specific. In transit-amplifying cells, CASP5C binds DVL proteins through its catalytic domain, cleaves APC primarily at D556, and weakens the AXIN1-containing beta-catenin destruction complex. Reduced beta-catenin phosphorylation and ubiquitination allow beta-catenin to accumulate and enter the nucleus, increasing beta-catenin/TCF transcription. CASP5C does not replace canonical Wnt regulation; it amplifies pathway output in cells moving away from the Wnt-rich crypt base but still requiring proliferative signaling. CASP5A and CASP5B provide a counterweight by binding DVL without producing the same Wnt activation and by competing with CASP5C for DVL2 association.

Disease-associated observations extend the relevance of this mechanism without proving disease causation. CASP5 and CASP4 expression increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while preserving their cell-type distributions. CASP5 mutations occurred in about 3% of colon or rectal adenocarcinomas, tended to co-occur with other Wnt pathway mutations, and were mutually exclusive with APC mutations in the reported analysis. In microsatellite instability-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were more common than APC mutations, about 68% versus about 17%. These patterns suggest a possible relationship between CASP5 and Wnt-altered epithelial states but remain associative.

Several constraints define the strength of inference. The mechanistic evidence is broad, but much of the causal work used cell-line, organoid, overexpression, knockdown, and biochemical assays rather than in vivo human intestine. Tissue and public-dataset analyses support cell-type localization and disease association but do not establish human in vivo necessity or sufficiency. Mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce CASP5C Wnt activity, limiting mouse-to-human extrapolation. APC D556 is strongly supported as a primary cleavage site, but additional cleavage events may contribute.

## Conclusion

CASP5 has a human intestinal epithelial role distinct from CASP4-mediated noncanonical inflammasome activation. Among the three CASP5 isoforms, CASP5C is the Wnt-amplifying form: it lacks the CARD, depends on catalytic activity, associates with dishevelled proteins, cleaves APC at D556, reduces beta-catenin destruction, and supports proliferative organoid behavior. CASP5A and CASP5B restrain this activity by competing for DVL binding. Together, these data support a model in which a primate CASP5 isoform helps tune Wnt signaling in transit-amplifying cells during epithelial homeostasis and repair, while disease links remain suggestive directions for future investigation.

## Article_J

# Article_J

# A CARD-less CASP5 isoform amplifies epithelial Wnt signaling through APC cleavage

## Abstract

CASP5 is an inflammatory caspase-family protease related to CASP4, yet its function has remained unclear because CASP4 is the dominant human mediator of cytosolic lipopolysaccharide-triggered noncanonical inflammasome activation. Here, CASP5 is shown to have a distinct role in intestinal epithelial biology. Among three epithelial isoforms, CASP5C, a 33.3-kDa protein lacking the caspase activation and recruitment domain (CARD), uniquely amplified Wnt/beta-catenin signaling. CASP5C associated with dishevelled proteins, cleaved APC at D556, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination and disrupted the AXIN1-containing beta-catenin destruction complex. This activity promoted Wnt reporter output, nuclear beta-catenin accumulation, Wnt-responsive gene expression, organoid growth and proliferation. CASP5C was enriched in transit-amplifying cells, proliferative intestinal epithelial progenitors positioned between stem cells and differentiated enterocytes. CASP5A and CASP5B bound dishevelled but did not activate Wnt; instead, they restrained CASP5C by competition. These findings identify CASP5C as a human epithelial Wnt amplifier separate from CASP4-mediated inflammasome function.

## Introduction

Human CASP4 and CASP5 are orthologues of mouse caspase 11 and share substantial sequence similarity. This resemblance has obscured the function of CASP5, because CASP4 is closer to mouse caspase 11 and carries the main ancestral role in noncanonical inflammasome activation after detection of cytosolic lipopolysaccharide. CASP5 arose after primate-specific gene duplication, suggesting that it might have acquired a different function rather than simply duplicating CASP4.

The intestinal epithelium is a compelling context for this question. CASP5 transcripts are concentrated mainly in the intestinal tract and appendix, and single-cell data place them largely in intestinal epithelial cells and monocytes/macrophages. In healthy colonic epithelial fractions and colonic organoids, CASP5 transcripts are more abundant than in monocyte-derived macrophages, whereas CASP4, CASP1 and PYCARD show the reverse pattern. The intestine also depends on continuous Wnt/beta-catenin signaling to balance stem-cell renewal, transit-amplifying cell proliferation and differentiation.

Wnt signaling is controlled through dishevelled, or DVL, proteins that link receptor activation to downstream beta-catenin regulation. A central downstream target is the beta-catenin destruction complex, a multiprotein assembly including AXIN1 and APC. APC is a scaffold protein and tumor suppressor that helps promote beta-catenin phosphorylation and ubiquitination. When this complex is weakened, beta-catenin accumulates, enters the nucleus and drives TCF-dependent transcription, measurable with the TOPFlash reporter. The study asked whether CASP5 has a non-inflammasome epithelial function and, if so, which isoform mediates it.

## Methods

The investigation combined computational, cellular, biochemical, organoid, tissue and public-dataset analyses. Sequence and phylogenetic comparisons evaluated human CASP4, human CASP5 and mouse caspase 11. Expression datasets compared CASP4 and CASP5 tissue and cell-type distributions.

Primary human colonic and small intestinal organoids modeled epithelial CASP5 function. Human monocyte-derived macrophages served as an inflammatory-caspase comparator. Infection and bacterial uptake assays tested noncanonical inflammasome responses. HEK293T, HCT116, MODE-K and APC-knockout HEK293T cells were used for reporter assays, overexpression, knockdown, APC rescue and cleavage experiments.

CASP5-associated proteins were identified using BioID2 proximity labeling, mass spectrometry, co-immunoprecipitation, domain mapping and immunoblotting. Wnt function was measured by reporters, beta-catenin localization, Wnt-responsive gene expression, beta-catenin ubiquitination and organoid growth assays. Catalytically inactive CASP5C(C173A), CARD-deleted CASP5 constructs, APC D556A, APC fragments and recombinant CASP5C tested enzymatic mechanism. Irradiated organoids modeled epithelial injury and recovery, while tissue and disease datasets assessed localization and associations.

## Results

CASP4, not CASP5, mediated the tested noncanonical inflammasome responses. Although CASP4 and CASP5 both contain lipopolysaccharide-binding CARD regions in their longer forms, CASP4 was more similar to mouse caspase 11. In intestinal epithelial cells derived from colonic organoids, noncanonical inflammasome activation and clearance of intracellular Salmonella Typhimurium and Shigella flexneri depended on CASP4. In monocyte-derived macrophages, phagocytosis of virulent and avirulent live bacteria also triggered CASP4-dependent inflammasome activation and bacterial clearance. These findings motivated a search for a separate CASP5 function.

Human colonic and small intestinal organoids expressed three CASP5 isoforms. CASP5A is about 49.7 kDa and contains a CARD, the caspase activation and recruitment domain. CASP5B is about 43.2 kDa and lacks part of this N-terminal region. CASP5C is about 33.3 kDa and lacks the entire CARD. siRNA experiments supported CASP5C as a distinct isoform rather than a cleavage product of CASP5A or CASP5B. In organoids, CASP5 increased after Salmonella Typhimurium, IFN-beta or IFN-gamma stimulation, but not after lipopolysaccharide alone, consistent with low epithelial TLR4 expression.

BioID2 proximity labeling in human colonic organoids linked CASP5 to Wnt signaling. DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3 were among the leading CASP5-associated proteins, and Wnt-related processes were enriched among CASP5 interactors. Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed that endogenous CASP5 isoforms interacted with DVL proteins. AXIN1 and beta-catenin were present in CASP5-DVL2 complexes, placing CASP5 near the destruction-complex machinery. Domain mapping implicated the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain, although the interaction may be direct or indirect.

CASP5C uniquely promoted Wnt pathway activity. In HEK293T and HCT116 cells lacking endogenous CASP5, CASP5C increased TOPFlash reporter activity with or without WNT3A. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 and CASP4 did not produce the same effect. CASP5C increased AXIN2 and TCF7 expression and promoted nuclear beta-catenin accumulation. Co-expression of CASP5A or CASP5B reduced CASP5C-mediated reporter activity and competed with CASP5C for DVL2 binding. CARD-less mouse caspase 11 isoforms did not regulate Wnt in the tested MODE-K or HEK293T systems, indicating that CASP5C specialization is not a general feature of all caspase 11-related proteins.

Primary human organoids showed the same isoform specificity. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity in colonic and small intestinal organoids. CASP5C caused cytosolic and nuclear beta-catenin accumulation and increased Wnt-responsive and stem/proliferation-associated markers. In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased AXIN2 about threefold, TCF7 about 1.4-fold, MYC about 1.3-fold, LGR5 about 1.2-fold and MKI67 about 2.4-fold. It reduced differentiated epithelial markers ALPI and MUC2 by about 57% and 53%. CASP5C-expressing organoids had more viable cells, were about 40% larger, contained more Ki-67-positive and EdU-positive cells, and had fewer CK20-positive enterocytes.

CASP5C also supported stress recovery. It enabled prolonged Wnt-independent organoid growth and allowed organoids to re-enter the cell cycle after Wnt restoration. In irradiated colonic organoids, CASP5c transcript increased about 42.4-fold within 24 hours, compared with about 5.6-fold for CASP5a and 6.6-fold for CASP5b. After irradiation, CASP5C-expressing organoids rapidly increased LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7 and MYC, consistent with enhanced Wnt-dependent epithelial repair programs.

Mechanistically, CASP5C acted through catalytic disruption of the beta-catenin destruction complex. After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phosphorylated beta-catenin, APC and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes and reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. The catalytic mutant CASP5C(C173A) lost the ability to promote Wnt reporter activity, beta-catenin localization and Wnt-responsive gene expression. CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and deleting the CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased enzymatic activity and Wnt reporter signaling. Thus, the absence of the inhibitory CARD enables the activity required for Wnt amplification.

APC was the critical substrate. Bioinformatic prediction identified candidate CASP5 cleavage sites in APC, mostly in the N-terminal region. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, produced APC cleavage products in cells, whereas CASP5C(C173A) did not. Recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. Mutation of APC D556 to alanine abolished the major CASP5C-mediated cleavage products, establishing D556 as a primary cleavage site. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, reintroduced wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the destruction complex and enhance Wnt reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A) reduced this effect. APC fragments mimicking cleavage products decreased beta-catenin in the destruction complex and increased Wnt reporter activity, although additional cleavage sites may contribute.

Tissue and dataset analyses placed CASP5C in the transit-amplifying compartment. Transit-amplifying cells are proliferative epithelial progenitors between stem cells and differentiated enterocytes. In healthy human colon single-cell data, CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent from LGR5-positive stem cells, increased in transit-amplifying cells and were most enriched in enterocytes, whereas CASP4 was more uniform. In situ hybridization showed CASP5a and CASP5b more luminally, while CASP5c localized deeper in crypts, overlapping strongly with MKI67-positive transit-amplifying zones and more with AXIN2 than the other isoforms. Immunohistochemistry, CIBERSORTx and pseudotime analyses supported CASP5C enrichment in proliferative early-lineage states and CASP5A/B association with mature or differentiating epithelial states.

Endogenous perturbation supported a competitive isoform model. During organoid differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b increased later with mature epithelial markers. Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C enhanced Wnt reporter activity in colonic and small intestinal organoids, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination by about 50% in colonic organoids, increased organoid size by about 60%, increased EdU-positive and Ki-67-positive cells, and raised ATP concentration. Concurrent CASP5c knockdown returned reporter activity toward baseline and prevented the growth effects. Thus, CASP5A and CASP5B can restrain CASP5C by competing for dishevelled binding.

## Discussion

These findings define CASP5C as a human intestinal epithelial modulator of Wnt signaling rather than a duplicate of CASP4 inflammasome function. The model is coherent across expression patterns, interaction mapping, catalytic mutants, APC cleavage assays, organoid growth measurements and tissue localization. CASP5C binds the DVL-linked signaling apparatus and cleaves APC at D556, weakening destruction-complex control over beta-catenin and amplifying downstream transcription.

This mechanism is best understood as modulation, not replacement, of canonical Wnt regulation. CASP5C appears suited to a local epithelial need: transit-amplifying cells move away from the Wnt-rich crypt base but still require Wnt output to proliferate. CASP5C enrichment in these cells, together with its induction after irradiation, suggests a role in supporting proliferative and repair-associated programs in human epithelium. Later expression of CASP5A and CASP5B in differentiating cells may help attenuate this signal by occupying DVL proteins without productive APC cleavage.

The evidence also has limits. The causal experiments rely heavily on cell lines, organoids, overexpression, knockdown and biochemical assays. Human tissue and public datasets support localization but do not prove in vivo causal function in human intestine. Mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce CASP5C Wnt activity in the tested systems, limiting direct mouse-to-human inference. APC D556 is strongly supported as a primary cleavage site, but additional CASP5C sites are not excluded.

Disease observations remain hypothesis-generating. CASP5 and CASP4 expression increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining their cell-type distributions. CASP5 mutations occurred in about 3% of colon or rectal adenocarcinomas, tended to co-occur with other Wnt pathway mutations, and were mutually exclusive with APC mutations in the reported analysis. These associations suggest possible relevance to inflammatory or neoplastic epithelial states, but they do not establish causation or validate CASP5C as a therapeutic target.

## Conclusion

CASP5C is a CARD-less human CASP5 isoform with a non-inflammasome role in intestinal epithelial homeostasis. By binding dishevelled proteins and catalytically cleaving APC at D556, CASP5C disrupts the beta-catenin destruction complex, reduces beta-catenin ubiquitination and amplifies Wnt transcriptional output. This activity supports proliferation and growth in human intestinal organoids and is enriched in transit-amplifying cells. CASP5A and CASP5B do not share this Wnt-amplifying function and instead can restrain CASP5C through competition for dishevelled binding. Together, the results expand the functional range of inflammatory caspase-family proteins and identify CASP5C as a human epithelial amplifier of Wnt-dependent renewal and repair.

CASP5C does not replace canonical Wnt control. Instead, it modulates pathway strength by acting near the destruction complex.

The isoform logic explains how CASP5 activity may be tuned across the crypt. CASP5C lacks the CARD region, displays higher enzymatic activity, binds DVL through its catalytic domain, and cleaves APC at D556. Human tissue and public datasets support localization and association, not direct in vivo causality in human intestine. These observations suggest disease-relevant questions, but they do not show that CASP5C causes inflammatory bowel disease or colorectal cancer, and they do not validate CASP5C as a therapeutic target.

## Conclusion

CASP5C defines a non-inflammasome role for human CASP5 in intestinal epithelial biology. By lacking the CARD region, retaining high catalytic activity, binding DVL proteins, and cleaving APC at D556, CASP5C weakens beta-catenin destruction and amplifies Wnt-dependent transcription. This activity supports organoid growth and proliferation and is enriched in transit-amplifying cells, where local Wnt amplification may help sustain epithelial renewal and repair. CASP5A and CASP5B restrain this activity by competing for DVL binding, suggesting that CASP5 isoform balance tunes Wnt output across epithelial differentiation.

CASP5-associated proteins connected the isoforms to Wnt signalling.
BioID2 proximity labelling in human colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2,
and DVL3 among leading CASP5-associated proteins, and Wnt-related processes
were enriched among interactors.
Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed endogenous interactions between CASP5 isoforms
and DVL proteins.
AXIN1 and beta-catenin were present in CASP5-DVL2 complexes.
Domain mapping implicated the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain,
although the data allow either direct or indirect association.

CASP5C uniquely activated Wnt signalling.
In HEK293T and HCT116 cells lacking endogenous CASP5, CASP5C increased
TOPFlash activity with or without WNT3A.
CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1, and CASP4 did not produce the same effect.
CASP5C increased AXIN2 and TCF7 expression and promoted nuclear beta-catenin
accumulation.
Co-expression of CASP5A or CASP5B reduced CASP5C-driven reporter activity and
competed with CASP5C for DVL2 interaction.
Tested CARD-less mouse caspase 11 isoforms did not regulate Wnt in MODE-K or
HEK293T cells, consistent with a primate CASP5C specialisation.

Primary human organoids showed the same isoform specificity.
CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity in colonic
and small intestinal organoids.
It caused cytosolic and nuclear beta-catenin accumulation and increased
Wnt-responsive and proliferation-associated markers.
In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased AXIN2 by about 3-fold, TCF7 by about
1.4-fold, MYC by about 1.3-fold, LGR5 by about 1.2-fold, and MKI67 by about
2.4-fold.
It reduced ALPI and MUC2 by about 57% and 53%, respectively.
CASP5C-expressing colonic organoids had more viable cells, were about 40%
larger, contained more Ki-67-positive and EdU-positive cells, and had fewer
CK20-positive enterocytes.
After irradiation, CASP5c transcript increased about 42.4-fold within 24
hours, and CASP5C-expressing organoids rapidly increased LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7,
and MYC.

Mechanistic assays linked CASP5C to the destruction complex.
After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phosphorylated
beta-catenin, APC, and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes.
It also reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination.
CASP5C(C173A) lost the ability to activate Wnt reporters, redistribute
beta-catenin, or induce Wnt-responsive genes.
CASP5C had higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and CARD deletion
from CASP5A or CASP5B increased activity and Wnt reporter signalling.
These results indicate that absence of the CARD enables CASP5C enzymatic
activity.

APC was the critical substrate.
Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites in APC clustered mainly in the N-terminal
region.
CASP5C, but not CASP5A, CASP5B, or CASP5C(C173A), produced APC cleavage
products in cells.
Recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro.
Mutation of APC D556 to alanine abolished the major CASP5C-mediated cleavage
products, identifying D556 as a primary cleavage site.
In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the
destruction complex and increase Wnt reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A)
reduced this effect.
APC fragments mimicking cleavage products decreased beta-catenin in the
destruction complex and increased Wnt reporter activity.
Additional APC cleavage sites may also contribute.

CASP5C localised to the transit-amplifying compartment.
In healthy human colon single-cell data, CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent
from LGR5-positive stem cells, increased in transit-amplifying cells, and were
most enriched in enterocytes.
CASP4 was more uniform across epithelial cell types.
In situ hybridisation placed CASP5a and CASP5b more luminally, whereas CASP5c
localised deeper in crypts and overlapped with MKI67-positive and AXIN2-positive
zones.
Immunohistochemistry and pseudotime analyses supported CASP5C enrichment with
proliferative crypt states, while CASP5A and CASP5B associated more with
differentiated epithelial states.

Endogenous knockdown experiments supported a competitive model.
During colonic organoid differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked
early with MKI67, while CASP5a and CASP5b increased later.
Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C enhanced Wnt reporter
activity in colonic and small intestinal organoids.
Concurrent CASP5c knockdown restored reporter activity towards baseline.
The same isoform-selective knockdown reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination by
about 50%, increased organoid size by about 60%, increased EdU-positive and
Ki-67-positive cells, and increased ATP concentration.
Simultaneous CASP5c knockdown prevented these growth effects.

## Discussion

These findings assign CASP5 a function distinct from CASP4-mediated
noncanonical inflammasome activation.
CASP4 retained the tested bacterial inflammasome role in epithelial cells and
macrophages, whereas CASP5 showed specialised epithelial expression and an
isoform-specific effect on Wnt signalling.
This division explains why CASP5 function could not be inferred from sequence
similarity alone.

The mechanism connects CASP5C enzymatic activity to a central Wnt control
node.
CASP5C binds DVL proteins, associates with destruction-complex components, and
cleaves APC at D556.
This reduces beta-catenin ubiquitination and increases beta-catenin-dependent
transcription.
The CASP5C(C173A) and APC(D556A) experiments show that catalytic activity and
APC cleavage are required for the strongest pathway effects in the tested
systems.

The cell-state distribution gives the model biological coherence.
Transit-amplifying cells sit between the Wnt-rich crypt base and differentiated
luminal cells.
CASP5C may help sustain beta-catenin signalling during this transition without
serving as the main driver of all intestinal Wnt activity.
CASP5A and CASP5B provide an opposing layer by competing for DVL binding and
restraining CASP5C.

Disease-associated observations are suggestive but not definitive.
CASP5 and CASP4 expression increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while
retaining their cell-type distributions.
Colon or rectal adenocarcinomas carried CASP5 mutations in about 3% of cases,
and CASP5 mutations showed associations with Wnt pathway alterations.
In microsatellite instability-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were more
common than APC mutations in the analysed comparison.
These findings raise hypotheses about inflammatory and neoplastic contexts but
do not establish causality, patient outcomes, or therapeutic utility.

Several caveats remain.
The evidence is mechanistically strong but relies heavily on cell-line,
organoid, overexpression, knockdown, and biochemical assays.
Human tissue and public datasets support localisation and association, not
in vivo causality.
Mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce CASP5C Wnt activity in the tested systems,
limiting direct mouse-to-human inference.
APC D556 is strongly supported as a primary cleavage site, but other CASP5C
cleavage sites may also contribute.

## Conclusion

CASP5C defines a non-inflammasome function for CASP5 in human intestinal
epithelial biology.
By lacking the CARD region, displaying higher catalytic activity, binding DVL
proteins, and cleaving APC at D556, CASP5C amplifies Wnt/beta-catenin
signalling in transit-amplifying cells.
This activity supports organoid growth, proliferation, and repair-associated
responses while remaining restrained by CASP5A and CASP5B through competition
for DVL binding.
The findings broaden the functional scope of inflammatory caspase-family
proteins and identify CASP5C as a local modulator of epithelial renewal.

## Article_A

# Article_A

# A CARD-less CASP5 isoform amplifies epithelial Wnt signalling

## Abstract

Human CASP5 resembles CASP4, the main human mediator of noncanonical
inflammasome activation, but its function has remained unclear.
We investigated whether CASP5 has a separate role in human intestinal epithelial
cells, where Wnt/beta-catenin signalling maintains renewal.
CASP4, not CASP5, mediated the tested bacterial inflammasome responses in
organoid-derived epithelial cells and monocyte-derived macrophages.
Human organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C, with CASP5C lacking the
N-terminal CARD.
Proteomic and biochemical assays showed that CASP5 isoforms bind dishevelled
proteins, Wnt signalling hubs that connect receptor activity to downstream
beta-catenin regulation.
Only CASP5C amplified beta-catenin/TCF-dependent Wnt transcription.
CASP5C required catalytic activity, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination, cleaved
APC at D556 and disrupted the beta-catenin destruction complex.
In organoids, CASP5C promoted proliferation, growth and recovery after
irradiation.
Tissue and dataset analyses placed CASP5C in transit-amplifying cells.
These findings support a model in which CASP5C provides local Wnt amplification,
while CASP5A and CASP5B restrain it by competing for dishevelled binding.

## Introduction

Human CASP4 and CASP5 are orthologues of mouse caspase 11, yet their functional
relationship is unequal.
CASP4 is closer to mouse caspase 11 and retains the ancestral role in
noncanonical inflammasome activation after cytosolic lipopolysaccharide
detection.
CASP5 arose after primate-specific gene duplication and shares high sequence
similarity with CASP4.
Despite this, its biological contribution has remained unclear.

The human intestine provides a relevant setting for resolving this question.
CASP5 transcripts are concentrated mainly in the intestinal tract and appendix,
and single-cell data place CASP5 expression largely in intestinal epithelial
cells and monocytes or macrophages.
At the same time, epithelial renewal depends on graded Wnt/beta-catenin
signalling along the crypt.
Wnt activity is highest near the crypt base, but proliferative progeny must
continue dividing as they move away from that Wnt-rich niche.
These transit-amplifying cells sit between stem cells and differentiated
enterocytes.
Mechanisms that tune Wnt signalling in this compartment are therefore central to
epithelial homeostasis and repair.

Canonical Wnt signalling is controlled by the beta-catenin destruction complex,
a multiprotein assembly that includes AXIN1 and APC.
APC is a scaffold protein and tumour suppressor that helps promote
beta-catenin phosphorylation and ubiquitination.
When Wnt signalling is active, dishevelled proteins, also called DVL proteins,
link receptor activation to downstream regulation of this destruction complex.
Stabilised beta-catenin enters the nucleus and drives TCF-dependent
transcription, which is measured by the TOPFlash reporter.

We asked whether CASP5 has a non-inflammasome function in human intestinal
epithelial cells, which isoform mediates that function and how it connects to
Wnt pathway regulation.
The evidence identifies CASP5C, a CARD-less CASP5 isoform, as a Wnt amplifier
that acts through dishevelled binding and APC cleavage.

## Methods

The study combined sequence analysis, public datasets, human organoids,
cell-line assays, biochemical reconstitution and tissue analysis.
Sequence and phylogenetic comparisons assessed human CASP4, human CASP5 and
mouse caspase 11.
Human Protein Atlas and single-cell datasets compared tissue and cell-type
expression.
Human colonic and small intestinal organoids modelled epithelial CASP5 function,
with monocyte-derived macrophages as an inflammatory comparator.
HEK293T, HCT116, MODE-K and APC-knockout HEK293T cells supported reporter,
overexpression, knockdown, cleavage and rescue experiments.
TOPFlash and FOPFlash measured Wnt transcriptional output.
Beta-catenin localisation, Wnt-responsive gene expression, ubiquitination and
organoid growth assays measured pathway activity.
BioID2 labelling, mass spectrometry, co-immunoprecipitation, domain mapping and
immunoblotting identified CASP5-associated complexes.
Mechanistic tests used CASP5C(C173A), CARD-deleted CASP5 constructs, APC
fragments, APC(D556A) and recombinant CASP5C.
Irradiated human colonic organoids modelled epithelial injury and recovery.
Human tissue and disease datasets assessed localisation and associations.

## Results

CASP4 accounted for the tested noncanonical inflammasome responses.
Although CASP4 and CASP5 share sequence similarity and can bind
lipopolysaccharide through CARD regions, CASP4 was more similar to mouse caspase
11.
In human intestinal epithelial cells derived from colonic organoids, clearance
of intracellular Salmonella Typhimurium and Shigella flexneri depended on
CASP4 rather than CASP5.
The same pattern was observed in monocyte-derived macrophages, where live
bacteria activated noncanonical inflammasome responses and bacterial clearance
through CASP4.
Thus, CASP5 did not explain the tested inflammasome phenotypes, supporting a
search for another function.

Expression analyses pointed to the intestinal epithelium as the main setting for
CASP5 biology.
CASP4 transcripts were broadly distributed, whereas CASP5 transcripts were
concentrated in the intestinal tract and appendix.
Healthy colonic epithelial fractions and colonic organoids contained more CASP5
transcript than monocyte-derived macrophages.
Human organoids expressed three isoforms: CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C.
CASP5A is about 49.7 kDa, CASP5B about 43.2 kDa and CASP5C about 33.3 kDa.
CASP5B lacks part of the CASP5A CARD, whereas CASP5C lacks the entire CARD.
The CARD is a caspase activation and recruitment domain at the N terminus of
CASP5A and CASP5B.

Proteomic analysis connected CASP5 to Wnt signalling.
BioID2 proximity labelling in human colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2 and
DVL3 among leading CASP5-associated proteins.
Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed that endogenous CASP5 isoforms interact with
DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3 in colonic organoids.
AXIN1 and beta-catenin were present in CASP5-DVL2 complexes.
Domain mapping linked this interaction to the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5
catalytic domain.

Functional assays separated the three CASP5 isoforms.
In HEK293T and HCT116 cells lacking endogenous CASP5, CASP5C increased TOPFlash
activity with or without WNT3A.
CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 and CASP4 did not show the same activity.
CASP5C increased expression of Wnt-responsive genes including AXIN2 and TCF7
and promoted nuclear beta-catenin accumulation.
Co-expression of CASP5A or CASP5B reduced CASP5C-mediated reporter activation
and competed with CASP5C for DVL2 interaction.
Tested CARD-less mouse caspase 11 isoforms did not regulate Wnt activity,
suggesting that CASP5C acquired a specialised function after primate CASP5
duplication.

CASP5C also amplified Wnt signalling in primary human organoids.
In colonic and small intestinal organoids, CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B,
increased Wnt reporter activity.
CASP5C increased cytosolic and nuclear beta-catenin and raised Wnt-responsive
and stem or proliferation-associated markers.
In colonic organoids, CASP5C increased AXIN2 about 3-fold and MKI67 about
2.4-fold, with smaller increases in TCF7, MYC and LGR5.
It reduced ALPI and MUC2 and produced larger, more proliferative organoids with
fewer CK20-positive enterocytes.
After irradiation, CASP5c transcript increased about 42.4-fold within 24 hours,
and CASP5C-expressing organoids showed rapid induction of repair-associated Wnt
targets.

The mechanism required CASP5C enzymatic activity.
CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phosphorylated beta-catenin, APC and
beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes after Wnt stimulation.
It reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination.
The catalytic mutant CASP5C(C173A) failed to promote Wnt reporter activity,
beta-catenin localisation and Wnt-responsive gene expression.
CASP5C showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and deleting the
CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased activity and Wnt reporter signalling.
These results indicate that absence of the inhibitory CARD permits CASP5C
enzymatic activity.

APC was the key substrate linking CASP5C activity to the destruction complex.
CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, produced APC cleavage products in cells, and
CASP5C(C173A) did not.
Mutation of APC D556 to alanine abolished the major CASP5C-mediated cleavage
products, establishing D556 as a primary cleavage site.
In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, reintroduced wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to
disrupt the destruction complex and increase TOPFlash activity.
APC(D556A) reduced this effect.
APC fragments mimicking cleavage products decreased beta-catenin in the
destruction complex and increased Wnt reporter activity, although additional APC
cleavage sites may also contribute.

Cell-type analyses placed CASP5C in a proliferative epithelial compartment.
In situ hybridisation showed that CASP5a and CASP5b were more luminal, whereas
CASP5c localised deeper in crypts and overlapped strongly with MKI67-positive
transit-amplifying zones.
CIBERSORTx and pseudotime analyses similarly associated CASP5c with
early-lineage or transit-amplifying states and CASP5a and CASP5b with mature or
differentiating epithelial states.

Endogenous isoform perturbation supported a competitive model.
During organoid differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with
MKI67, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b increased later with differentiation markers.
Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C enhanced Wnt reporter
activity, reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination by about 50% in colonic organoids
and increased organoid growth.
Concurrent CASP5c knockdown returned reporter activity towards baseline and
prevented these growth effects.
Together, the data suggest that high CASP5C in transit-amplifying cells favours
DVL2 binding and Wnt amplification, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B compete for DVL2
in more differentiated cells and restrain CASP5C.

## Discussion

These findings assign CASP5 a role in human intestinal epithelial biology that
is distinct from CASP4-mediated noncanonical inflammasome activation.
The central distinction is isoform-specific: CASP5C lacks the CARD region, shows
greater enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and depends on catalytic
activity to amplify Wnt signalling.
By binding dishevelled proteins and cleaving APC at D556, CASP5C reduces
destruction-complex integrity, lowers beta-catenin ubiquitination and increases
beta-catenin-dependent transcription.

The cell-type localisation helps explain why this mechanism may be useful in
epithelial homeostasis.
Transit-amplifying cells proliferate after leaving the crypt-base stem-cell
niche, where Wnt ligands are most abundant.
CASP5C could help sustain Wnt responsiveness in this intermediate zone without
replacing canonical Wnt regulation.
The later rise of CASP5A and CASP5B during differentiation provides a possible
brake, because these isoforms bind dishevelled but do not activate Wnt in the
same way.
Competition among isoforms may therefore tune the duration of Wnt amplification
as epithelial cells move towards differentiated states.

The disease-associated observations are suggestive but not definitive.
CASP5 and CASP4 expression increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while
retaining their cell-type distributions.
Colorectal cancer datasets showed CASP5 mutations in a minority of colon or
rectal adenocarcinomas and in many microsatellite instability-high tumours.
These patterns motivate further study, but they do not establish that CASP5C
causes inflammatory bowel disease or colorectal cancer.
They also do not validate CASP5C as a therapeutic target.

Several caveats shape the interpretation.
The mechanistic evidence is strong across biochemical, cellular and organoid
systems, but it relies heavily on overexpression, knockdown and in vitro assays.
Human tissue and public datasets support localisation and associations, yet they
do not prove causal function in the human intestine in vivo.
Mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce CASP5C Wnt activity in the tested systems,
which limits direct mouse-to-human inference.
Finally, APC D556 is strongly supported as a primary CASP5C cleavage site, but
other CASP5C cleavage sites may also contribute.

## Conclusion

CASP5C is a CARD-less human CASP5 isoform that amplifies Wnt/beta-catenin
signalling in intestinal epithelial systems.
It acts outside the tested CASP4-dependent noncanonical inflammasome pathway by
binding dishevelled proteins, cleaving APC at D556, weakening the
beta-catenin destruction complex and reducing beta-catenin ubiquitination.
This activity promotes organoid proliferation and repair-associated responses
and is enriched in transit-amplifying cells.
CASP5A and CASP5B provide an opposing influence by competing for dishevelled
binding.
The resulting model places CASP5C as a human epithelial Wnt amplifier that helps
link inflammatory caspase evolution to intestinal homeostasis.
