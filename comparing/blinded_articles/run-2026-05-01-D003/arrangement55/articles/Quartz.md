# Caspase 5c amplifies epithelial Wnt signaling through APC cleavage in intestinal homeostasis models

## Abstract

Inflammatory caspases are best known for innate immune detection and pyroptotic signaling, but the function of human caspase 5 (CASP5) has remained incompletely defined. Although CASP5 is related to CASP4 and mouse caspase 11, experiments in human intestinal epithelial cells and macrophages indicated that CASP4, rather than CASP5, was the principal mediator of the tested noncanonical inflammasome response to cytosolic lipopolysaccharide and bacteria. Here, evidence from human intestinal organoids and cell-based systems supports a distinct epithelial role for the CARD-less isoform CASP5C. CASP5 was enriched in intestinal tissues and epithelial compartments, where organoids produced CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C. Proximity labelling and co-immunoprecipitation linked CASP5 isoforms to Dishevelled proteins. Functionally, CASP5C, but not CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 or CASP4, increased Wnt reporter activity, promoted beta-catenin redistribution, induced Wnt target genes and enhanced proliferative features of colonic organoids. Mechanistically, CASP5C required catalytic activity and cleaved the Wnt destruction-complex scaffold APC. These findings identify CASP5C as an epithelial amplifier of Wnt signaling and suggest that alternative CASP5 isoforms can tune intestinal lineage and repair-associated programs.

## Introduction

The intestinal epithelium must sustain rapid renewal while preserving barrier integrity during microbial exposure, inflammation and injury. Wnt/beta-catenin signaling is central to this balance, supporting development, repair, stem-cell renewal and differentiation decisions. Dishevelled proteins connect receptor activation to beta-catenin control, whereas AXIN1, APC and beta-TrCP participate in destruction-complex regulation. APC is therefore both a core pathway component and a tumor suppressor whose disruption has major consequences for epithelial growth.

Inflammatory caspases provide another layer of host defense and tissue response. Human CASP4 and CASP5 are related to mouse caspase 11 and have often been considered in the context of noncanonical inflammasome signaling. However, sequence and functional analyses indicated that human CASP4 more closely resembles mouse caspase 11 and serves as the dominant actor in the tested responses to cytosolic lipopolysaccharide and intracellular bacteria. CASP5 did not carry out this role in the examined human intestinal epithelial cells and macrophages, raising the possibility that CASP5 has a different function, particularly in tissues where its expression is concentrated.

The intestine emerged as a relevant setting for this question. CASP5 expression was enriched in the intestinal tract and appendix, with signal in epithelial cells and in some monocyte and macrophage populations. In colonic organoids, CASP5 expression exceeded CASP4, CASP1 and PYCARD relative to macrophages. Human colon and small-intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C, with approximate sizes of 49.7, 43.2 and 33.3 kDa. CASP5B lacks part of the CARD region, and CASP5C lacks the CARD entirely. Knockdown experiments indicated that CASP5C was not simply a cleavage product of CASP5A or CASP5B.

## Methods

The study integrated expression analysis, human intestinal organoid models, cell-based reporter assays, proximity labelling, immunoprecipitation, mutagenesis and biochemical cleavage assays. CASP5 expression was assessed across intestinal and immune cell contexts. Induction conditions included Salmonella infection, IFN-beta or IFN-gamma stimulation, and epithelial exposure to lipopolysaccharide alone.

CASP5 protein neighborhoods were examined in human colonic organoids using BioID2 proximity labelling. Candidate interactions were evaluated by co-immunoprecipitation with DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3, with additional analysis of AXIN1- and DVL2-containing complexes. Wnt pathway activity was measured using TOPFlash assays in HEK293T and HCT116 cells and in organoids, with or without WNT3A. Isoform-specific effects were tested by expressing CASP5A, CASP5B or CASP5C and comparing them with CASP1, CASP4 and CARD-less mouse caspase 11 forms.

Organoid phenotypes were assessed through viability, size, Ki-67, EdU incorporation, differentiation markers and transcriptional readouts including AXIN2, TCF7, MYC, LGR5 and MKI67. Wnt withdrawal and restoration experiments tested persistence and re-entry into the cell cycle. Irradiated colonic organoids were used to model injury-associated responses.

Mechanistic experiments tested dependence on catalytic activity using CASP5C(C173A), compared enzymatic activity among isoforms, and evaluated the consequences of removing CARD regions from CASP5A and CASP5B. APC cleavage was examined using predicted CASP5 cleavage sites, APC mutants, APC knockout HEK293T cells reconstituted with wild-type or mutant APC, and recombinant CASP5C cleavage of truncated APC in vitro. Single-cell and computational analyses, including pseudotime and CIBERSORTx, were used to relate CASP5 isoforms to epithelial lineage states.

## Results

CASP5 showed an epithelial distribution consistent with a specialized intestinal role. In organoids, CASP5 increased after Salmonella exposure and after IFN-beta or IFN-gamma stimulation. Lipopolysaccharide alone did not markedly raise epithelial CASP5, consistent with low epithelial TLR4 expression. This induction pattern separated CASP5 from the dominant cytosolic lipopolysaccharide-response role assigned to CASP4.

Proximity labelling in human colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3 near CASP5, and Wnt-related terms were enriched among CASP5-associated proteins. Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed association of CASP5 isoforms with DVL family members. DVL2 complexes also contained AXIN1 and beta-catenin, placing CASP5-associated proteins close to the beta-catenin destruction machinery. Mapping implicated the DVL DEP domain and CASP5 catalytic domain, although direct binding was not fully proven.

Functional experiments identified CASP5C as the Wnt-active isoform. In HEK293T and HCT116 cells, CASP5C increased TOPFlash reporter activity both with and without WNT3A. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 and CASP4 did not produce the same effect. CASP5C also increased AXIN2 and TCF7 expression and promoted redistribution of beta-catenin into nuclear and cytosolic fractions. CASP5A and CASP5B could compete with CASP5C for DVL2 and reduce this response. CARD-less mouse caspase 11 variants did not reproduce the reporter effect, suggesting that CASP5C activity is not explained solely by absence of a CARD.

The same isoform specificity was observed in intestinal organoids. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity. CASP5C-expressing organoids contained more viable cells, were approximately 40% larger, had more Ki-67- and EdU-positive cells, and showed fewer CK20-positive enterocytes. Transcriptionally, CASP5C increased Wnt and proliferation-associated markers, including AXIN2 by about threefold, TCF7 by 1.4-fold, MYC by 1.3-fold, LGR5 by 1.2-fold and MKI67 by 2.4-fold. Differentiation-associated markers were reduced, with ALPI and MUC2 decreasing by about 57% and 53%, respectively.

CASP5C also altered organoid behavior during Wnt perturbation and injury-associated modeling. CASP5C-expressing organoids survived longer after Wnt withdrawal and re-entered the cell cycle after Wnt restoration. In irradiated human colonic organoids, CASP5c transcript increased 42.4-fold within 24 h, exceeding CASP5a and CASP5b, which rose 5.6-fold and 6.6-fold. LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7 and MYC increased faster in CASP5C-expressing organoids. These findings are consistent with epithelial regenerative programs in vitro, while not establishing a repair effect in the human body.

Mechanistic experiments linked CASP5C activity to disruption of the beta-catenin destruction complex. After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phospho-beta-catenin, APC and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes. It also reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. The catalytic mutant CASP5C(C173A) lost reporter activation and beta-catenin effects, showing that enzymatic activity was required. CASP5C had higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and removing the CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased activity and Wnt reporter signaling.

APC emerged as a key CASP5C substrate. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites in APC were concentrated mostly in the N-terminal region. CASP5C generated APC cleavage products, whereas CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C(C173A) did not. Recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro, supporting a direct enzymatic relationship. Mutation of APC D556 removed major cleavage products. In APC knockout HEK293T cells, reintroduction of wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the destruction complex and increase Wnt reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A) weakened this effect. APC fragments resembling cleavage products reduced beta-catenin in destruction-complex fractions and increased reporter activity. D556 therefore appears to be a primary functional site, although additional APC cleavage sites may also contribute.

Single-cell and lineage analyses suggested that CASP5 isoforms occupy different epithelial states. CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent from LGR5-positive stem cells in one analysis, higher in transit-amplifying cells and highest in enterocytes. However, CASP5c was detected deeper in crypts and overlapped more with MKI67 and AXIN2 than CASP5a or CASP5b. CASP5A and CASP5B were more luminal and associated with differentiated, CK20-positive compartments. Pseudotime and CIBERSORTx analyses supported CASP5c in early-lineage or transit-amplifying states, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b aligned with mature or differentiating states.

Isoform knockdown experiments further supported a competition model. Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while maintaining CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity, organoid size, ATP content, EdU incorporation and Ki-67 positivity. When CASP5c was also knocked down, these effects were reduced. Thus, CASP5A and CASP5B can restrain CASP5C activity, likely in part through competition for DVL2-containing complexes.

## Discussion

These findings define a noncanonical epithelial function for CASP5 that is distinct from inflammasome-like activities of related caspases. CASP5C, a CARD-less CASP5 isoform, amplified Wnt/beta-catenin signaling in cell lines and human intestinal organoids. Its activity required the catalytic cysteine, correlated with beta-catenin redistribution and reduced ubiquitination, and involved cleavage of APC. The data support a model in which CASP5C associates with DVL-containing complexes and weakens destruction-complex function.

The isoform specificity is central. CASP5C promoted Wnt signaling, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B did not. Instead, CASP5A and CASP5B could oppose CASP5C-driven signaling by competing for DVL2. Their association with differentiated epithelial states, compared with the earlier transit-amplifying pattern of CASP5c, suggests that alternative CASP5 isoforms may tune the transition between proliferative and differentiating compartments.

The study also refines how inflammatory caspase family members are viewed in human epithelia. CASP4 remained the closer functional counterpart of mouse caspase 11 in the tested noncanonical inflammasome response, while CASP5C acted through a Wnt-associated mechanism. The failure of CARD-less mouse caspase 11 forms to mimic CASP5C suggests that the effect is not a simple consequence of domain deletion.

Disease-associated observations provide context but not proof of pathogenesis. CASP5 and CASP4 were increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while maintaining cell-type patterns. CASP5 mutations occurred in approximately 3% of colon or rectal cancers, similar to AXIN1, and tended to co-occur with Wnt-pathway mutations while being mutually exclusive with APC mutations. In microsatellite instability-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were reported more often than APC mutations in one comparison. These associations raise hypotheses but do not establish CASP5C as a cause of disease or a validated therapeutic target.

In summary, CASP5C links an inflammatory caspase-family protein to Wnt pathway amplification through APC cleavage. By distinguishing CASP5C from CASP5A and CASP5B, the work provides a framework in which CASP5 isoform balance modulates epithelial proliferation, differentiation and injury-associated organoid responses. Future studies should determine how CASP5C is regulated in vivo, whether additional APC cleavage sites contribute to pathway control, and how CASP5 isoform expression interacts with human intestinal disease states.
