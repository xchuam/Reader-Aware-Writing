# Caspase 5c amplifies Wnt signalling through APC cleavage in human intestinal epithelial models

## Abstract

Human CASP5 is an inflammatory caspase-family protease whose role has been less clear than that of CASP4 or mouse caspase 11. The findings summarized here support a distinct epithelial function for CASP5C, a CARD-less CASP5 isoform, in Wnt/beta-catenin signalling. CASP5 was enriched in intestinal tissues and epithelial organoids, but did not act as the principal tested mediator of noncanonical inflammasome responses to cytosolic LPS or bacteria. Instead, proximity labelling and co-immunoprecipitation placed CASP5 isoforms near DVL proteins, and functional assays showed that CASP5C, but not CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 or CASP4, increased Wnt reporter activity and beta-catenin signalling. In human intestinal organoids, CASP5C promoted larger, more proliferative organoids and helped maintain Wnt-responsive growth after Wnt withdrawal or irradiation-like stress. Mechanistically, CASP5C required catalytic activity and generated APC cleavage products, with D556 acting as a major but not necessarily exclusive cleavage site. CASP5A and CASP5B could restrain this effect by competing for DVL2. These data identify CASP5C as an isoform-specific amplifier of Wnt signalling in tested human intestinal epithelial models, while leaving disease causality and therapeutic relevance unresolved.

## Introduction

Inflammatory caspases are usually interpreted through innate immune sensing, inflammasome signalling and pyroptotic cell death. CASP4 fits this framework in human cells: it resembles mouse caspase 11 by sequence and phylogeny and was the main actor in the tested noncanonical inflammasome response to cytosolic LPS and bacteria. CASP5, although related, did not perform that role in human intestinal epithelial cells and macrophages. This divergence raised the possibility that CASP5 has a different function, particularly in the intestine, where its expression is concentrated.

The intestinal epithelium is a relevant setting because epithelial renewal depends on tightly controlled Wnt/beta-catenin signalling. Wnt activity supports intestinal development, repair, stem-cell renewal and differentiation. DVL proteins connect receptor-proximal Wnt events to downstream beta-catenin control, whereas APC acts as a scaffold and tumour suppressor in the beta-catenin destruction complex. A caspase input into this pathway would be important only if it could be assigned to a specific isoform and mechanism rather than to CASP5 as a single gene-level signal.

The evidence supports such an isoform-specific model. Human colon and small-intestinal organoids expressed CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C, with approximate sizes of 49.7, 43.2 and 33.3 kDa. CASP5B lacks part of the CARD region, and CASP5C lacks the CARD entirely. siRNA experiments indicated that CASP5C was not simply a cleavage product of the longer isoforms. Across cellular, organoid and biochemical assays, CASP5C amplified Wnt/beta-catenin signalling, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B did not and could instead restrain CASP5C.

## Results

CASP5 expression patterns first pointed toward an epithelial role. CASP5 was concentrated in the intestinal tract and appendix and appeared in epithelial cells as well as selected monocyte and macrophage populations. In colonic organoids, CASP5 was higher than CASP4, CASP1 and PYCARD compared with macrophages, whereas CASP4 was broader. CASP5 increased after Salmonella infection and after IFN-beta or IFN-gamma exposure in organoids, but LPS alone had little effect in epithelium, consistent with low epithelial TLR4 expression.

Protein-interaction experiments connected CASP5 to the Wnt pathway. BioID2 in human colonic organoids identified DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3 near CASP5, and Wnt-related terms appeared in the interactor analysis. CASP5 isoforms co-immunoprecipitated with DVL family proteins; DVL2 complexes also contained AXIN1 and beta-catenin. Mapping implicated the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic domain. These data do not prove a direct binary interaction, but they place CASP5 isoforms near the machinery that controls beta-catenin fate.

Functional assays showed that the Wnt effect belonged specifically to CASP5C. In TOPFlash assays in HEK293T and HCT116 cells, CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity with or without WNT3A. CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 and CASP4 did not. CASP5C also increased AXIN2 and TCF7 and moved beta-catenin into nuclear and cytosolic fractions. CASP5A and CASP5B could compete with CASP5C for DVL2 and reduce this response. CARD-less mouse caspase 11 forms did not reproduce the effect, suggesting a possible primate CASP5 specialization without requiring a broad evolutionary claim.

The same isoform logic held in human intestinal organoids. CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity. CASP5C-expressing organoids contained more viable cells, were about 40% larger, showed more Ki-67 and EdU positivity and contained fewer CK20-positive enterocytes. Wnt and proliferation markers increased, including AXIN2 by about threefold, TCF7 by 1.4-fold, MYC by 1.3-fold, LGR5 by 1.2-fold and MKI67 by 2.4-fold. Differentiation markers moved in the opposite direction: ALPI and MUC2 fell by about 57% and 53%. Thus, CASP5C biased organoids toward a proliferative Wnt-responsive state in vitro.

Stress and withdrawal experiments further linked CASP5C to epithelial growth capacity. CASP5C-expressing organoids grew longer without Wnt and re-entered the cell cycle after Wnt was restored. In irradiated human colonic organoids, CASP5c transcript increased 42.4-fold within 24 h, compared with 5.6-fold for CASP5a and 6.6-fold for CASP5b. LGR5, OLFM4, TCF7 and MYC rose faster in CASP5C-expressing organoids after irradiation. These results support a model in which CASP5C helps organoids maintain or regain Wnt-responsive proliferation after stress, but they do not prove the same process drives repair in the human body.

Mechanistic experiments placed CASP5C upstream of beta-catenin destruction-complex remodeling. After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin, phospho-beta-catenin, APC and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes and reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination. The catalytic mutant CASP5C(C173A) lost reporter activation and beta-catenin effects. CASP5C also showed higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and removing the CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased activity and Wnt reporter signalling. These observations indicate that CASP5C catalytic activity, enabled in part by its CARD-less structure, is required for Wnt amplification.

APC cleavage provides the clearest substrate-level explanation. Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites were mostly N-terminal in APC. CASP5C generated APC cleavage products, whereas CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C(C173A) did not, and recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro. Mutation of APC D556 removed the major cleavage products, identifying D556 as a primary site while leaving room for additional sites. In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the complex and increase Wnt reporter activity, whereas APC(D556A) weakened the effect. APC fragments resembling cleavage products reduced beta-catenin in destruction-complex fractions and increased reporter activity.

Cell-state analyses refined where CASP5C may act within the epithelium. Total CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent from LGR5-positive stem cells, higher in transit-amplifying cells and highest in enterocytes in one single-cell analysis. Yet CASP5c showed deeper crypt localization and overlapped more with MKI67 and AXIN2 than CASP5a or CASP5b, which were more luminal and associated with CK20-positive or differentiated states. Pseudotime and CIBERSORTx supported CASP5c enrichment in early-lineage or transit-amplifying states, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b aligned with mature or differentiating states. During differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67; CASP5a and CASP5b rose later with differentiation markers.

Perturbing isoform balance supported a competition model. Knocking down CASP5a and CASP5b while retaining CASP5C increased Wnt reporter activity, organoid size, ATP readouts and EdU and Ki-67 positivity. When CASP5c was also knocked down, these effects declined. This pattern fits the DVL2 data: CASP5A and CASP5B can restrain CASP5C-dependent Wnt amplification, probably by competing within DVL2-containing complexes.

Disease-associated datasets gave context without proving causality. CASP5 and CASP4 increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining their cell-type patterns. CASP5 mutations occurred in about 3% of colon or rectal cancers in one analysis, similar to AXIN1. CASP5 mutations tended to co-occur with Wnt-pathway mutations and were mutually exclusive with APC mutations in the reported analysis. In MSI-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were reported more often than APC mutations in one comparison, about 68% versus 17%. These observations do not show that CASP5C causes ulcerative colitis, colorectal cancer or any disease phenotype, and they do not establish CASP5C as a validated drug target.

## Discussion

The central finding is that CASP5C, a CARD-less human CASP5 isoform, amplifies Wnt/beta-catenin signalling in epithelial and organoid systems through catalytic APC cleavage. The evidence is strongest because it links expression, proximity to DVL proteins, isoform-specific reporter activity, organoid proliferation, catalytic-mutant loss of function and direct APC cleavage. The boundary is equally important: the activity should not be generalized to CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP4, CASP1 or inflammatory caspases as a class.

The work also argues that CASP5 isoform balance matters. CASP5A and CASP5B were not redundant with CASP5C; they failed to activate Wnt signalling in the same way and could dampen CASP5C by competing for DVL2. This provides a plausible explanation for why CASP5c is linked to proliferative and early-lineage states, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b are associated with differentiating epithelial states.

APC cleavage connects CASP5C to the core beta-catenin destruction complex. D556 is a major supported cleavage site, but not necessarily the only relevant site, and APC may not be the only substrate affected in cells. The available data therefore support APC cleavage as a central mechanism for CASP5C-dependent Wnt amplification, while leaving the full substrate range and in vivo consequences open.

Taken together, these findings define CASP5C as an epithelial Wnt amplifier in human intestinal models rather than as a canonical noncanonical-inflammasome caspase. They provide a mechanistic basis for studying CASP5 isoforms separately in intestinal homeostasis, epithelial stress responses and Wnt-associated disease contexts, with the disease links remaining hypothesis-generating rather than causal.
