# Caspase 5c amplifies epithelial Wnt signalling through APC cleavage

## Abstract

Inflammatory caspases are best known as proteases in innate immune signalling.
Human CASP5 belongs to this family, but its specific cellular function has been
less clear than that of CASP4 or mouse caspase 11.
Here we describe evidence that a short CASP5 isoform, CASP5C, promotes Wnt
signalling in human intestinal epithelial models.
CASP5 was enriched in the intestinal tract and appendix, and human intestinal
organoids expressed three isoforms: CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C.
CASP5C lacked a caspase activation and recruitment domain and was not explained
as a cleavage product of the longer isoforms.
Interaction studies in colonic organoids identified dishevelled proteins as
CASP5-proximal partners, linking CASP5 to the Wnt pathway.
Among the tested isoforms, CASP5C increased beta-catenin reporter activity,
induced Wnt target genes and shifted beta-catenin into cytosolic and nuclear
fractions.
CASP5C also increased proliferation-associated features in human intestinal
organoids, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B restrained this activity, at least in part
by competing for dishevelled binding.
Mechanistically, CASP5C required catalytic activity and cleaved APC, a scaffold
in the beta-catenin destruction complex.
Mutation of APC residue D556 reduced the major cleavage products, although the
data do not exclude additional APC cleavage sites.
These findings support a model in which CASP5C tunes Wnt signalling
by proteolytically weakening the APC-containing destruction complex.
They also separate this epithelial function from the noncanonical inflammasome
activity attributed mainly to CASP4 in the tested human cells.

## Introduction

Inflammatory caspases connect danger sensing to cell fate and inflammation.
CASP4, CASP5 and mouse caspase 11 are related inflammatory caspases, but their
functions are not equivalent.
In the tested human intestinal epithelial cells and macrophages, CASP4 more
closely matched mouse caspase 11 in sequence, phylogenetic position and
noncanonical inflammasome activity after cytosolic lipopolysaccharide or
bacterial challenge.
CASP5 did not serve as the main mediator of that response in those experiments.
This raised a distinct question: whether CASP5 has a separate role in tissues
where it is preferentially expressed.

Expression patterns pointed to the intestine as a plausible site of such a
role.
CASP5 expression was concentrated in the intestinal tract and appendix and was
detected in epithelial cells and selected monocyte or macrophage populations.
In human colonic organoids, CASP5 was expressed at higher relative levels than
CASP4, CASP1 and PYCARD when compared with macrophages, whereas CASP4 showed a
broader distribution.
Organoids from human colon and small intestine expressed three CASP5 isoforms,
CASP5A, CASP5B and CASP5C, with approximate sizes of 49.7, 43.2 and 33.3 kDa.
CASP5B lacks part of the caspase activation and recruitment domain, and CASP5C
lacks that domain entirely.
siRNA experiments indicated that CASP5C was not simply a proteolytic product of
CASP5A or CASP5B.

The intestine is also a setting in which Wnt signalling is central to tissue
maintenance.
Wnt and beta-catenin signalling controls intestinal development, epithelial
repair, stem-cell renewal and differentiation.
Dishevelled proteins connect receptor-level Wnt events to downstream regulation
of beta-catenin, and APC acts as a tumour suppressor scaffold in the
beta-catenin destruction complex.
The experiments summarised here identify CASP5C as an epithelial regulator of
this pathway and distinguish its activity from that of longer CASP5 isoforms.

## Results

CASP5 responded to inflammatory and infectious stimuli in epithelial organoids,
but not in a pattern that supported a simple lipopolysaccharide sensor role.
Salmonella infection, interferon beta and interferon gamma increased CASP5 in
human intestinal organoids.
Lipopolysaccharide alone had little effect in epithelial cells, probably because
TLR4 expression was low in that compartment.
These observations support inducibility of CASP5 in epithelial stress without
assigning it the same role as CASP4 in cytosolic lipopolysaccharide responses.

To identify candidate epithelial functions, proximity labelling with BioID2 was
performed in human colonic organoids.
This analysis recovered DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3 near CASP5, and Wnt-related terms
appeared in the interactor analysis.
Co-immunoprecipitation experiments supported association of CASP5 isoforms with
DVL1, DVL2 and DVL3.
DVL2-containing complexes also included AXIN1 and beta-catenin, placing the
interaction near the beta-catenin destruction machinery.
Mapping experiments implicated the DVL DEP domain and the CASP5 catalytic
domain in the interaction.
The data are consistent with a close, potentially direct interaction, although
direct binding was not fully established.

Functional assays separated CASP5C from the other tested inflammatory
caspases.
In HEK293T and HCT116 cells, CASP5C increased TOPFlash Wnt reporter activity
with or without WNT3A stimulation.
CASP5A, CASP5B, CASP1 and CASP4 did not produce the same effect.
CASP5C increased AXIN2 and TCF7 expression and increased cytosolic and nuclear
beta-catenin.
By contrast, CASP5A and CASP5B could compete with CASP5C for DVL2 and reduce
its effect on Wnt signalling.
CARD-less forms of mouse caspase 11 did not reproduce the Wnt reporter effect,
which suggests a possible primate CASP5 specialisation after gene duplication.
That evolutionary interpretation remains tentative.

Human intestinal organoid experiments supported the cellular relevance of this
isoform-specific activity.
CASP5C, but not CASP5A or CASP5B, increased Wnt reporter activity in organoids.
CASP5C expression produced more viable cells, organoids that were about 40%
larger, more Ki-67-positive and EdU-positive cells, and fewer CK20-positive
enterocytes.
It also increased Wnt and proliferation markers, including AXIN2 by about
three-fold, TCF7 by 1.4-fold, MYC by 1.3-fold, LGR5 by 1.2-fold and MKI67 by
2.4-fold.
Differentiation-associated markers decreased, with ALPI and MUC2 reduced by
about 57% and 53%.
CASP5C-expressing organoids survived longer without exogenous Wnt and could
re-enter the cell cycle after Wnt was restored.
These findings are consistent with a repair-supporting epithelial programme in
vitro, but they do not prove that CASP5C drives repair in the human body.

Irradiation experiments further connected CASP5C to epithelial stress and
regrowth.
In irradiated human colonic organoids, CASP5c transcript increased 42.4-fold
within 24 h, whereas CASP5a and CASP5b increased 5.6-fold and 6.6-fold.
After irradiation, CASP5C-expressing organoids showed faster increases in LGR5,
OLFM4, TCF7 and MYC.
This pattern places CASP5C upstream of, or alongside, a proliferative Wnt-linked
response after damage.

Single-cell and differentiation analyses indicated that CASP5 isoforms occupy
distinct epithelial states.
In one single-cell RNA analysis, overall CASP5 transcripts were nearly absent
from LGR5-positive stem cells, higher in transit-amplifying cells and highest
in enterocytes.
However, isoform-resolved analysis placed CASP5c deeper in crypt regions, where
it overlapped more with MKI67 and AXIN2 than CASP5a or CASP5b did.
CASP5A and CASP5B showed a more luminal and differentiated pattern, including
association with CK20.
Pseudotime and CIBERSORTx analyses also linked CASP5c with early-lineage or
transit-amplifying states and linked CASP5a and CASP5b with mature or
differentiating states.
During differentiation after Wnt withdrawal, CASP5c peaked early with MKI67,
whereas CASP5a and CASP5b increased later with differentiation markers.

Loss-of-function experiments supported a competition model among CASP5
isoforms.
Knockdown of CASP5a and CASP5b while preserving CASP5C increased Wnt reporter
activity, organoid size, ATP abundance, EdU incorporation and Ki-67 staining.
When CASP5c was also knocked down, these effects were reduced.
Together with the DVL2-binding data, these results suggest that CASP5A and
CASP5B can restrain CASP5C by competing for access to dishevelled-containing
complexes.
This interpretation also explains why it would be inaccurate to describe all
CASP5 isoforms as Wnt activators.

Mechanistic experiments identified APC cleavage as a key downstream event.
After Wnt stimulation, CASP5C reduced DVL2, beta-catenin,
phospho-beta-catenin, APC and beta-TrCP in AXIN1-containing complexes.
CASP5C also reduced beta-catenin ubiquitination.
These changes indicate that CASP5C weakens the beta-catenin destruction complex
and thereby favours beta-catenin accumulation.
The catalytic mutant CASP5C(C173A) lost reporter activation and beta-catenin
effects, showing that protease activity is required.
CASP5C had higher enzymatic activity than CASP5A or CASP5B, and removal of the
CARD from CASP5A or CASP5B increased their activity and conferred greater Wnt
reporter signalling.

APC was the most direct substrate identified in the pathway.
Predicted CASP5 cleavage sites clustered mainly in the N-terminal region of
APC.
CASP5C generated APC cleavage products, whereas CASP5A, CASP5B and
CASP5C(C173A) did not.
Recombinant CASP5C directly cleaved truncated APC in vitro.
An APC D556A mutation removed the major cleavage products.
In APC-knockout HEK293T cells, wild-type APC allowed CASP5C to disrupt the
complex and increase Wnt reporter activity.
The APC(D556A) mutant weakened this response.
APC fragments resembling cleavage products reduced beta-catenin in the
destruction complex and increased reporter activity.
D556 therefore appears to be a primary cleavage site, but additional APC sites
may also contribute.

Disease-associated datasets provide context but not causal proof.
CASP5 and CASP4 were increased in ulcerative colitis datasets while retaining
their cell-type patterns.
CASP5 mutations appeared in about 3% of colon or rectal cancer cases, tended to
co-occur with Wnt-pathway mutations and were mutually exclusive with APC
mutations in the reported analysis.
In microsatellite-instability-high colorectal cancer, CASP5 mutations were
reported more often than APC mutations in one comparison, at about 68% versus
17%.
These observations justify further study, but they do not show that CASP5C
causes intestinal disease or is a validated therapeutic target.

## Discussion

The data support a model in which CASP5C links epithelial stress responses to
Wnt pathway amplification.
CASP5C associates with dishevelled-containing complexes, requires its catalytic
site for activity and cleaves APC.
APC cleavage reduces the integrity of the AXIN1-associated beta-catenin
destruction complex, lowers beta-catenin ubiquitination and permits increased
beta-catenin-dependent transcription.
In organoids, this molecular activity corresponds to larger epithelial
structures, increased proliferation markers and reduced differentiation
markers.

This model shows why CASP5 should not be treated as one functional unit.
CASP5C promotes Wnt signalling in the tested systems, whereas CASP5A and CASP5B
do not show the same effect and can restrain CASP5C.
The CARD region may contribute to this distinction by limiting catalytic
activity or altering access to relevant complexes.
The absence of a comparable effect from CARD-less mouse caspase 11 forms argues
against a simple domain-deletion explanation and suggests that human CASP5C has
acquired a specialised epithelial function.

The findings also separate CASP5C activity from canonical assumptions about
inflammatory caspases.
CASP4 behaved as the principal human counterpart of mouse caspase 11 in the
tested noncanonical inflammasome setting.
CASP5C instead acted on an epithelial homeostatic pathway.
This distinction matters because inflammatory caspases are not interchangeable
sensors or executioners of innate immune responses.
The present evidence supports a more specific view in which CASP5 isoforms have
different tissue, lineage and pathway activities.

Several limitations constrain interpretation.
The strongest functional evidence comes from engineered cells and organoids,
not direct human in vivo intervention.
CASP5-dishevelled association is supported by proximity and
co-immunoprecipitation, but direct binding remains incompletely resolved.
D556 is a major APC cleavage site, but other sites may contribute.
The disease analyses are associative and should be used to generate hypotheses,
not claims of causation or therapeutic readiness.

Within these limits, CASP5C emerges as a regulator of intestinal epithelial Wnt
tone.
By cleaving APC and weakening the destruction complex, CASP5C can amplify
beta-catenin signalling during epithelial proliferation and stress responses.
Longer CASP5 isoforms appear to counterbalance this effect through competition
for dishevelled-containing complexes and through lower catalytic activity.
This isoform-level regulation explains how an inflammatory caspase gene can
contribute to intestinal epithelial homeostasis without acting as the main
noncanonical inflammasome protease.
